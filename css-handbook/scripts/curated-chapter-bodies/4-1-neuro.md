## INTRODUCTION

Sudden neurological deterioration is a common reason for patient admission to emergency medical services. It can be caused by various critical illnesses or injuries. Prompt treatment is essential to limit secondary brain injury and improve patient outcome. Primary injury is the initial brain damage caused by a critical illness or injury. Regardless of the cause, primary injury triggers secondary injury, which can lead to further brain damage. Our management can affect secondary brain damage through interventions that address hypoxia, hypotension, and increased intracranial pressure.

## CAUSES OF SUDDEN NEUROLOGICAL DETERIORATION AND POTENTIAL DIFFERENTIAL DIAGNOSIS

### NEUROLOGICAL CAUSES

- Ischaemic or haemorrhagic stroke.
- Traumatic brain injury (TBI; the morphological substrate spans epidural haematoma, subdural haematoma, brain contusions, diffuse axonal injury, and brain oedema).
- Brain tumours.

### SYSTEMIC CAUSES

- Infection, including septic encephalopathy and central nervous system infection in the form of meningitis and/or encephalitis.
- Organ system failure, such as shock, renal failure, hepatic failure, hypoxaemia, and/or hypercarbia due to respiratory failure.
- Endocrine disorders, including hypoglycaemia, hyperglycaemia, thyroid dysfunction, and Addison crisis.
- Electrolyte and acid-base disorders, including hypo- or hypernatraemia, hypercalcaemia, and hypophosphataemia.
- Drugs and poisoning, such as narcotics, benzodiazepines, and carbon monoxide poisoning.
- Hypothermia and hyperthermia.

## INITIAL PATIENT ASSESSMENT AND MANAGEMENT

### ABCDE + SAMPLE

### A (AIRWAY)

- Check airway patency because CNS depression may cause partial or complete airway obstruction. Noisy breathing or snoring and asynchronous movement of the chest and abdomen suggest partial obstruction. Complete obstruction is silent, with visible accessory muscle use and a see-saw breathing pattern for a brief period before cardiac arrest.
- In suspected trauma, stabilise the cervical spine.
- Aspirate any blood or gastric content from the airway, if present.
- Use simple airway opening manoeuvres, such as head-tilt chin-lift or jaw-thrust, or place an oro-/nasopharyngeal airway if the airway is not patent.
- Tracheal intubation and mechanical ventilation are indicated in patients with GCS <= 8, as they may lose protective airway reflexes and develop hypoventilation with resultant hypercapnia, which worsens intracranial hypertension. Advanced airway techniques that require anaesthetic and neuromuscular blocking drugs should only be performed by experienced personnel, because failed attempts can worsen secondary brain injury.

### B (BREATHING)

- Assess breathing using the "look, listen, and feel" approach to evaluate respiratory rate and effort, breath sounds, added sounds, chest movement symmetry, and breathing patterns such as Cheyne-Stokes and Kussmaul respirations.
- Look for causes of hypoxia such as subcutaneous emphysema, tracheal deviation, and jugular venous distention.
- Continuously monitor oxygen saturation (SpO2) with pulse oximetry and end-expiratory CO2 (EtCO2), if available.
- Administer oxygen if SpO2 is below 90%.
- Ensure adequate oxygenation at all times to prevent secondary brain injury and maintain blood oxygen saturation above 90%.
- If the patient has insufficient minute ventilation or oxygenation even after airway opening and oxygen therapy, begin manual ventilation with a tight-fitting face mask and self-inflating bag and consider anaesthesia and tracheal intubation if you are experienced in the procedure. Insufficient minute ventilation can be expected in bradypnoeic patients, opioid overdose, imminent brainstem herniation, and respiratory muscle fatigue.

### C (CIRCULATION)

- Assess heart rate, blood pressure, capillary refill time, skin colour, and hourly urine output, and monitor these parameters every 3-5 minutes. Look for patterns that may indicate the cause of neurological deterioration, such as Cushing's triad.
- Place continuous ECG monitoring.
- Hypotension, together with hypoxia, is a major contributor to secondary brain injury. Cerebral perfusion pressure depends on mean arterial pressure and intracranial pressure.
- In the initial management of a patient with neurological deterioration, aim for systolic blood pressure above 110 mmHg or mean arterial pressure above 70 mmHg to maintain adequate brain perfusion.
- If blood pressure is below the target values, consider volume resuscitation with 0.9% sodium chloride or hypertonic sodium chloride, because these solutions will not worsen brain oedema.
- If blood pressure remains low, consider vasoconstrictors such as noradrenaline 0.01-0.5 mcg/kg/min IV, titrated to the target blood pressure with regular reassessment.
- Once IV access is established, obtain blood for testing, including complete blood count, glucose, sodium, potassium, and coagulation tests. Add other tests such as C-reactive protein, procalcitonin, urea, creatinine, ammonium, liver enzymes, and arterial blood gases as guided by the differential diagnosis.

### D (DISABILITY/OTHER ACUTE CONSIDERATIONS)

- Before performing the neurological assessment, correct hypoxia and hypotension because they can cause or worsen decreased consciousness.
- Begin by assessing blood glucose, Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS), pupillary size and symmetry, and light response.
- Check for asymmetry in motor response between the left and right side and perform a targeted neurological examination for signs of raised intracranial pressure, meningeal irritation, subtle myoclonus such as eyelid fasciculation, and evidence of previous seizure such as lateral tongue biting or uncontrolled urination.
- Normal pupils are equal in size, reactive to light, and usually 2-8 mm in diameter. Any asymmetry or reduced responsiveness is pathological in sudden neurological deterioration and can help with the differential diagnosis.
- Targeted neurological examination should also assess breathing pattern, plantar and deep tendon reflexes, and brainstem reflexes such as pupillary, corneal, and oculovestibular reflexes. Tracheal suction response may be useful if brain death is suspected.

#### GLASGOW COMA SCALE (GCS)

- Eye opening: spontaneous (4), to speech (3), to pain (2), no response (1).
- Verbal response: oriented in time, space, and person (5), confused/disoriented (4), inappropriate words (3), incomprehensible sounds (2), no response (1).
- Motor response: obeys commands (6), localises pain (5), withdraws to painful stimulus (4), abnormal flexion/decortication (3), abnormal extension/decerebration (2), no response (1).

#### REVERSIBLE AND URGENT CAUSES

- Hypoglycaemia is a common and easily treatable cause of coma. If present, give high-concentration glucose, such as 40% glucose, and titrate to recovery of consciousness and adequate blood glucose levels. If rapid glucose testing is not available, 50 mL of 40% glucose should be administered because the potential benefit outweighs the risk of transient hyperglycaemia.
- Repeated or prolonged seizures substantially increase secondary brain injury and should be stopped immediately with IV benzodiazepines.
- Signs of elevated intracranial pressure include decreased consciousness with anisocoria or bilaterally dilated unresponsive pupils, Cushing's triad, and similar findings. Consider mannitol 0.5-1 g/kg IV with or without furosemide 20 mg IV after placing a urinary catheter, brief hyperventilation, and head-of-bed elevation to 15-30 degrees. If adequately trained, give analgesia and sedation and proceed with tracheal intubation and mechanical ventilation.
- In suspected drug overdose, administer an antidote if available, such as naloxone for opioid overdose or flumazenil for benzodiazepine overdose.

### SAMPLE

- **S (Signs/symptoms)** Obtain information from any witness present. How did the deterioration develop: over minutes or hours? Did the patient complain of headache, vomiting, fever, or other symptoms before the deterioration?
- **A (Allergies)** Ask about known allergies or hypersensitivity to medications or other substances.
- **M (Medications)** Ask about current medications, especially antiplatelet or anticoagulant therapy, psychiatric therapy, insulin, oral antidiabetic medication, and antiepileptic therapy.
- **P (Past illnesses)** Ask about recent surgery, infections, and other comorbidities. Clarify any relevant family history.
- **L (Last oral intake)** If less than six hours have passed since the last meal, the risk of regurgitation and aspiration is higher during analgosedation or tracheal intubation. Use rapid sequence intubation if those procedures are planned.
- **E (Events leading up to present illness/injury)** Look for empty medication containers or bottles near the patient and consider whether this could be a suicide attempt or intentional overdose.
