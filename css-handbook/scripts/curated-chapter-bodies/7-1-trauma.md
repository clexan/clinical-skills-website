## INTRODUCTION

Polytrauma, together with acute coronary syndrome and stroke, is a time-dependent disease. Recognition of life-threatening injuries, initial stabilisation, and definitive care are directly connected with survival during the "golden period" from injury to definitive treatment. In prehospital polytrauma management, the goal is to minimise time at the scene, ideally to about 10 minutes, while identifying and treating immediately life-threatening conditions, immobilising the patient, and transporting them to the appropriate trauma centre.

The most common approaches used in Europe are the International Trauma Life Support (ITLS) approach, adapted to prehospital conditions, and the European Trauma Course (ETC) approach for hospital trauma care. The trauma examination itself remains the same.

## MANAGEMENT OF TRAUMA PATIENTS

We use the ITLS algorithm for the structured survey of injured patients in emergency medicine:

- ITLS primary survey: scene size-up, initial assessment, and rapid trauma survey or focused survey.
- ITLS ongoing exam: monitoring of changes in the patient's condition.
- ITLS secondary survey: complete examination and identification of all injuries.

### SCENE SIZE-UP

Assessment of the scene involves identifying and removing hazards before approaching the patient. If the scene is unsafe, for example because of electrical risk, explosion, fire, building collapse, or road traffic, it must be secured by other emergency services or the victim must be removed without endangering the team. Personal protective equipment is mandatory because of possible contact with blood and other potentially infectious material.

Scene size-up should also assess the mechanism of injury (MOI), the number of victims, the equipment needed, and the need for additional medical assistance. If there are several severely injured people, reinforcements should be requested. As a rule, one vehicle and one ambulance team are needed for each severely injured person.

## MECHANISM OF INJURY

The mechanism of injury can be generalised, when large forces act on the whole body and life-threatening injuries can be expected, or focused, when forces act on an isolated region. Significant mechanisms of injury include ejection from a vehicle, falls from more than four and a half metres, vehicle rollover, high-speed collision, pedestrian-versus-car collision, and motorcycle accidents. In such patients, always expect more severe injuries than are immediately visible.

A motor vehicle collision implies three consecutive collisions: vehicle collision, body collision, and organ collision. Additional injury may result from unsecured objects moving like projectiles or from secondary impacts. Vehicle safety systems can also contribute to injuries of the abdomen, cervical and lumbar spine, shoulders, and clavicles.

### BASIC MECHANISMS OF INJURY

- Blunt injuries: sudden forward deceleration (collision), sudden vertical deceleration (fall), and energy transfer from blunt objects (impact).
- Penetrating injuries: missiles, knives, and falls onto a fixed object.

### POTENTIAL INJURY BY TRAUMA MECHANISM

- Frontal impact: cervical spine fracture, flail chest, myocardial contusion, pneumothorax, aortic disruption, spleen or liver laceration, posterior hip dislocation, and knee dislocation.
- Lateral impact: cervical spine fracture, flail chest, pneumothorax, aortic disruption, diaphragmatic rupture, laceration of spleen/liver/kidney, and pelvic fracture.
- Rear impact: cervical spine injury.
- Pedestrian versus car: head injury, abdominal visceral injuries, fracture of the lower extremities and pelvis, and spine fracture.

## INITIAL ASSESSMENT

The team leader performs the primary ITLS assessment and should not stop until it is complete unless cardiopulmonary arrest, airway obstruction, uncontrolled external bleeding, or new danger at the scene forces an interruption. In a critically injured patient, the goal is to complete the primary examination within 2 minutes and to remain on scene for less than 10 minutes.

Team members perform procedures by a "fix it" tactic delegated by the team leader. Major external bleeding changes the usual ABC priority to C-ABC. Before approaching the patient, assess the general impression, obvious deformities, respiratory distress, and whether the patient belongs to a higher-risk group such as children, older adults, or pregnant patients.

The level of consciousness is quickly assessed with AVPU:

- **A (Alert)** Awake, oriented, and obeys commands.
- **V (Verbal)** Responds to verbal stimuli.
- **P (Pain)** Responds to touch or painful stimuli.
- **U (Unresponsive)** Does not respond to stimuli.

If consciousness is at the P or U level, airway compromise is likely.

### A (AIRWAY)

- If the victim responds, the airway is open.
- In impaired consciousness, listen for snoring, gurgling, or other abnormal airway sounds.
- Snoring suggests partial obstruction and requires airway opening manoeuvres or placement of an oropharyngeal or nasopharyngeal airway.
- Gurgling suggests liquid in the oral cavity and requires suction.
- The see-saw phenomenon, with asynchronous chest and abdominal movement, is a sign of complete airway obstruction.

### B (BREATHING)

- Assess respiratory rate, chest rise symmetry, use of accessory muscles, and pulse oximetry.
- Deliver high-flow oxygen (12-15 L/min) via reservoir mask to every critically injured person.
- If the patient is apnoeic, provide bag-mask ventilation with a self-inflating bag.
- Assist ventilation if breathing is inadequate because of low rate or insufficient tidal volume.

### C (CIRCULATION)

- Assess pulse rate, rhythm, and strength; skin temperature, colour, and sweating; and capillary refill time.
- In early shock, the skin is pale, cold, and sweaty, the pulse is rapid and weak, and capillary refill time is prolonged.
- Reassess for visible external bleeding.
- Establish IV access if possible, but do not delay critical interventions or transport for IV placement.

### D (DISABILITY)

- Assess consciousness initially with AVPU.
- A more detailed neurological assessment, including Glasgow Coma Scale and pupils, should be performed at the end of the primary ITLS assessment before loading if consciousness is impaired.
- If the patient is fully conscious, a more detailed neurological examination can be postponed to the secondary survey.
- Measure blood glucose in patients with impaired consciousness or diabetes.

### E (EXPOSURE)

- Look for environmental clues such as needles, pills, or evidence of exposure.
- Inspect the patient for additional wounds, temperature problems, and hidden injuries.

### SAMPLE

Because the injured patient may lose consciousness before reaching hospital, the prehospital team may be the only team able to obtain anamnestic data. If the patient is already unconscious, obtain heteroanamnesis from eyewitnesses or family members.

- **S (Signs/symptoms)** Ask about symptoms and visible injuries.
- **A (Allergies)** Clarify known allergies.
- **M (Medications)** Review chronic medications.
- **P (Past illnesses)** Ask about relevant medical history.
- **L (Last oral intake)** Ask when the patient last ate or drank.
- **E (Events preceding the incident)** Clarify what happened before and during the injury.

## RAPID TRAUMA SURVEY

Rapid trauma survey is a systematic head-to-toe examination used in patients with a generalised mechanism of injury or any abnormality in the initial assessment. In isolated or minor trauma, a focused examination of the injured body part may be sufficient.

Each region is examined first by inspection and then by palpation. The chest may also require auscultation and percussion.

### BODY REGIONS TO EXAMINE

- **Head**: visible injuries, haematoma, deformity, tenderness, crepitation, and instability.
- **Neck**: visible injuries, jugular venous distention, tenderness, tracheal deviation, and subcutaneous emphysema.
- **Chest**: visible injuries, unstable segments, open wounds, tenderness, crepitation, instability, breath sounds, heart sounds, and percussion if findings are unequal.
- **Abdomen**: injuries, seat-belt marks, tenderness, rigidity, and distension.
- **Pelvis**: injuries, deformity, tenderness, crepitation, and instability.
- **Extremities**: visible injuries, deformity, tenderness, crepitation, instability, bleeding, distal pulses, motor function, and sensation.
- **Posterior surface**: visible injuries, tenderness, crepitation, instability, and spinal motion restriction needs.

Inspection helps detect visible injuries, bleeding, and deformities. The neck should be checked for jugular venous distention because it may indicate tension pneumothorax or pericardial tamponade. In the chest, look for open wounds and unstable segments. A seat-belt mark on the abdomen suggests a high probability of intra-abdominal injury. Do not remove foreign bodies unless they obstruct the airway or are located in the cheek. Stabilise them in place. Do not return abdominal evisceration into the abdomen; cover it with sterile gauze soaked in isotonic saline and an impermeable cover. Amputated parts should be placed in a dry bag, which is then placed in a second bag containing ice and water.

Palpation reveals tenderness, instability, and crepitation suggesting fracture. Palpate the cervical and later the entire spine for pain, deformity, and paravertebral muscle spasm. The chest can be palpated with the "click-clack" technique by alternating pressure from both hands. Palpate the abdomen in four quadrants. If there is pain, consider injury of intra-abdominal organs with risk of shock. Test the pelvis gently by pressing the symphysis and then compressing the iliac crests inward; if the pelvis is painful or unstable, stop further pelvic examination and apply a pelvic splint.

When examining the extremities, evaluate instability, crepitation, pain, bleeding, and distal pulses below any suspected fracture. In altered consciousness, use facial grimacing as a guide to pain during palpation. Auscultate the chest bilaterally in the midaxillary line. If breath sounds are unequal, percussion may help distinguish pneumothorax from haemothorax.

Examine the back with a log roll unless there are contraindications such as unstable pelvis, bilateral femoral fractures, foreign body in the abdomen or chest, or clear signs of spinal injury. In those situations, use a scoop stretcher instead. In patients at risk of shock, obtain two large-bore IV lines and begin fluid therapy. In suspected internal bleeding, target systolic blood pressure around 90 mmHg. Excessive fluid replacement can worsen bleeding, dilute clotting factors, and aggravate hypothermia.

## IMMOBILISATION

### CERVICAL SPINE IMMOBILISATION WITH A CERVICAL COLLAR

Spinal immobilisation begins with manual stabilisation of the head and neck and continues with the placement of a cervical collar and other immobilisation equipment. Before placing the collar, examine the entire neck because inspection and palpation become difficult afterwards.

Choose the correct collar size by measuring the distance between the top of the trapezius and the point where a line dropped from the chin meets the side of the neck. Transfer that measurement to the collar and adjust the width. In a supine patient, slide the back of the collar under the neck first and then place the front under the chin. In a sitting patient, place the front first.

### SPINAL IMMOBILISATION WITH A LONG BACKBOARD (LOG ROLL)

Complete immobilisation requires a cervical collar, long backboard, lateral head stabilisers, two adhesive headbands, straps, and ideally three or four people. Manual in-line stabilisation is maintained throughout. The long backboard is placed parallel to the patient. While the leader stabilises the head and directs the team, the other team members support the shoulders, hips, and legs and log roll the patient as one unit.

While the patient is on the side, inspect the back and buttocks. The patient is then rolled back onto the board and secured in a fixed order: strap under the arms and above the breasts, strap across the pelvis, strap above the knees (or below them in femur fracture), and an "8" strap around the feet. Finally, attach the head stabilisers. This order matters because vomiting and side turning can destabilise an incompletely secured patient. A vacuum mattress is an alternative and is increasingly recommended for longer transport.

## ETC APPROACH TO TRAUMA PATIENTS

The European Trauma Course was developed for multidisciplinary in-hospital trauma care. On arrival to the emergency department, begin with a rapid 5-second round:

- Are there signs of airway obstruction?
- Is there massive external bleeding?
- Are there signs of arrest?

After excluding those immediate threats, the senior clinician gathers information about the patient and event, performs a rapid trauma survey and FAST ultrasound, and decides on further laboratory testing, imaging, and removal of immobilisation equipment.

## SPECIFIC INJURIES

### CHEST INJURIES

The thoracic cage protects the lungs, heart, great vessels, liver, spleen, transverse colon, and spinal cord. Trauma may produce life-threatening disruption of any of these systems. Chest injuries may be blunt or penetrating. Open wounds should not be explored and foreign bodies should not be removed in the field; they should be stabilised in place.

Life-threatening chest injuries should be recognised during the primary survey. The most important symptoms are dyspnoea and chest pain. Tenderness, instability, and crepitation are found on palpation. Use auscultation, pulse oximetry, capnography, ECG monitoring, and IV access as needed. Multiple rib fractures can impair respiratory mechanics and are commonly associated with pulmonary contusion.

### TENSION PNEUMOTHORAX

Tension pneumothorax is progressive accumulation of air in the pleural space without egress, producing a one-way valve effect. Rising intrathoracic pressure shifts the mediastinum, impairs venous return, decreases cardiac output, and leads to obstructive shock. Without rapid treatment, respiratory and cardiac arrest follow within minutes.

Typical signs include dyspnoea, tachypnoea, possible tracheal deviation, distended jugular veins, overexpansion of the affected hemithorax with poor respiratory movement, reduced or absent breath sounds, and hyperresonance on percussion.

Needle decompression is a temporary but life-saving intervention. Needle thoracocentesis can be performed through the second intercostal space in the midclavicular line or through the fourth or fifth intercostal space in the anterior or midaxillary line using the widest practical needle/catheter. Definitive treatment is thoracic drainage in hospital.

### OPEN PNEUMOTHORAX

Open pneumothorax results from an open chest wound that allows air to enter the pleural space. If the wound is wider than two thirds of the tracheal diameter, air preferentially enters through the wound during inspiration. Cover the wound with an occlusive dressing taped on three sides so that one corner remains free, creating a flutter valve.

### MASSIVE HAEMOTHORAX

Massive haemothorax can follow blunt or penetrating trauma. The source may be the lung, major vessels, intercostal vessels, or even the heart. It is defined by at least 1,500 mL of blood in the pleural cavity. Thoracotomy is indicated when blood loss exceeds 1,500 mL or roughly one third of blood volume, or when drainage exceeds 200 mL/h (about 3 mL/kg/h) over 2-4 hours.

### FLAIL CHEST

Flail chest results from fracture of three or more adjacent ribs in two or more places, creating an unstable segment with paradoxical movement. The most important associated pathophysiology is lung contusion with respiratory insufficiency, atelectasis, and pneumothorax. Management focuses on stabilisation, adequate analgesia, and, when needed, endotracheal intubation and assisted ventilation.

### CARDIAC TAMPONADE

Pericardial tamponade most often follows penetrating chest trauma. Rapid blood accumulation in the pericardium compresses the ventricles and reduces cardiac output, causing obstructive shock. Beck's triad includes hypotension, jugular venous distention, and muffled heart sounds. Tachycardia, tachypnoea, and pulsus paradoxus may also occur. Without ultrasound, tamponade may be difficult to distinguish clinically from tension pneumothorax. Treatment is emergency pericardiocentesis with drainage by an experienced operator.

### DIAPHRAGMATIC RUPTURE

Diaphragmatic rupture from blunt trauma is uncommon but occurs in about 5% of polytrauma patients. It is usually caused by traffic accidents and is often initially missed because of other severe injuries. The left hemidiaphragm is affected most often. Blunt injuries may produce lacerations with herniation of abdominal organs into the chest; penetrating injuries may create small perforations that present later as hernias. Suspect diaphragmatic rupture in severe blunt chest trauma, especially after compression or rapid deceleration.

### ABDOMINAL INJURIES

Abdominal trauma is life-threatening because of the risk of haemorrhagic shock and is difficult to assess even in hospital. Prehospital care should therefore emphasise careful assessment of the mechanism of injury and minimisation of scene time. Abdominal trauma may be blunt or penetrating. Common findings are abdominal pain, tenderness, wall tension, bruising, and seat-belt marks. A horizontal seat-belt sign carries a high risk of mesenteric or intestinal injury.

### BLUNT ABDOMINAL INJURIES

Blunt abdominal injuries are often caused by traffic accidents, falls, bicycle handlebar impact, sports injuries, and assault. Intra-abdominal bleeding, hollow-organ injury, and retroperitoneal injury may occur. The most common bleeding sources are the spleen, liver, and mesenteric vessels. Splenic injury is often associated with fractures of the lower ribs.

Jejunal or terminal ileal rupture can lead to peritonitis after mid-abdominal compression against the spine. Symptoms include pain, nausea, abdominal rigidity, vomiting, and distension. Duodenal and pancreatic injury may occur in the retroperitoneum. Retroperitoneal haematoma commonly results from renal rupture and may coexist with splenic injury.

### PENETRATING ABDOMINAL INJURIES

Penetrating abdominal injuries are usually caused by stabbing or firearms. They can damage the abdominal wall vessels and internal organs such as the liver, spleen, stomach, intestines, and mesentery. Eviscerated bowel should never be forced back into the abdomen; cover it with warm saline-soaked gauze. Projectile wounds may represent abdominal penetration even when the entry site is outside the abdomen.

### PELVIC INJURIES

Pelvic injuries usually reflect high-energy trauma and may be accompanied by severe bleeding and rapid haemorrhagic shock. They are often associated with injuries in other body regions. Stabilisation with a pelvic splint is a simple and important intervention.

Pelvic stabilisation has four goals:

- Prevent further tissue damage caused by pathological movement of fractured pelvic bones.
- Reduce pelvic volume.
- Minimise bleeding from bone and soft tissue.
- Reduce pain.

Use a pelvic splint in the following situations:

- Haemodynamically unstable patient with a mechanically unstable pelvis.
- Haemodynamically unstable patient with a suspected pelvic fracture.
- Haemodynamically stable patient with a mechanically unstable pelvis.

Before and after immobilisation, check the 4 Ps: pain, pockets, penis, and pulse. Place the splint at the level of the greater trochanters and tighten it enough to provide clamping force without excessive pressure.
