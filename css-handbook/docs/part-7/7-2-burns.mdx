## INTRODUCTION

Most physicians who work in emergency medical services will encounter a patient with large burns, a challenging and life-threatening injury. The initial management of these patients will make a difference between good and poor outcomes. Epidemiology data suggest that burns are more common in men than women, with flame and scald as the most common causes. Moreover, children under 5 years of age are five times more likely to be injured than adults. Approximately 10% of burned patients will need hospital admission, and 10-15% of hospitalised patients will need to be treated in burn centres. The incidence of severe burns in Europe is 0.2-2.9/10,000.

### RISK FACTORS FOR DEVELOPING SEVERE COMPLICATIONS AND

POOR OUTCOMES INCLUDE:

- second-degree burns covering more than 10% of total body surface area (TBSA) in patients under 10 and over 50 years of age
- second-degree burns covering more than 20% TBSA
- third-degree burns covering more than 5% TBSA
- burns that involve the face, hands, feet, genitalia, perineum, and/ or major joints
- chemical burns (management includes contacting the Poison Control Centre, for Croatia available at +38512348342)
- electrical burns (including lightening injuries)
- burns associated with other trauma injuries
- patient's preexisting medical condition that has implications on wound healing and/or burn management (e.g., chronic heart failure, diabetes, immunosuppression, pregnancy).

- Assess the safety of the patient and the rescuer(s), e.g., consider potential hazards due to electricity, gas, traffic, etc.
- If ongoing, stop the burning process.
- Every burned patient should also be managed as a trauma patient due to common concomitant injuries such as head trauma due to suffocation and loss of consciousness, escaping from fire by jumping through a window, etc.
- Check if the patient responds to stimuli:
- If not, call for help, open the airway, and check for breathing sounds and pulse for 10 seconds (follow the advanced life support algorithm).
- If the patient is alive, proceed with ABCDEF: A (AIRWAY) / B (BREATHING) / CERVICAL SPINE CONTROL:
- Check the airway patency and open the airway in the case of partial or complete obstruction (apply the cervical spine control in suspected trauma).
- Evaluate breathing ("look, listen, and feel" approach: respiratory rate and effort, breath and added sounds, symmetry of chest movement, check for subcutaneous emphysema, tracheal deviation, jugular vein dilation, cyanosis), and use pulse oximetry.
- Apply high-flow oxygen therapy through a non-rebreathing reservoir mask.
- If the patient has deep burns to the lower face and/or neck, burned nasal hair, blisters, or oedema of the lip and/or tongue, and if the fire was in a closed room, a direct and thorough inspection of the oropharynx is needed, preferably by a physician experienced in airway management.
- Hoarseness, carbonaceous sputum, hypoxia, stridor, wheezing, signs of respiratory distress, and/or use of accessory muscles indicate inhalation injury of the airway and/or lungs, and most experts advocate for emergency tracheal intubation with a cuffed endotracheal tube (ETT). Prepare a selection of ETT sizes as smaller size might be needed due to airway oedema and secure the ETT position thoroughly as the intubation might be impossible once the oedema fully develops.
- Mind that signs and symptoms of airway oedema might become apparent only after the fluid resuscitation is started, e.g., during the transport of the patient to the hospital.
- Check for signs of carbon monoxide or cyanide poisoning.

### C (CIRCULATION)

Every burn injury larger than 10% of TBSA in children or 15% of TBSA in adults requires meticulous intravenous fluid resuscitation to prevent the development of hypovolaemic shock!

- Check for heart rate, blood pressure, capillary refill time, skin colour, and (hourly) urine output. Monitor the dynamics every 3-5 minutes.
- Place two large bore intravenous cannulas (* 18 G, "green, grey, or orange"), preferably at sites that are not burned, and secure them well.
- If this is not possible, establish intraosseous access.
- Development of hypovolaemic shock takes time: if the burned patient has low blood pressure immediately after the injury, search for other causes of hypotension (such as bleeding).
- Place continuous ECG monitoring, especially in the case of electrical injury or hypothermia.
- Check for peripheral pulses on all extremities due to the risk of developing compartment syndrome, especially in the case of circumferential burns.
- Place a urinary catheter to monitor hourly urine output (target value: > 1 mL/kg/h in adults, > 2 mL/kg/h in children).
- After finishing the initial assessment, apply analgaesia: e.g., morphine 4-6 mg IV, add 1-2 mg IV every 10 minutes until an adequate level of analgaesia is achieved (pain activates the sympathetic nervous system and releases catecholamines, which may intensify skin hypoperfusion and depth of burns).

### D (DISABILITY)

- Check for blood glucose level, AVPU/GCS, reactivity, and symmetry of the pupils. Put special emphasis on toxicological examinations in the case of suspected carbon monoxide or cyanide poisoning.

### E (EXPOSURE/ENVIRONMENTAL CONTROL)

- Consider cooling the burned sites with clean and cold water, at an approximate temperature of 12-15 °C (not colder than that), for 2-3 minutes for the theoretical possibility of limiting the thermal injury, reducing oedema, and relieving pain. Note that cooling may cause hypothermia.
- Irrigate chemical injuries.
- Wet and dirty clothes should be removed, but do not pull burnt clothing that is stuck to the skin. Remove all jewellery.

- If the burns are patchy, the TBSA can be estimated by approximating the area of the patient's hand, in which case the patient's hand represents approximately 1% of their TBSA.

### F (EARLY FLUID THERAPY)

- In adult patients with burns covering more than 20% of TBSA, start with 500 mL of warmed intravenous fluid during the first hour after the injury.
- In children with burns covering more than 10% of TBSA, start with 50-100 mL of warmed intravenous fluid, depending on the child's body weight.
- Mark the vials/packages so that you are aware of the exact volume of applied intravenous fluids (excessive fluid resuscitation can exaggerate the extracellular space oedema, worsening the airway oedema and/or decreasing skin perfusion with the resultant delay of burn wound healing).
- Intravenous fluid: balanced crystalloid solution, ideally Ringer lactate or Ringer acetate, prewarmed at body temperature.
