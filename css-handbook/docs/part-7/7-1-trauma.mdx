## INTRODUCTION

Polytrauma, along with other conditions such as acute coronary syndrome and stroke, is a "time-dependent disease", meaning that the recognition of life-threatening injuries, initial stabilisation, and definitive care are directly connected with the survival rate, which is referred to in the literature as the "golden period" (previously known as the "golden hour"). The specified time starts from the moment of injury to the moment of definitive treatment in the operating room. When talking about the pre-hospital management of polytrauma as part of the golden period, the aim is to spend as less time as possible at the scene of the accident, ideally 10 minutes, to examine the patient, identify and manage life-threatening conditions, immobilise the patient, and transport them to the appropriate institution - trauma centre. When talking about the approach to the polytrauma patient, there are several different associations that hold courses and provide guidelines for patient care. The most common approaches used in Europe are the International Trauma Life Support (ITLS) approach, which is adapted to outpatient conditions, and the European Trauma Course (ETC) approach as primary hospital trauma care, but the trauma examination remains the same.

### SCENE SIZE-UP

Assessment of the scene involves taking precautions to identify and remove all hazards from the scene of the accident before approaching the patient. If it is determined that the place of the accident is not safe (e.g., risk of electric shock, risk of explosion or fire, risk of building collapse, unsecured road, etc.), it must be made safe by other emergency services or the victim must be removed from the scene without jeopardising your own safety or the safety of your team. It is always necessary to use your own protective equipment due to possible contact with blood or other potentially infectious material. You should also protect the patient from contact with other people's body fluids by changing protective gloves when examining multiple injured persons. When assessing the scene, we must also assess the mechanism of injury (MOI), the number of victims, the equipment necessary for treating the injured, and the need for additional medical assistance. If it is determined that there are more injured people at the scene, reinforcements should be requested. As a rule, one vehicle and one ambulance team are needed for each severely injured person. When approaching an injured person, we must assess the mechanism of injury - the way the patient was injured, which is especially important in considering possible injuries.

## MECHANISM OF INJURY

The mechanism of injury can be generalised when large forces act on the whole body and life-threatening injuries can be expected, or focused, when forces act on an isolated region (e.g., a stone falling on the foot), although these injuries can also be life-threatening (e.g., a penetrating wound with a sharp object). A significant mechanism of injury includes falling out of a vehicle, falling from a height of more than four and a half meters, overturning a vehicle, colliding with a car at high speeds, colliding with a pedestrian, and having a motorcycle accident. When we examine a patient with any of the significant mechanisms of injury, we should always expect more severe injuries that may not be visible at first glance. A motor vehicle collision implies three consecutive collisions: a vehicle collision, a body collision, and an organ collision. ## INITIAL ASSESSMENT

The team leader performs the primary ITLS assessment and does not stop it until it is fully completed, except in cases of cardiopulmonary arrest, airway obstruction, external bleeding that cannot be stopped, or new danger at the scene of the accident. When dealing with people who are in critical condition, the goal is to complete the primary examination within two minutes and stay on the scene for less than ten minutes. To perform procedures and interventions during an ITLS assessment, the team leader delegates team members using the "fix it" tactic. For example, after the doctor checks the airway and cervical spine, the "fix it" procedure may be to place a Schantz collar and an oxygen mask. One member of the team is positioned above the injured person's head and manually limits the mobility of the cervical spine until the final immobilisation on a long backboard with a strap and lateral head restraints or in a vacuum mattress. Before approaching the injured person, the team leader assesses the general impression. The team leader notices major deformities and obvious breathing disorders and determines the risk group (elderly, children, pregnant women). Impaired state of consciousness, compromised airway, respiratory or circulatory disorder, and penetrating injuries to the head, neck, body, or extremities proximal to the elbow or knee indicate a critical patient. If the team leader notices major external bleeding after stabilising the head and neck with their hands, they delegate to a team member to stop the bleeding, which changes the usual priority approach from ABC to C-ABC. The initial assessment is performed by the team leader using the ABCDE algorithm. The team leader approaches the injured person and addresses them only when the team leader has stabilised with their hands the injured person's head and neck. The team leader introduces themselves and asks what happened. Depending on the patient's answer, the level of consciousness is quickly assessed using the AVPU scale. "A" (alert) means the patient is awake, oriented, and obeys commands. "V" (verbal) means the patient responds to verbal stimuli, is awake but confused, or unconscious but responds in some way to verbal stimuli. "P" (pain) means unconscious but responds in some way to touch or painful stimuli. "U" (unresponsive) means that the patient does not respond to any stimuli. If the level of consciousness is "P" or "U", there is a high risk of airway compromise. The stabilisation of the head and neck is taken over by a team member, and the team leader continues with the examination.

It consists of calculating the respiratory rate, observing the symmetry of chest elevation, using the auxiliary respiratory muscles, and attaching a pulse oximeter. High-flow oxygen (12-15 L/min) should be delivered via a mask with a tank to each critically injured person. ### C (CIRCULATION)

It consists of pulse assessment (rate, rhythm, strenght), skin (temperature, colour, dryness/sweating), and capillary refill time. In the early stage of shock, the skin is pale, cold, and sweaty, the pulse is poorly palpable and rapid, and the capillary refill time is prolonged. We check again to see if there is any visible external bleeding. On top of that, we try to obtain IV access. However, establishing IV access must not delay critical interventions and emergency transport of a vitally endangered injured person.

We assess the patient's state of consciousness as part of the initial assessment using the AVPU scale. If the injured person has an impaired state of consciousness or diabetes, it is necessary to measure their blood glucose level.

By evaluating exposure to environmental factors, we identify objects found in the immediate environment (needles in intravenous drug addicts, pills in suspected overdoses) or factors related to the victim (stab wounds, body temperature, etc.).

## RAPID TRAUMA SURVEY

Rapid trauma survey is a systematic examination of the injured person by body regions to determine the resulting injuries and their extent (Figure 2). It begins after the initial ABCDE assessment is completed. It is performed in patients with a generalised mechanism of injury or if any part of the initial assessment is abnormal. In patients with isolated or minor trauma, instead of a quick trauma survey, only a targeted physical examination of the injured body part is required. A rapid trauma survey is performed by assessing region by region (head, neck, chest, abdomen, pelvis, extremities, and finally the back). We first assess each region by inspection and then by palpation (we also perform auscultation of the chest and, if necessary, percussion), and only then do we move on to the next region ("from head to toe"). Inspection enables us to detect visible injuries, hematomas, bleeding, and deformities. The neck is specially inspected for jugular venous distention (if present, it may indicate tension pneumothorax or pericardial tamponade). In chest examination, we should notice open wounds (suspected open pneumothorax) and unstable segments (flail chest), whereas the mark of a seat belt on the abdomen indicates a high probability of injury to the abdominal organs and the possibility of developing shock. By examining the extremities, we can notice open fractures. We never remove foreign bodies (except in the case when they cause airway obstruction or are located in the cheek area), but we stabilise them in the found position. Do not return abdominal protrusions to the abdominal cavity but cover them with sterile gauze abundantly soaked in isotonic saline solution and covered with an impermeable foil. The amputated parts are placed in a dry bag, which is then immersed in another bag containing ice and water. The amputated part must not come into contact with water or into direct contact with ice. Palpation reveals sensitivity, instability, and crepitation which indicate a possible fracture. We also auscultate the heart sounds (are they still normal or have they become muffled).

### RAPID TRAUMA SURVEY

We examine the injured person's back after turning them on the side using the so-called log roll technique. In the case of contraindications for turning on the side (unstable pelvis, bilateral femoral fracture, foreign body in the abdomen or chest, or clear signs of a spinal injury abnormality of sensation or motor function of the extremities, deformity and pain of the cervical spine), the back is examined after lifting the patient with a scoop stretcher (Figure 3). The assessment should be carried out as described, without skipping steps, so that injuries can be detected and addressed with a systematic approach. In patients at risk of developing shock or with signs of developed shock, it is necessary to obtain two large-bore IV lines and start with fluid replacement. In the case of suspected internal bleeding (intra-abdominal, haemothorax), the goal is to achieve a systolic blood pressure of 90 mmHg. Excessive fluid replacement can increase blood pressure and thus worsen bleeding (the formed clot dislodges, and the pressure at the bleeding site is higher), dilution of clotting factors occurs, and cold fluid additionally contributes to coagulation disorder. When possible, remove the patient's clothes for proper examination. It should always be assumed that an injured patient with a significant mechanism of injury may also have a spinal injury. Contraindications for turning on the side are unstable pelvis, bilateral femoral fracture, foreign body in the abdomen or chest, or clear signs of a spinal injury.

To place the patient on a long backboard and completely immobilise the spine, we need the following: a cervical collar, a long backboard, lateral stabilisers for the head and neck, two adhesive headbands, straps, and three or four people (Figure 5). Manual stabilisation of the head and neck is the fastest way to protect the spine until an appropriate immobilisation device is selected. If the patient is conscious, we should explain the spinal immobilisation procedure to them and ask for their cooperation. By placing a cervical spine immobilisation collar, we keep the injured person's head and neck in a neutral position while the spine is being immobilised. We still use our hands to stabilise the head and neck. The long backboard should be positioned parallel to the patient. While one team member maintains in-line manual stabilisation, another supports the shoulder and hips, and the third supports the hips and lower extremities. A team member on the patient's head directs the other team members to log roll the patient as a unit onto the side toward the emergency care providers. While the injured person is on their side, the back and buttocks are examined for possible injuries. The team member holding the injured person's shoulder must not move it, as this would endanger the stability of the spine, and performs the back examination with their other ("lower") hand. The fourth person, if there is one, places a long backboard for immobilisation parallel to the patient's body. The patient is then log rolled on the long backboard back to the supine position. The injured person needs to be secured on the immobilisation board using straps. First, place the upper horizontal strap under the arms, above the breasts. Then place the second horizontal strap across the pelvis, the third strap above the knees (in the case of a femur fracture, place it below the knees), and with the fourth strap, form an "8" around the feet. Finally, we install the head motion-restriction device lateral stabilisers for the head and neck. It is important to always place the straps and lateral stabilisers in this order, otherwise, in the event of vomiting and turning the patient on the long backboard onto the side, the patient could slide off the board and the spine would become unstable. Another option for immobilisation is a vacuum mattress, which is increasingly recommended by professional associations, especially for longer transports.

### !. CHEST INJURIES

Thoracic bones protect most of the vital organs in the chest: lungs, heart, large blood vessels, liver, spleen, transverse colon, and spinal cord. Trauma can cause significant disruptions in each of these systems that can be life-threatening if not quickly identified and treated. According to the mechanism of injury chest injuries are divided into blunt or closed and open or penetrating. Closed ones are usually caused by traffic accidents, falls from heights, being buried, or a blunt blow to the chest. Open injuries are most often caused by stab wounds or gunshot wounds. A foreign body should not be removed from the wound, but it should be stabilised. Life-threatening injuries should be identified during the primary ITLS survey. Difficulty breathing and chest pain are the main signs of chest injuries. Tenderness, instability, and crepitations can be determined by palpation. Auscultation checks whether breath sounds are present and symmetrical. Multiple rib fractures with or without the formation of an unstable segment can lead to hypoxia due to disorders of respiratory mechanics and lung contusion. ### TENSION PNEUMOTHORAX

Pneumothorax is the accumulation of air in the potential space between the visceral and parietal pleura, resulting in complete lung collapse. Tension pneumothorax is a progressive accumulation of air in the pleural space that continuously accumulates without exiting, which results in the "one-way valve" phenomenon and further accumulation of air. Intra-thoracic pressure on the affected side increases, displacing the heart and trachea to the opposite side and collapsing the superior and inferior vena cava, thus occluding venous return to the heart. This leads to a drop in cardiac output and obstructive shock. If rapid intervention is not performed, respiratory and cardiac arrests occur within a few minutes.

### OPEN PNEUMOTHORAX

Open pneumothorax is an accumulation of air in the potential space between the visceral and parietal pleura due to an open chest wound. It occurs as a result of penetrating chest trauma or an open rib fracture. If the chest wound is greater than two-thirds the diameter of the trachea, air will dominantly enter through the wound during inspiration. Open pneumothorax must be covered with an occlusive dressing that is attached to the skin on three sides with adhesive tape so that one corner remains free. This forms a "flutter valve", which enables air to be expelled through the wound during exhalation but prevents "suction" during inhalation.

### MASSIVE HAEMOTHORAX

Massive haemothorax can result from blunt or penetrating trauma, and the source of bleeding can be in the lungs, major vessels, intercostal vessels, or even the heart. Massive haemothorax is defined by the presence of at least 1,500 mL of blood loss into the pleural space of the thoracic cavity. Clinical signs of shock may be apparent. The indications for thoracotomy are the following:

- blood loss > 1,500 mL or 1/3 blood volume
- blood loss > 200 mL/h (3 mL/kg/h) over 2-4 hours.

### FLAIL CHEST

Flail chest is an emergency condition resulting from a fracture of three or more adjacent ribs in two or more sites, causing chest wall instability and paradoxical movement of the "flail segment" in a spontaneously breathing patient. A flail segment no longer moves in continuity with the rest of the chest and retracts during inspiration and protrudes during expiration. It occurs as a result of blunt thoracic trauma in traffic accidents, violence, and falls. The most significant complication and accompanying pathophysiology is lung contusion, respiratory insufficiency, lung atelectasis, and pneumothorax. Management includes patient stabilisation, so adequate analgaesia, endotracheal intubation, and assisted ventilation are often required.

### DIAPHRAGMATIC RUPTURE

Diaphragmatic rupture caused by blunt trauma is not a common injury, but about 5% of polytrauma patients have this injury. Traffic accidents are the main cause of traumatic diaphragmatic ruptures. In many cases, the diaphragmatic rupture is initially not recognised due to severe multiple injuries. Most often, the diaphragm ruptures on the left side. Diaphragmatic injuries can occur as a result of blunt or penetrating trauma (especially on the left side). Blunt injury causes lacerations that can lead to herniation of abdominal structures into the thoracic cavity. Penetrating injuries can cause small perforations that may present as herniations years later. Diaphragm injuries should be suspected in severe blunt chest trauma, especially if compressive or rapid deceleration forces are involved.

### 2. ABDOMINAL INJURIES

Abdominal trauma is life-threatening due to the high possibility of haemorrhagic shock. Abdominal trauma is very difficult to assess even in hospital conditions, therefore, it is very important to obtain as much information as possible on the mechanism of injury in pre-hospital conditions and then consider an intra-abdominal injury. Minimising the time spent in the field and fast transport increases the chances of survival. According to the mechanism of occurrence, abdominal trauma can be divided into blunt or penetrating injuries. Signs and symptoms include abdominal pain, tenderness, abdominal wall tension, and bruising of the outer abdominal wall. In the case of a blunt abdominal injury, it is very important to look for external signs of injury such as skin abrasions, hematomas, and seat belt marks. A horizontal belt track is a clinical sign that carries a 30% risk of mesenteric and intestinal injury. The abdomen is palpated in four quadrants, and the pelvis and lower ribs should be examined for signs of fracture. In many patients subsequently diagnosed with an intra-abdominal injury, there are no clear signs of such an injury on the initial ITLS examination, so it is very important to obtain information about the mechanism of injury. For larger open injuries, it is extremely important not to attempt to return the organs to the abdominal cavity. Instead, cover them with warm gauze moistened with saline or water. Patient management includes obtaining several venous accesses, administering prescribed analgaesics,

constantly monitoring the patient, and observing their condition until arrival at the hospital.

### BLUNT ABDOMINAL INJURIES

Blunt abdominal injuries are caused by the blunt action of an external force and are a diagnostic challenge due to the very frequent absence of visible signs on the body surface. They are usually caused by traffic accidents, falls from a height, bicycle handlebar injuries, sports injuries, and intentional injuries. Intra-abdominal bleeding, hollow organ injury, and retroperitoneal injury may occur in the case of a blunt abdominal injury. The most common causes of intra-abdominal bleeding are ruptured spleen, liver, and, less often, mesenteric blood vessels. The symptoms are the same as in hypovolaemic shock, with pain sensitivity and distension. Rupture of the spleen is most often associated with a fracture of the lower ribs. The consequence of rupture of the initial jejunum or terminal ileum is peritonitis. Rupture occurs due to midabdominal compression between the external force and the spine. The main symptoms are pain, nausea, and hard stomach. Vomiting and abdominal distention occur in the case of intestinal paresis. In the case of abdominal contusion, rupture most often occurs in the less mobile part of the intestines, such as the jejunum, and terminal ileum. Duodenal rupture usually occurs if the abdominal muscles are relaxed at the time of abdominal contusion. The spleen is the most frequently injured organ of the abdominal region during trauma and the second most frequently injured organ in children. Retroperitoneal haematoma is most often the result of a kidney rupture, which can be combined on the left side with a ruptured spleen. In the retroperitoneal space, the pancreas and duodenum can be injured as a result of compression between the external force and the spine. Pancreatic injury can manifest as traumatic pancreatitis, which is the most common form of pancreatic injury.

## Trauma assessment and managment

### PENETRATING ABDOMINAL INJURIES

Penetrating injuries are mostly caused by stabbing or firearms. These injuries cause damage to the blood vessels of the abdominal wall, but injuries to the internal abdominal organs (liver, spleen, stomach, intestines, and mesentery) can also occur. Sometimes a part of the intestinal coil protrudes through the opening on the abdominal wall, which is a definite sign of a penetrating injury. If the wound is caused by a projectile, then a penetrating abdominal injury could result from an entry wound at almost any part of the body. In the case of larger open injuries, it is extremely important not to attempt to return the organs to the abdominal cavity. Instead, cover them with warm gauze moistened with saline or water.

## PELVIC INJURIES

Pelvic injuries are complex because they are mostly caused by trauma with high energy transfer (traffic accidents, falls from a greater height...) and can be accompanied by profuse bleeding and rapid development of haemorrhagic shock. In most cases, a pelvic fracture appears associated with injuries to other body regions (intra-thoracic and intra-abdominal injuries). In the case of a pelvic fracture, we can expect profuse bleeding and the rapid development of circulatory shock. Stabilisation of the pelvis is performed with a pelvic splint. Pelvic stabilisation is an important and simple intervention in the treatment of severe pelvic trauma. It has four main goals:

- preventing further tissue damage caused by possible pathological movement of the fractured pelvic bones
- reducing the volume of the pelvis
- minimising bleeding from injured bony structures and soft tissue reducing the patient's pain. When do we need to use a pelvic splint?
- in a haemodynamically unstable patient with a mechanically unstable pelvis
- in a haemodynamically unstable patient with a suspected pelvic fracture
- in a haemodynamically stable patient with a mechanically un stable pelvis.
