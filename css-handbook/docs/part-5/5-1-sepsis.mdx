## INTRODUCTION

Sepsis is a severe and potentially life-threatening condition that occurs when the body's immune system responds inadequately to infection, leading to widespread inflammation and tissue damage. It is important to understand basic pathophysiology to diagnose and treat it effectively. The mortality rate of sepsis is high, ranging from 20-50%, depending on the underlying pathology and the timing of initial treatment. The excessive release of inflammatory mediators during sepsis leads to generalised vasodilation and increased permeability of the blood vessels, causing relative hypovolaemia and hypoperfusion with damage to tissues and organs throughout the body. To diagnose sepsis and initiate prompt treatment, two criteria should be met:

1. the presence of an infection (suspected or documented: e.g., justified suspicion is sufficient, such as patient history suggesting urinary infection, physical examination involving audible rales over part of the lungs, etc.)
2. change in Sequential Organ Failure Assessment (SOFA) score of at least 2 points (Table 2). However, the SOFA score requires specific laboratory findings for interpretation, which takes time. To overcome this limitation, a quick SOFA (qSOFA) score was developed, which consists of three clinical findings available through physical examination, making it

easy to use and repeat as often as necessary. Timely recognition and treatment of sepsis are essential determinants of patient outcome.

Early recognition and prompt treatment of septic shock are critical for reducing mortality rates. Treatment includes early antibiotic administration, fluid resuscitation, and vasoactive medications to maintain adequate blood pressure and organ perfusion.

The initial management of sepsis involves the following:

### A (AIRWAY)

- Assess the patient's airway to ensure it is open. In cases of obstruction due to the collapse of soft tissues, open the airway by tilting the head, lifting the mandible, and placing an oropharyngeal or nasopharyngeal airway if necessary.
- It is essential to note that patients with sepsis, particularly the elderly, may have reduced consciousness that can lead to airway obstruction, often audible as snoring.

### B (BREATHING)

- Check the symmetry of chest expansion on inhalation and respiratory rate, and perform lung auscultation and percussions.
- Place a pulse oximeter to monitor oxygen saturation.
- Provide high-flow oxygen.

- Patients with sepsis may experience respiratory failure due to several reasons. For instance, sepsis often originates from pneumonia, leading to tachypnoea as the body compensates for the reduced diffusion of gases in the lungs due to the inflammatory infiltrate. Another possibility is sepsis caused by an infection of a different origin, leading to metabolic acidosis, which the body compensates for by increasing the respiratory rate (by reducing p6CO2, the body returns the pH to physiological levels). Patients with sepsis often have a respiratory rate greater than 22/min. If a patient with sepsis has a respiratory rate &lt; 20/min, uses auxiliary respiratory muscles, and has a small inspiratory volume, this indicates respiratory fatigue, which needs to be treated with non-invasive or invasive mechanical ventilation.

### SEPTIC SHOCK

Septic shock is a severe and life-threatening condition that can occur in patients with sepsis. It is characterised by abnormal circulatory physiology and cellular metabolism, resulting in increased mortality. To diagnose septic shock, the patient must meet the following criteria, in addition to the diagnosis of sepsis:

1. hypotension with mean arterial pressure below 65 mmHg, despite fluid resuscitation with a 30 mL/kg of balanced crystalloid solution (this is needed to rule out hypovolaemia as the cause of low blood pressure),
2. serum lactate levels higher than 2 mmol/L.

Prolonged capillary refill time may be indicative of tissue and organ hypoperfusion; the more pronounced the hypoperfusion, the longer the capillary refill time will be. ECG monitoring is necessary as sepsis can cause new arrhythmias or worsen existing ones, and may lead to cardiac arrest due to hypoxia and/or (relative) hypovolaemia. If hypotension is marked, the pulse may only be palpated in the central blood vessels, with a systolic blood pressure greater than approximately 80 mmHg when palpated on the radial artery. Establish at least one large-bore intravenous line (* 18 G) to enable the rapid administration of balanced crystalloid intravenous fluids at a dose of 30 mL/kg of body weight. Once an intravenous line is established, perform laboratory tests such as complete blood count, C-reactive protein, procalcitonin, sodium, potassium, creatinine, and liver function test, and obtain blood and microbial samples from the presumed origin of infection (such as urine culture). Additionally, arterial blood gases should be measured to calculate the SOFA score. Place a urinary catheter to monitor hourly urine output, check for pyuria, and send urine for analysis. The administration of intravenous antibiotics as soon as possible is an ABSOLUTE PRIORITY in patients with suspected sepsis. Start with a broad-spectrum antibiotic, and adapt it to the microorganisms most likely causing the infection at the presumed site of origin. Patients with sepsis require hospitalisation, and those with respiratory insufficiency or septic shock require intensive care. Sepsis is a life-threatening condition with a mortality rate of up to 50% and is a significant cause of death worldwide. Timely recognition and appropriate management are essential to improve patient outcomes. Therefore, it is crucial to recognise the signs and symptoms of sepsis and initiate appropriate treatment promptly.

### D (DISABILITY)

- Perform a neurological assessment of the patient, including AVPU or GCS scale and pupil examination. Check the patient's blood glucose to rule out hypo-/hyperglycaemia as the cause of altered consciousness.
- Patients with diabetes may experience difficulty regulating their blood glucose levels, which could be the first sign of an infection.

### E (EXPOSURE AND ENVIRONMENT)

- If the source of sepsis has a surgical cause, such as perforated appendicitis, it should be surgically managed as soon as possible to achieve source control.
