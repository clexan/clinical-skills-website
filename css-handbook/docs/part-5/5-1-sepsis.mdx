## Introduction

Sepsis is a severe and potentially life-threatening condition that occurs when the body’s immune system responds inadequately to infection, leading to widespread inflammation and tissue damage. Basic pathophysiology is important for timely diagnosis and effective treatment.

The mortality rate of sepsis is high, ranging from 20–50%, depending on the underlying pathology and the timing of initial treatment. Excessive release of inflammatory mediators causes generalised vasodilation and increased vascular permeability, resulting in relative hypovolaemia, hypoperfusion, and tissue and organ injury.

To diagnose sepsis and initiate prompt treatment, two criteria should be met:

1. The presence of infection, whether suspected or documented. Justified clinical suspicion is sufficient, such as a history suggesting urinary infection or physical examination showing focal lung crackles.
2. An increase in the Sequential Organ Failure Assessment (SOFA) score of at least 2 points.

Because the SOFA score requires laboratory data and is therefore slower to apply in acute settings, the quick SOFA (qSOFA) score is often used as an initial bedside screening tool.

Timely recognition and treatment are essential determinants of outcome.

Early recognition and prompt treatment of septic shock are critical for reducing mortality. Treatment includes early antibiotic administration, fluid resuscitation, and vasoactive medication to maintain blood pressure and organ perfusion.

## Common Sources of Infection

1. Respiratory tract
2. Abdomen
3. Urinary tract
4. Indwelling catheters
5. Skin and soft tissues

## Diagnostic Criteria

### qSOFA

A qSOFA score of 2 or more suggests a high risk of poor outcome in a patient with suspected infection.

| Criterion | Threshold |
|---|---:|
| Respiratory rate | ≥ 22/min |
| Systolic blood pressure | < 100 mmHg |
| Mental status | GCS < 15 |

## Septic Shock

Septic shock is a severe and life-threatening subset of sepsis characterised by abnormal circulatory physiology and cellular metabolism with increased mortality.

To diagnose septic shock, the patient must meet the criteria for sepsis and also have:

1. Hypotension with mean arterial pressure below 65 mmHg despite fluid resuscitation with 30 mL/kg of a balanced crystalloid solution.
2. Serum lactate level greater than 2 mmol/L.

## Initial Management: ABCDE + SAMPLE

### A (Airway)

- Assess whether the airway is open.
- If obstruction is caused by collapse of soft tissues, open the airway with head tilt and chin lift or jaw thrust, and insert an oropharyngeal or nasopharyngeal airway if needed.
- Patients with sepsis, especially elderly patients, may have impaired consciousness that leads to partial airway obstruction, often audible as snoring.

### B (Breathing)

- Assess chest expansion symmetry, respiratory rate, percussion, and auscultation.
- Apply continuous pulse oximetry.
- Provide high-flow oxygen.
- Respiratory failure in sepsis may occur for several reasons. Pneumonia may cause tachypnoea due to impaired gas exchange, while sepsis from another source may cause metabolic acidosis with compensatory hyperventilation.
- Patients with sepsis often have a respiratory rate above 22/min.
- If a patient with suspected sepsis has a respiratory rate below 20/min together with use of accessory respiratory muscles and low inspiratory volume, consider respiratory fatigue and the need for non-invasive or invasive mechanical ventilation.

### C (Circulation)

- Assess heart rate, blood pressure, capillary refill time, and central and peripheral pulses.
- Maintain ECG monitoring.
- Tachycardia may be present, but it is not universal. Elderly patients and patients taking beta-blockers may not compensate by increasing heart rate.
- Hypotension is common because inflammatory mediators cause vasodilation and increased endothelial permeability.
- Assess for hypovolaemia and treat with a bolus of balanced crystalloid solution.
- If hypotension persists after initial fluid resuscitation, start a vasopressor such as noradrenaline at 0.01–0.5 μg/kg/min.
- Prolonged capillary refill may indicate tissue hypoperfusion.
- Sepsis can provoke new arrhythmias, worsen existing arrhythmias, and contribute to cardiac arrest through hypoxia and relative hypovolaemia.
- In marked hypotension, pulses may be palpable only centrally.
- Establish at least one large-bore intravenous line (≥ 18 G) to allow rapid administration of balanced crystalloids at 30 mL/kg.
- Once vascular access is secured, obtain blood tests including complete blood count, C-reactive protein, procalcitonin, sodium, potassium, creatinine, and liver function tests.
- Obtain blood cultures and microbiological samples from the presumed source of infection, such as urine culture.
- Measure arterial blood gases to help calculate the SOFA score and assess lactate.
- Insert a urinary catheter to monitor hourly urine output, check for pyuria, and send urine for analysis.
- **Intravenous antibiotics as soon as possible are an absolute priority in suspected sepsis.** Start with broad-spectrum therapy and tailor treatment to the most likely pathogens and source.
- The first-hour bundle refers to the key measures that should be initiated within one hour of hospital admission or recognition.

### D (Disability)

- Perform a neurological assessment using AVPU or GCS, and examine pupils.
- Check blood glucose to exclude hypo- or hyperglycaemia as a cause of altered mental status.
- In patients with diabetes, poor glycaemic control may be an early clue to infection.

### E (Exposure and Environment)

- Measure body temperature. A septic patient may be febrile, normothermic, or hypothermic.
- Elderly patients with multiple comorbidities may not develop fever; normal temperature does not exclude sepsis.
- Examine the patient thoroughly for visible sources of infection, such as cellulitis, a gangrenous foot, an infected wound, or abdominal distension.
- If the source is surgical, such as perforated appendicitis, source control should be achieved as early as possible.
- Keep the patient warm.

## Disposition

Patients with sepsis require hospital admission. Patients with respiratory insufficiency or septic shock require intensive care.

Sepsis remains a major global cause of death, with mortality reaching up to 50% in severe cases. Recognition and rapid treatment are critical for improving outcomes.
