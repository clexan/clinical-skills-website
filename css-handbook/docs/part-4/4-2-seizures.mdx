## INTRODUCTION

Seizures are caused by abnormal electrical impulses in the brain that disrupt normal brain function, resulting in acute neurological deterioration. This can manifest as decreased consciousness and focal or generalised involuntary movements. Diagnosis is usually based on clinical presentation, laboratory findings, electroencephalogram (EEG), and imaging studies such as CT or MRI of the brain. Treatment involves identifying and addressing the underlying cause, administering anticonvulsant drugs, and in some cases, surgical intervention. Overall, understanding the causes and types of seizures is crucial for the effective diagnosis and management of patients with seizure disorders. As medical professionals, it is important to have a thorough understanding of the conditions to provide optimal care for our patients. Seizures can be classified into different types, including:

- Epilepsy (epileptic convulsive disorder) - a chronic brain disorder characterised by recurrent seizures that are not caused by a reversible factor.
- Symptomatic epilepsy - seizures due to a known reason, such as brain tumour, TBI, stroke, etc.
- Non-epileptic seizures - seizures caused by a temporary systemic disorder that interferes with normal brain function, e.g., electrolyte disorders, hyperthermia, etc.

## TREATMENT

Effective management of seizures requires attention to both general measures and drug therapy. One of the most critical aspects of management is securing the airway to ensure adequate airflow. For example, in patients with generalised tonic-clonic seizures, it is important to clear the area around the neck to prevent compression from clothing. Attempting to remove the patient's tongue should be avoided, as this can result in injury to the rescuer's fingers, as well as injury to the patient. Patients should be turned to their left side to decrease the risk of aspiration in the case of vomiting. If a generalised motor seizure occurs, immediate administration of medication is indicated. There are three lines of drug treatment for seizures. The firstline drugs include benzodiazepines such as midazolam, lorazepam,

and diazepam. In prehospital settings, midazolam can be administered intramuscularly (0.2 mg/kg to a maximum of 10 mg), intranasally (0.2 mg/kg to a maximum of 5 mg), or buccally (0.5 mg/kg to a maximum of 10 mg). Buccal administration of lorazepam (0.1 mg/kg to a maximum of 4 mg) or rectal administration of diazepam (0.5 mg/kg to a maximum of 20 mg) are also options. In the hospital setting, the drugs of choice for convulsive status are also midazolam (0.1 mg/kg to a maximum of 5 mg), lorazepam (0.1 mg/kg to a maximum of 4 mg), or diazepam (0.3 mg/kg to a maximum of 10 mg), administered intravenously. Prehospital medication administration should be considered when choosing the next line and dose of treatment. If the patient does not respond to the first-line treatment or has received the medication twice, second-line drugs such as phenytoin (20 mg/kg to a maximum of 1000 mg), phenobarbital (20 mg/kg to a maximum of 1000 mg), and levetiracetam (60 mg/kg to a maximum of 3000 mg) can be used. If the patient remains in a convulsive state after the first administration of the second-line drugs, another second-line drug can be given. If symptoms persist, third-line drugs such as propofol (2.5 mg/kg initially, continuous infusion 3 mg/kg/h), thiopental (2-5 mg/kg initially, continuous infusion 1-4 mg/kg/h), or midazolam (continuous infusion 1 mcg/kg/h) may be used in the intensive care setting. In such cases, securing the airway with endotracheal intubation and mechanical ventilation is important, along with other intensive care measures.

## INITIAL PATIENT CARE: ABCDE + SAMPLE

Proper initial care is essential when managing a patient with seizures, The following steps can help ensure the patient's safety:

### A (AIRWAY)

- Maintain an open airway by tilting the head and using supraglottic aids such as the oropharyngeal or nasopharyngeal airway.
- Endotracheal intubation may be necessary if hypnotics are used.

### B (BREATHING)

- Administer 100% oxygen via face mask with reservoir.
- Continuously measure SpO2 values.
- Regularly assess the respiratory rate and the need for mechanical ventilation.

### C (CIRCULATION)

- Continuously monitor vital signs.
- Check pulse and blood pressure, particularly for hypotension when using hypnotics.
- Place two wide venous lines as soon as possible.
- Perform standard laboratory tests for electrolyte disorders.

The patient's age at the time of the first seizure can provide clues to the underlying cause. For example, febrile states and hereditary neurological or metabolic disorders are common causes of seizures in patients younger than 2. Idiopathic convulsive disorders are the most common cause of seizures in patients between the ages of 2 and 14. In adulthood, seizures most often occur due to brain tumours, TBI, or stroke. To properly classify seizure disorders, it is important to recognise whether the seizure is focal or generalised, as well as whether a motor component is present. Generalised convulsive disorders involve electrical impulses that affect both hemispheres of the brain, often leading to impaired consciousness. Within this category, seizures can present with a motor response such as tonic-clonic, exclusively tonic, or exclusively clonic. In contrast, seizure disorders with a focal response are characterised by impulses originating from one hemisphere, usually from subcortical structures. These types of seizures often present with simpler manifestations, such as automatisms, muscle atony, or clonic muscle spasms, with or without loss of consciousness. Focal seizures can also appear without the motor component and may involve dysfunction of the autonomic nervous system (gastrointestinal sensations, feelings of heat or cold) or cognitive and/or emotional impairment. It is important to note that a focal seizure can progress to a generalised seizure at any moment! Status epilepticus is a life-threatening condition caused by a continuous burst of impulses in the central nervous system. It can manifest in two forms: convulsive and non-convulsive. Convulsive status epilepticus is characterised by tonic-clonic activity lasting longer than 5 minutes or two consecutive seizures without regaining consciousness. Non-convulsive status epilepticus is typically characterised by changes in mental status and can be more challenging to diagnose.

E (exposure):

- Remove the patient's clothing but avoid tying the patient to prevent injuries from seizures.
- Remove clothing or jewellery around the neck to prevent suffocation.
- Check for injuries such as tongue biting and sphincter relaxation. In addition to the ABCDE approach, obtaining a SAMPLE or AMPLE history is important: S (Signs/symptoms): Examine the symptoms, including any presence of an aura before the seizure (headache, dizziness, sensory sensations). A (Allergies): Check for drug and food allergies, particularly those related to the use of antiepileptic drugs. M (Medications): Determine if the patient is already taking antiepileptic drugs or other medications. P (Past illnesses): Check if this is the first manifestation of epilepsy or if the patient has a previous diagnosis of seizure disorder. L (Last oral intake): Determine when the patient last ate and whether there is a risk of stomach content aspiration. E (Events leading up to present illness/injury): Identify any precipitating factors for the onset of seizures, such as loud sounds, bright lights, insomnia, etc.

### D (DISABILITY)

- Check glucose levels as hypoglycaemia is a common cause of seizures. Administer 40% glucose in the case of hypoglycaemia.
- Assess the patient's state of consciousness using the AVPU/ GCS scale.
