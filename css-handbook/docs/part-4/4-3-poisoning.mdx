## INTRODUCTION

Poisoning is a clinical condition characterised by a sudden or gradual disease onset due to contact with the poison. The symptoms that develop are closely related to the type of poison. Its absorption into the bloodstream is essential for the systemic action of the poison. Acute poisoning requires prompt diagnosis and urgent treatment. As these are sometimes substances we do not encounter daily, it is important to have constant access to the data and possible therapeutic interventions that should be done in the event of poisoning (usually a contact number for a poison control centre). Any substance taken in the wrong dose can be toxic. Acute poisoning can be accidental, suicidal, criminal, environmental, or industrial. They are usually accidental. Suicidal poisoning is caused by the deliberate intake of poison to cause death. Environmental poisonings are usually caused by food, but they can also be caused by plants or animals, such as venomous snakes or spiders.

## INITIAL PATIENT ASSESSMENT: ABCDE + SAMPLE

### A (AIRWAY)

- Patency primarily depends on the effect of the poison on the state of consciousness.
- In the case of poison ingestion, damaged airways are a possibility (e.g., strong acids).
- Gastric content in the mouth (in the case of vomiting).
- Some poisons may cause seizures, and bite marks on the tongue can be visible.
- Airway manoeuvres depend on the patient's state.

### C (CIRCULATION)

- Possible abnormalities (depending on the poison - fast or slow heart rate, arrhythmias) are diverse.
- Blood pressure can be affected (depending on the dose and poison).
- Skin appearance and CRT may indicate shock.
- Bleeding can also happen because of poisoning (e.g., unintentional anticoagulant poisoning).
- IV access and standard monitoring are obligatory.
- Some poisons have specific antidotes that should be applied in a life-threatening condition.

### D (DISABILITY)

- Level of consciousness can be affected due to the direct effect of the poison on the central nervous system (CNS) or indirectly by affecting other vital functions.
- In the case of a coma, advanced airway management and mechanical ventilation are indicated.
- Pupils may help differentiate poisons (e.g., myosis in opiate poisoning, mydriasis in sympathomimetic drug poisoning) and whether the poisoning caused severe CNS damage (for example, diffuse brain oedema - unreactive dilated pupils).
- Glucose levels can be high/normal/low (persons with type 1 diabetes may attempt suicide with insulin).

### E (EXPOSURE)

- Very important! Check the patient and their surroundings, which can provide critical clues about the type and dose of poison and potential injuries.
- Suicide attempt - the means of the attempt are usually in the person's immediate environment (i.e., empty pill blisters).
- SAMPLE - be thorough; it varies depending on the type of poisoning. Specific measures that can be taken include decontamination, stimulation of excretion, and administration of specific antidotes. These are taken immediately, as soon as we identify the poison and assess the patient. Since we are surrounded by an infinite number of poisons, it is impossible to know specific measures for all of them - hence the establishment of poison control centres available 24/7.

Decontamination is directly dependent on the type of contact between the poison and the body. Usually, this means decontamination of the digestive system by performing gastric lavage. Rinsing the eyes that came into contact with a chemical is also a method of decontamination. There is no evidence of the usefulness of gastric lavage and/or activated charcoal. Stimulation of excretion can be done via the gastrointestinal system or urinary tract. Administering activated charcoal is one way of stimulating excretion. If a poison can be excreted in the urine or filtered by dialysis, renal replacement therapy may be commenced in a critically ill patient. Some poisons suitable for this are barbiturates, lithium, metformin, salicylates, and methanol. Antidotes are drugs with an effect opposite to the poison. Airway management must be done prior to antidote administration. Also, it is important to note that some antidotes have a shorter time of duration than the poison. Therefore, it is crucial to monitor the patient and repeat the doses if needed. One such antidote is naloxone.

### B (BREATHING)

- Respiratory rate may be affected (e.g., opiate poisoning - low respiratory rate).
- Abnormal auscultatory findings may help in the suspicion of aspiration of gastric content in a patient with an altered level of consciousness.
- If the airway is not patent and breathing is inadequate, the saturation (SpO2) will decrease - if so, start oxygen therapy and secure the airway properly.
