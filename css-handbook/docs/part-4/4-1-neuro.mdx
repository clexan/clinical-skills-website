Sudden neurological deterioration is a common reason for patient admission to emergency medical services. It can be caused by various critical illnesses or injuries. Prompt treatment is essential to limit secondary brain injury and improve patient outcome. Primary injury is the initial brain damage caused by a critical illness or injury. Regardless of the cause, primary injury triggers secondary injury, which can lead to further brain damage. Our management can affect secondary brain damage through interventions that address hypoxia, hypotension, and increased intracranial pressure.

## CAUSES OF SUDDEN NEUROLOGICAL DETERIORATION AND POTENTIAL DIFFERENTIAL DIAGNOSIS

### NEUROLOGICAL CAUSES

- Ischaemic or haemorrhagic stroke.
- Traumatic brain injury (TBI; the morphological substrate spanning from epidural haematoma, subdural haematoma, brain contusions to diffuse axonal injury, and brain oedema).
- brain tumours, etc.

### SYSTEMIC CAUSES

- Infection (septic encephalopathy, central nervous system (CNS) infection in the form of meningitis and/or encephalitis).

## INITIAL PATIENT ASSESSMENT AND MANAGEMENT:

### ABCDE + SAMPLE

### A (AIRWAY)

- Check the airway patency as CNS depression may cause partial or complete loss of airway patency. Noisy breathing/snoring and asynchronous movement of the chest and abdomen are signs of partial obstruction, while complete obstruction is silent with visible accessory respiratory muscles involvement and see-saw phenomenon for a brief period before the patient goes into cardiac arrest.
- In suspected trauma, place the cervical spine stabilisation.
- Aspirate any blood or gastric content from the airway, if present.
- Use simple airway opening manoeuvres (head-tilt chin-lift or jaw-thrust) or oro-/nasopharyngeal airway to secure the airway if it is not patent.
- Tracheal intubation and mechanical ventilation are indicated in patients with GCS &lt;= 8, as they may lose protective airway reflexes and develop hypoventilation with resultant hypercapnia, which worsens intracranial hypertension. However, advanced techniques for securing the airway, including the use of anaesthetic and neuromuscular blocking drugs, should only be performed by experienced personnel, as failed attempts of tracheal intubation can worsen secondary brain injury.

### B (BREATHING)

- Assess breathing using the "look, listen, and feel" approach to evaluate respiratory rate and effort, breath and added sounds, and chest movement symmetry, as well as breathing patterns associated with different causes of decreased consciousness, such as Cheyne-Stokes and Kussmaul breathing. Look for possible causes leading to hypoxia, such as subcutaneous emphysema, tracheal deviation, and jugular vein distention.
- Monitor oxygen saturation (SpO2) continuously using a pulse oximeter and end-expiratory CO2 (EtCO2), if available.
- Administer oxygen if SpO2 is below 90%.
- Ensure adequate oxygenation at all times to prevent secondary brain injury and maintain blood oxygen saturation above 90%.
- If the patient has insufficient minute ventilation and oxygenation, even after opening the airway and administering oxygen therapy, start with manual ventilation using the tight-fitting face mask and self-inflating bag (e.g., Ambu bag) and consider anaesthesia and tracheal intubation if experienced in the procedure. Insufficient minute ventilation can be expected in bradypnoeic patients, for instance, those overdosed with opioids or with imminent brainstem herniation, and patients with respiratory muscle fatigue, such as those with acute respiratory insufficiency.

### C (CIRCULATION)

- Assess heart rate, blood pressure, capillary refill time, skin colour, and (hourly) urine output, and monitor these parameters every 3-5 minutes. Look for patterns that might indicate the cause of sudden neurological deterioration, such as Cushing's triad.
- Place continuous ECG monitoring.
- Hypotension, along with hypoxia, is a major contributor to secondary brain injury (CPP=MAP(ICP, where CPP is cerebral perfusion pressure, MAP is mean arterial pressure, and ICP is intracranial pressure).
- In the initial management of a patient with neurological deterioration, aim for systolic blood pressure of above 110 mmHg (or MAP above 70 mmHg) to achieve adequate brain perfusion.
- If blood pressure is below the target values, consider administering:
- **a)** volume resuscitation with 0.9% sodium chloride (or hypertonic sodium chloride), as this is the crystalloid solution with osmolarity similar to or higher than the human plasma and will not worsen brain oedema.
- **b)** vasoconstrictors, such as noradrenaline, 0.01-0.5 mcg/kg/min IV, dose titrated up to target blood pressure with regular blood pressure assessments.
- Once IV access is established, obtain blood samples for testing, including complete blood count (CBC), glucose, sodium, potassium, and coagulation tests; with additional tests such as C-reactive protein (CRP), procalcitonin, urea, creatinine, ammonium, liver enzymes, ABG, etc., added depending on the differential diagnosis.

### D (DISABILITY/OTHER ACUTE CONSIDERATIONS)

- Before performing the neurological assessment, correct hypoxia and hypotension as they can cause or contribute to the depth of decreased consciousness.
- Begin by assessing blood glucose level, Glasgow Coma Score (GCS), pupillary size, symmetry, and light response. Check for differences in motor response between the left and right side, and perform a targeted neurological examination to look for signs of increased intracranial pressure, meningeal irritation, and subtle myoclonus such as eyelid fasciculation, as well as evidence of previous seizure: lateral tongue biting, uncontrolled urination, etc.).
- Organ system failure (e.g., shock, renal failure, hepatic failure, hypoxaemia and/or hypercarbia due to respiratory failure). Endocrine disorders (hypoglycaemia, hyperglycaemia, thyroid dysfunction, Addison crisis, etc.). Electrolyte and acid-base disorders (hypo- or hypernatraemia, hypercalcaemia, hypophophytaemia, etc.). Drugs/poisoning (narcotics, benzodiazepines, CO poisoning) Hypo- and hyperthermia.

HYPOGLYCAEMIA: hypoglycaemia is a common and easily treatable cause of a comatose state. If present, administering glucose is an absolute priority, ideally in high concentration (e.g., 40% glucose), titrated up to recovery of consciousness and adequate blood glucose levels. If rapid glucose testing is not available in a patient with sudden neurological deterioration, then 50 mL of 40% glucose should be administered, as the potential benefit of treating hypoglycaemia outweighs the potential risk associated with hyperglycaemia. SEIZURES: repeated or prolonged seizures add substantially to secondary brain injury and should be stopped immediately. Administer IV benzodiazepines. SIGNS OF ELEVATED INTRACRANIAL PRESSURE: For example, decreased consciousness with present anisocoria or dilated unresponsive pupils, Cushing's triad, etc. Administer the following: mannitol 0.5-1 g/kg IV +/- furosemide 20 mg IV (before applying this therapy, place a urinary catheter) consider hyperventilation (shortly) elevation of the head of the bed to 15-30° (increases venous drainage and decreases ICP) if adequately trained for the administration of anaesthetics, give analgaesia and sedation and proceed with tracheal intubation and mechanical ventilation of the patient. DRUG OVERDOSE: if available, administer an antidote (e.g., naloxone in opiate overdose, flumazenil in benzodiazepine overdose, etc.).
