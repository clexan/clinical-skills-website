Oxygen is one of the most commonly used drugs in modern medicine, and until recently, it was considered harmless and no special dosing recommendations were needed. However, today, we are aware of many adverse effects of oxygen, which, like any other drug, has a recommended dosage regimen, administration route, side effects, indications, and contraindications. We all breathe oxygen at slightly less than 21% (FiO2 0.21). When climbing to high altitudes, the percentage of oxygen remains the same, but the partial pressure of gases decreases. Administering oxygen increases oxygen concentration in the inspired air. The oxygen dose is the FiO2, which is determined clinically (administration route and flow rate). Loss of haemoglobin in bleeding significantly reduces the oxygen-carrying capacity of the blood and that can lead to severe tissue hypoxia, even with normal saturation levels of the (remaining) haemoglobin. Pulse oximetry is a non-invasive method of continuous measurement of haemoglobin oxygen saturation. The probe is placed at the fingertip, and the screen shows the SpO2 percentage, oximetry curve, and numerical heart rate. It measures light absorption from the blood at two different wavelengths - 660 nm and 940 nm. One fraction of light passes through the tissue and another one is absorbed in the tissue. It uses two wavelengths to distinguish between oxyhaemoglobin and deoxyhaemoglobin molecules.

### LIMITATIONS

- It does not provide information about ventilation, only about oxygenation.
- In the case of carbon monoxide poisoning, a falsely high reading occurs due to similar wavelengths of oxyhaemoglobin and carboxyhaemoglobin.
