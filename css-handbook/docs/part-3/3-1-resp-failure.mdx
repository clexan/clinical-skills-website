## INTRODUCTION

Acute respiratory failure happens as a primary failure in breathing or as a secondary failure following other disorders in the body. It is characterised by the inability of the lungs to meet the body's requirements for oxygen and/or carbon dioxide removal. Respiratory control is a complex activity. Automated rhythmic breathing is controlled by the brain stem through multiple neuronal groups. It can be interrupted by coughing and vomiting and is susceptible to voluntary control (e.g., speech). Central chemoreceptors in the medulla oblongata are sensitive to hydrogen ion concentration (H+). An increase in the arterial carbon dioxide partial pressure (PaCO2) increases the PCO2 in the cerebrospinal fluid (CSF), which causes a drop in pH and drives breathing. Peripheral chemoreceptors (in the carotid bodies) are sensitive to hypoxia, but only by sensing a drop in the arterial oxygen partial pressure (PaO2) and not so much the total oxygen content. Acidaemia, fever, and hypoperfusion are also important ventilation drives.

### OXYGEN TRANSPORT IN THE BLOOD

Oxygen is transported via haemoglobin (oxyhaemoglobin), and a smaller fraction is dissolved in the blood. Oxygen delivery (DO') is described by the following equation:

DO'=CO x ((1.39 x [Hb] x SaO2)+ (PaO2 x 0.03)) DO' - oxygen delivery (mL/min) CO - cardiac output (L/min)

1. 39 mL/g - oxygen-binding capacity of haemoglobin [Hb] - haemoglobin concentration (g/L) SaO2 - blood oxygen saturation (haemoglobin), expressed as a fraction (e.g., 0.97 instead of 97%) PaO2 - oxygen partial pressure in arterial blood (mmHg)
0. 03 - amount of dissolved oxygen in the blood (mL/L/mmHg) (37 °C) Under normal circumstances, oxygen delivery is abundant compared to oxygen consumption. When something pathological occurs, there is an imbalance between oxygen delivery and oxygen consumption. This is not exclusive to breathing difficulties. When a patient bleeds, the haemoglobin content decreases, which also alters the oxygen delivery capacity.

### OXYGEN2HAEMOGLOBIN DISSOCIATION CURVE

The curve shown above describes the relationship between oxygen saturation and partial pressure. It is not linear because the haemoglobin-oxygen affinity increases in an oxygen-rich environment. Haemoglobin is a tetramer, and each molecule binds four oxygen molecules. Each oxygen molecule binds independently. The binding of the first oxygen molecule increases the affinity for the next one (and so on). The curve shifts left or right depending on certain conditions (as shown in the figure above). A leftward shift (higher affinity/low tissue release) occurs with hypothermia, alkalosis, and carbon monoxide poisoning. A rightward shift (lower affinity/high tissue release) occurs in fever and acidosis. In practical terms, it means that more oxygen is released in the tissues during fever (which is good when the body is fighting an infection).

### HCO+, + H-

Deoxyhaemoglobin has approximately 3.5 times greater affinity for CO2. Why is that important? In chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), patients have trouble breathing the air out of their lungs effectively, which increases the amount of CO2 content in the body. Usually, it binds with deoxyhaemoglobin. When such a patient receives oxygen therapy, this often leads to a further increase in PaCO2 levels, which can be fatal (why and how is explained in a later chapter of this manual).

If we sum this all up and try to organise it in a better and clinically useful way, we can define four types of respiratory failure:

TYPE!. CLASSIC HYPOXAEMIC RESPIRATORY FAILUR PaO2 &lt; 60 mmHg (&lt; 8 kPa) CAUSES:

- alveolar oxygenation failure (low FiO2, alveolar collapse, alveoli filled with fluid or blood, infiltration…)
- disrupted transition of oxygen from the alveoli to the blood (interstitial lung disease, pulmonary vascular disease)
- reduced blood oxygen transport (obstructed flow, shunting, low Hb concentration, defective Hb molecule). TYPE 2. HYPERCAPNIC RESPIRATORY FAILURE PaCO2 > 45 mmHg (> 6kPa) Defined as an inability to adequately remove CO2 because of reduced alveolar minute ventilation. This increase also leads to hypoxaemia because of the substitution of O' with CO2. Hypoxia in this case is usually easily corrected with oxygen therapy. TYPE 3. PERIOPERATIVE RESPIRATORY FAILURE A specific type of failure due to the circumstances in which it occurs is caused by abnormal movements of the chest and abdomen due to surgery/anaesthesia (residual drug effects) or injury. Both hypoxia and hypercarbia occur. Treatment: prevention with an adequate level of anaesthesia and/or analgaesia, reducing unnecessary sedation, elevated head level, etc. TYPE 4. HIGH2DEMAND RESPIRATORY FAILURE The inability of healthy (or not primarily affected) lungs to maintain increased ventilation in the case of a systemic hypermetabolic state. Sepsis is one such condition. If the primary cause is left untreated or there is no therapeutic response due to the nature of the state, respiratory muscle fatigue sometimes requires mechanical ventilation.
