It is an emergency condition requiring immediate medical attention. It can be cardiogenic (an increase in hydrostatic pressure) or non-cardiogenic (an increase in microvascular permeability). If no lung injury is present, fluid flow in the intercellular space of the lung is primarily defined by hydrostatic pressure.

## INITIAL PATIENT ASSESSMENT: ABCDE + SAMPLE

### A (AIRWAY)

- Possible auditory phenomena from the upper airway because of fluid accumulation.

### B (BREATHING)

- Impaired gas exchange with subsequent hypoxia increases the work of breathing (WOB). It manifests with accessory respiratory muscle activation, high respiratory rate, and diaphoresis.
- Hypoxia-caused confusion may occur.
- Auscultatory findings: crackles, in the case of pleural effusion absent lung sounds.

### C (CIRCULATION)

- Signs of shock (tachycardia, initial hypertension as a product of sympathetic activation, hypotension develops in later stages)
- Skin is pale and clammy!
- ECG - various findings are possible, often depending on the underlying pathology causing pulmonary oedema.

### D (DISABILITY)

- All of the above can lead to impaired cerebral perfusion, which can affect consciousness. Confusion, agitation, and coma may occur.

### E (EXPOSURE AND ENVIRONMENT)

- Search for signs of heart failure (e.g., pretibial oedema indicates a probable cardiac cause). SAMPLE - symptoms: dyspnoea, orthopnoea, weakness, etc. Cardiogenic pulmonary oedema is usually preceded by chronic heart failure (of various causes). Non-cardiogenic oedema is more probable with a history of traffic accidents, infections, fever, etc.

### NON2CARDIOGENIC PULMONARY OEDEMA

Encompasses all clinical states promoting increased vascular permeability in the lungs resulting in an inflow of liquid and proteins in the interstitial and alveolar space. Causes of increased permeability:

- Direct injury (gastric fluid aspiration, pneumonia).
- States promoting acute capillary endothelial injury - systemic infections (sepsis), burns, trauma - they cause ALI (acute lung injury) and ARDS (acute respiratory distress syndrome). CONSEQUENCES:
- Respiratory failure (primarily type 1, progression to type 1 + type 2 in severe forms). TREATMENT:
- Treatment of the primary cause.
- Supportive treatment.
- Mechanical ventilation.

### CARDIOGENIC PULMONARY OEDEMA

If there is an underlying heart disease and it progresses or is left untreated, pulmonary oedema may occur at some stage (e.g., valvular defects, cardiomyopathies, etc.). Rarely it may happen due to other causes (e.g., volume overload, metabolic diseases, uncontrolled arterial hypertension). Typically, patients present with progressive dyspnoea. They are seated in the upright position, hoarse crackles are audible, they are tachypnoeic, and peripheral saturation is low with evident use of accessory respiratory muscles. Heart rate is usually rapid, the skin is diaphoretic, cold, and pale. Capillary refill time is prolonged. Blood pressure is usually elevated. The 12-lead ECG may

show various pathological findings, such as ischaemia, rhythm abnormalities, and more. In advanced stages, altered and reduced levels of consciousness can be expected. The purpose of this text is not to describe all possible pathological cardiac states that can lead to heart failure, but to present the most typical presentation and its treatment. The primary cause that leads to this presentation is left ventricle systolic failure. Because of a reduced ejection fraction, the LVEDP (left ventricular end-diastolic pressure) rises and fluid retention occurs. This increases high hydrostatic pressure in blood vessels and fills the alveoli with transudate (large amounts lead to pulmonary effusion). When the alveoli are filled with fluid, it is difficult for oxygen to reach the capillary bed, and because of reduced systolic function, blood delivery to the organs is compromised.

### BASIC PRINCIPLES - TREATMENT OF CARDIOGENIC OEDEMA

A (AIRWAY) AND B (BREATHING):

- Apply oxygen therapy if the patient is fully conscious and peripheral saturation is lower than 90%.
- In the case of inadequate response of a fully alert patient to oxygen therapy and peripheral saturation &lt; 90% or PaO2 &lt; 8 kPa with a respiratory rate of 25/min and over, non-invasive mechanical ventilation (NIV) should be considered.
- Endotracheal intubation and invasive mechanical ventilation are suitable for patients with life-threatening hypoxia and/or altered level of consciousness.

### C (CIRCULATION)

- High-dose loop diuretics with monitoring of urine output (caution
- Hypokalaemia).
- E.g., intravenous furosemide - the decision about initial dosing depends on the patient's chronic intake and dosage. If a new-onset acute heart failure occurs, the initial dose is a bolus of 40 mg IV, and the subsequent doses are adjusted depending on the diuretic response. In the case of acutely decompensated chronic heart failure (which is more common), patients often take doses as high as 125-250-500 mg daily (furosemide has a high therapeutic range). Repeating the chronic dose does not always result in expected or significant urine output, so repeated doses are necessary. Luckily, we can monitor the response by placing a urinary catheter and measuring urine output. Relief in symptoms is also a good sign of appropriate dosage. Always treat the patient, never the numbers.
- Nitrates or nitroprusside (contraindicated if the systolic blood pressure is below 110 mmHg), continuous intravenous infusion is the preferred route of administration (via perfusion pump).

### D (DISABILITY)

- Morphine - intravenous administration, anxiolysis, enhances oxygen delivery to the myocardium (indirectly reduces sympathetic activity, tachycardia, and hypertension) and can increase the compliance of a patient with non-invasive ventilation.
- Dosage - initially 2-4 mg IV, followed by further doses of 2 mg IV until the expected clinical response occurs (cooperative, calm). In the case of overdose - administer naloxone!

### CARDIOGENIC SHOCK

It is a condition defined as inadequate tissue perfusion following heart failure, and after excluding hypovolaemia as a possible cause. It is the most advanced stage of acutely decompensated heart failure (it happens in less than 2% of all cases). CHARACTERISTICS:

1. Systolic blood pressure below 90 mmHg (or a drop in MAP greater than 30 mmHg).
2. Heart rate over 60.
3. Oliguria.
4. Signs of organ congestion may or may not be present. The most common cause is acute decompensation of a previously known heart failure. It occurs rarely in patients with previously normal heart function/unknown heart disease (e.g., massive myocardial infarction).
