Partial pressure describes the number of molecules in a mixture of gases better than the percentage. For example, if we double the total pressure of a mixture, partial pressures go double, and the percentage stays the same. The atmospheric air we breathe is at 1 atm (100 kPa or 750 mmHg). The nitrogen partial pressure is 78 kPa (and 78%) and the oxygen partial pressure is 21 kPa (21%). When we breathe a 40% oxygen mixture, the oxygen partial pressure is 40 kPa. When a gas is dissolved in a liquid (e.g., blood), partial pressure in the liquid is the same as the gas that is in contact with the fluid. That is the Pmixture of a specific gas, e.g., PaO2 (partial pressure of oxygen in arterial blood). For arterial blood gas analysis and determining acid-base status, the following four key parameters are measured:

1. pH - hydrogen ion concentration in the body is extremely small (e.g., sodium concentration is 3 million times bigger), therefore, we use the pH scale (1-14) to determine hydrogen ion concentration. The pH of a normal blood sample is 7.35-7.45. The relationship between hydrogen ions and pH is inversely proportional. High pH means a drop in hydrogen ion levels. The increase in blood pH is called alkalaemia. Low pH means an increase in hydrogen ion levels. The drop in blood pH is called acidaemia. A decrease in the bicarbonate levels happens when they buffer the extra hydrogen ions. It is a state of metabolic acidosis. An increase in bicarbonate levels causes excess hydrogen ion buffering and a state of metabolic alkalosis. Metabolic and respiratory components are interdependent, and the derangement of one component typically causes the other to change as well to try to maintain a normal blood pH value. Because the bicarbonate does not directly change blood pH (it only serves as a buffer for the metabolic processes), there are some other things worth noting.

There are three independent variables that affect blood pH:

1. STRONG ION DIFFERENCE (SID) - a difference in the sum of strong cations (e.g., Na-, K-) and the sum of strong anions (e.g., Cl,, lactate).
2. Total concentration of plasma non-volatile buffers (A/0/) mostly determined by phosphates and albumin concentration.
3. PaCO2. These three variables have a direct influence on blood pH, whereas hydrogen ion and bicarbonate concentrations act as dependent variables. Standard base excess is a calculated value defined by how the SID should change to bring the pH value back to 7.4 with a steady-state CO2 value of 5.4 kPa (40 mmHg) at a normal body temperature. SID= ([Na- ]+[K-]+[Ca'-) ]+[Mg'-) ])-([Cl. ]+[lactate]) Metabolic acidosis is a product of a decreased SID value, which in turn causes chemical reactions that increase hydrogen ion concentration. SID is reduced when the human body loses sodium bicarbonate (e.g., diarrhoea) or in the abundance of exogenous anions (e.g., intoxication). Metabolic alkalosis is a product of an increase in SID, which occurs when there is a drop in strong anion concentration or an abundance of strong cations (e.g., vomiting, diuretic therapy). SID value is mostly regulated by kidneys, although the gastrointestinal system also contributes. Kidneys are unable to quickly excrete significant amounts of strong ions, and it takes a couple of minutes to hours to have an impact on SID. For every chloride ion that is filtrated and not reabsorbed, a reduction in SID happens. The equilibrium of chloride ion concentration is the main drive for SID changes. In the stomach, chloride ions are pumped into the lumen, which causes an increase in SID. However, it is reabsorbed in the duodenum and returns plasma SID to normal values. Should a person vomit, the chloride ion cannot be reabsorbed. The body loses chloride ions and that causes metabolic alkalosis (contrary to the seemingly logical conclusion that the loss of hydrogen ions is the main reason for alkalosis in such a scenario). Unlike the stomach, the pancreas produces fluid with higher SID levels than plasma (scarce in chloride ions). PaO- - normal partial pressure of oxygen in our atmosphere at sea level is 21 kPa (157.5 mmHg).
