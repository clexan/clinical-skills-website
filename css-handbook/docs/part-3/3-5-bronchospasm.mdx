In acute respiratory failure, one of the most common physical findings alongside dyspnoea and tachypnoea is wheezing. It occurs with prolonged airflow, through narrowed central and distal airways. It is more pronounced during exhalation, unlike in cases of upper respiratory tract obstruction when stridor is more pronounced during inspiration. Airway obstruction can occur due to bronchial muscle contraction (bronchospasm), smooth muscle hypertrophy, increased mucus secretion, and/or peribronchial inflammation. Wheezing is usually associated with lower airway diseases such as asthma or other obstructive pulmonary diseases, primarily chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD). Paradoxically, patients with severe airflow obstruction may not have audible wheezing, the so-called trapped lung syndrome. Approach to patient, diagnosis, and treatment differ depending on the underlying disease and the obstruction/spasm site. The most common diseases that lead to bronchospasm are asthma and COPD. Wheezing is also very common in heart failure (elderly patients without a history of asthma present with other symptoms and signs of heart failure). Other conditions that can manifest by wheezing and mimic asthma are presented in Table 1.

If patients with acute asthma attacks have SpO2 &lt; 92%, it is necessary to perform arterial blood gas analysis due to the risk of hypercapnia. Specifically, in acute asthma attacks, ventilation is stimulated, usually resulting in a decrease in the partial pressure of carbon dioxide (PaCO2) in arterial blood. Therefore, normal, or slightly elevated PaCO2 (e.g., > 42 mmHg) indicates extreme airway obstruction and fatigue. The aim of the oxygen therapy is to titrate the oxygen flow and concentration to target SpO2 of 94-98%. THERAPY:

1. Salbutamol (5 mg in a nebuliser) is the standard of asthma therapy (in acute exacerbations). The dose can be repeated every 15-20 minutes. The oxygen/air flow on the nebuliser must be at least 6 L/min. If the patient does not breathe sufficiently, the nebulised drug will not be delivered adequately.
2. Nebulised anticholinergics (ipratropium bromide 0.5 mg) can produce an additional bronchodilating effect.
3. The use of systemic corticosteroids is also useful.

### MAGNESIUM

In severe asthma, intravenous administration of magnesium (2 g in slow infusion) may also be useful. The side effects are nausea, fatigue, headache, and hypotension.

### AMINOPHYLLINE

There is no evidence of benefit from aminophylline compared with standard therapy. However, if it is decided to give it (in the presence of an experienced specialist), administer it at a dose of 5 mg/kg for 20-30 min, then with an infusion at a dose of 500-700 mcg/kg/h.

### FLUID THERAPY

It is used in patients who are dehydrated or have hypovolaemia. Caution: beta-2 agonists and corticosteroids may cause hypokalaemia.

## CHRONIC OBSTRUCTIVE PULMONARY DISEASE

Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) is characterised by persistent airflow disturbances that are progressive and associated with an impaired inflammatory response to numerous harmful particles in the inhaled air. COPD has two main forms: chronic bronchitis and emphysema, which, however, often occur together, so the traditional classification is of limited clinical significance. ### CHRONIC THERAPY

1. Pharmacotherapy: does not affect the progression of the disease, but leads to a decrease in symptoms, reduces the frequency of exacerbations, and improves the quality of life.
- **a)** Inhaled long-acting 42-agonists and inhaled corticosteroids.
- **b)** Theophylline: adjunctive therapy, inhibits phosphodiesterase and has an anti-inflammatory effect. Reserved for a limited number of cases.
- **c)** Mobilisation of secretions: stimulated by abundant oral fluid intake and humidification of the room. Limit the use of antihistamines, antitussives, mucolytics, and decongestants. Expectorants are of no clear benefit.
2. Smoking cessation: the only intervention that can reduce loss of lung function and mortality from respiratory causes.
3. Pulmonary rehabilitation: can improve exercise capacity and quality of life and is recommended for patients with moderate to severe COPD.
4. Vaccinations against pneumococcus and influenza are key to combating acute infections.
5. Oxygen (home oxygen therapy): long-term oxygen therapy reduces COPD mortality, and the goal is to increase the basic arterial oxygen partial pressure (PaO2) to ) 8 kPa or arterial oxygen saturation (SaO2) to ) 90% at rest.

### !. ACUTE EXACERBATIONS OF COPD

Acute exacerbations of COPD are characterised by worsening respiratory symptoms beyond normal daily variations and are usually caused by infection (over 75%) or tracheobronchial tree irritation (cold weather, hypoxia, narcotics, sedatives). Acute exacerbations of COPD are primarily due to mismatches of ventilation and perfusion rather than the limitation of expiratory airflow seen in acute asthma attacks. The clinical presentation is dominated by hypoxaemia (tachypnea, tachycardia, systemic hypertension, cyanosis, and changes in mental status) with CO2 retention and the development of respiratory acidosis. Diseases such as pneumonia, pneumothorax, pulmonary embolism, congestive heart failure (CHF), or acute abdomen can cause exacerbation of COPD.

In the early stages, arterial blood analysis reveals mild to moderate hypoxaemia without hypercapnia. As COPD progresses, hypoxaemia becomes more severe, and hypercapnia develops. ### 2. THERAPY FOR ACUTE EXACERBATIONS

The objectives of treatment are reversible bronchospasm relief, tissue oxygenation correction, and treatment of the underlying cause of exacerbation.

1. Oxygen +/- mechanical ventilation: the goal is to correct PaO2 > 8.0 kPa or SaO2 88-92% with CAUTION and monitoring of PaCO2 so that hypercapnia does not occur. Oxygen therapy increases the ventilation-perfusion mismatch (hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction) and leads to reduced haemoglobin affinity for CO2 (Haldane effect). Choosing aids for oxygen delivery and avoiding too high oxygen content are actions crucial for preventing the development of respiratory acidosis. If adequate oxygenation is not achieved and/or respiratory acidosis develops, assisted ventilation, primarily non-invasive, is required. In the case of inadequate response and/or further deterioration, invasive mechanical ventilation is required. Non-invasive ventilation provides a better outcome in terms of lower intubation rates, lower mortality rates, and shorter duration of hospitalisation (Table 4 and Table 5).
2. Pharmacotherapy: short-acting 42-agonists and anticholinergics are the first-line treatment for acute severe COPD exacerbation.
3. Corticosteroids: systemic administration for 5-7 days leads to improvement in lung function and hypoxaemia.
4. Antibiotics: administered if infection is suspected (change in volume and appearance of sputum). The most common causes are S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae, and M. catarrhalis. Choice of antibiotics: macrolides, tetracyclines, or amoxicillin with/without clavulanic acid.
