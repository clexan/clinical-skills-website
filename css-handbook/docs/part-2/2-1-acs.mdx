## INTRODUCTION

Acute coronary syndrome (ACS) is a clinical condition caused by sudden, critical myocardial ischaemia. It most often occurs due to atherosclerotic plaque rupture or erosion and the consequent clot formation in the coronary artery, leading to partial or complete luminal obstruction. This results in acute derangement in myocardial perfusion, which, depending on the severity and duration, leads to cardiomyocyte necrosis and myocardial infarction. The ACS is one of the leading causes of death in developed countries and is considered one of the greatest emergencies in modern cardiology and medicine in general. Therefore, it is crucial to promptly recognise a patient with ACS and start treatment as early as possible to reduce the risk of complications and mortality.

## DIAGNOSIS

Since this clinical condition is assigned the highest level of emergency, making the diagnosis as early as possible is crucial. It is essential to recognise the clinical signs and analyse the ECG, and then make a working diagnosis of the acute coronary syndrome without waiting for laboratory results to confirm or exclude cardiomyocyte injury.

### CLASSIFICATION OF ACUTE CORONARY SYNDROMES

Based on the initial ECG findings, ACS is classified into two forms:

1. ST-segment elevation ACS (STE-ACS) (> 20 minutes) (Figure 1)

Non-ST-segment elevation ACS (NSTE-ACS) - characterised by signs of myocardial ischaemia in the form of ST-segment depression, transient ST-segment elevation, and negative T waves, but the ECG findings may be normal (Figure 2 and Figure 3). This classification correlates with the pathophysiology of a critical coronary lesion and, at the same time, determines the treatment method. STE-ACS is usually caused by complete atherothrombotic occlusion of one of the coronary arteries and results in ST-segment-elevation myocardial infarction (STEMI). The aim with such patients is to enable reperfusion as soon as possible, i.e., to re-establish flow in the endangered myocardium area using percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) or, very rarely nowadays, using fibrinolytic drugs. On the other side, NSTE-ACS is usually caused by partial or intermittent coronary artery occlusion, which in some patients also results

in non-ST-segment-elevation myocardial infarction (NSTEMI), while in others, cardiomyocyte necrosis is absent (normal cardiac troponin levels), which is called unstable angina pectoris. In a small number of patients, NSTE-ACS can manifest as persistent myocardial ischaemia and consequent recurrent or refractory chest pain, acute heart failure, haemodynamic instability, or malignant ventricular arrhythmias. Accordingly, the approach to the treatment of NSTE-ACS depends on the estimated risk of adverse events. In certain patients, as well as in STEMI patients, emergency invasive approach and revascularisation are necessary, while in others, it is possible to start with drug therapy.

## MANAGEMENT OF ACUTE CORONARY SYNDROME

Despite the aforementioned classification, ACS is actually a spectrum of clinical conditions with a common aetiology and pathogenesis and, therefore, entails numerous overlaps in approach and treatment, especially in the early prehospital phase or emergency hospital admission.

## Initial assessment and managment of a patient with acute coronary syndrome

### CLINICAL PRESENTATION

In most patients, ACS is manifested by the sudden onset of chest discomfort that lasts more than 20 minutes. It usually occurs in the space behind the sternum as a burning, discomfort, pressure, or dull pain. Recurrent, shorter episodes of chest pain are also possible. The pain can spread to the neck, arms (most often to the left shoulder and arm), lower jaw, or back. In the case of inferior wall myocardial infarction, epigastric pain can occur with or without spreading to the retrosternal area. The pain often occurs at rest, without being provoked, typically early in the morning, and it usually awakens the patient from sleep. The onset of symptoms can also be triggered by physical exertion or emotional stress. When ischaemia affects a large part of the myocardium, it can lead to acute heart failure, the presentation of which can vary from mild signs of pulmonary congestion to acute pulmonary oedema or cardiogenic shock. Due to the increased electrical excitability of the ischaemic myocardium, various rhythm disorders are possible, from paroxysmal atrial fibrillation, ventricular extrasystoles, and short-term episodes of unstable ventricular tachycardia to persistent ventricular tachycardia accompanied by haemodynamic instability or ventricular fibrillation and consequent cardiorespiratory arrest. Conduction disorders at different levels are also possible; for example, varying degrees of AV block due to injury to the conduction system or accentuated vagal tone, usually associated with an inferior myocardial infarction. In some patients, like the elderly or people with long-standing diabetes, the clinical presentation of ACS can be atypical in the sense of undefined chest discomfort, shortness of breath, nausea, or generalised weakness. After the targeted medical history, a 12-lead ECG should be recorded, if possible, within 10 minutes of the first contact.

### TREATMENT OF ACUTE CORONARY SYNDROME

Suppression of pain and dyspnoea Reducing pain is one of the important initial steps in treating ACS patients since pain is associated with activation of the sympathetic nervous system, consequent vasoconstriction and tachycardia, and thus additional oxygen needs in an already ischaemic myocardium. A dose of 0.4 mg sublingual nitroglycerin is most often administered, which can be repeated up to three times at intervals of 5-10 minutes. Nitrates should be avoided in patients with systolic blood pressure below 90 mmHg and in suspected right ventricular infarction. In the case of persistent pain, it should be considered to administer intravenous morphine in single doses of 2-4 mg every 5 minutes until the pain stops, i.e., until reaching the highest dose of 20 mg. For preventing nausea and vomiting, antiemetic drugs should always be used with opioids. Morphine should be administered slowly by intravenous injection to prevent respiratory depression, hypotension, or bradycardia. Besides acting as an analgaesic, morphine also has a desirable haemodynamic effect due to vasodilation of the venous and arterial system, thus reducing the myocardial oxygen demand. In hypoxic patients, oxygen should be administered in case the Sa0' is below 90%, while routine oxygen administration is not recommended due to the possible harmful effect of hyperoxia on the injured myocardium. In the case of signs and symptoms of pulmonary congestion, the patient should be given an intravenous diuretic (furosemide 20-40 mg or the dose the patient takes at home), and depending on the severity of the accompanying respiratory insufficiency, non-invasive or invasive ventilation may be indicated.

### TREATMENT OF STE-ACS

In STE-ACS patients in whom symptoms appeared within 12 hours, urgent coronary angiography and primary PCI are indicated to mechanically open the occluded coronary artery as early as possible. Therefore, when diagnosing STE-ACS on-site or in health institutions

that do not have a PCI centre, the patient should urgently be transported to a centre with a primary PCI program. Since the activation of platelets and the coagulation cascade play a key role in the pathophysiology of ACS, it is essential to give dual antiplatelet therapy as early as possible, i.e., a combination of acetylsalicylic acid and a potent P2Y12 receptor inhibitor in a loading dose of:

- Oral acetylsalicylic acid 300 mg.
- Oral P2Y12 inhibitor - ticagrelor 180 mg or prasugrel 60 mg (or clopidogrel 600 mg). Compared to clopidogrel, ticagrelor and prasugrel lead to faster, more reliable, and stronger inhibition of platelet aggregation (onset of action 30 min vs. 2-6 h for clopidogrel), which is why clopidogrel is used only in case the two abovementioned drugs are not available or are contraindicated.

### PERIPROCEDURAL AND POSTPROCEDURAL TREATMENT OF STE-ACS

During coronary angiography and PCI, patients also receive an anticoagulant drug, i.e., unfractionated heparin (70-100 U/kg) or, less often, low-molecular-weight heparin (enoxaparin 0.5 mg/kg IV). If there are no other indications, further use of anticoagulant therapy after PCI is not indicated except for thromboprophylaxis (prevention of deep-vein thrombosis and pulmonary thromboembolism). Intravenous administration of GPIIb/IIIa inhibitors, which belong to the group of antiplatelet drugs, should be considered in patients with angiographically detected extensive coronary artery thrombosis or in patients without a no-reflow or slow-flow phenomenon. After PCI, patients continue with dual antiplatelet therapy in a maintenance dose for the next 12 months:

- Acetylsalicylic acid 75-100 mg
- Ticagrelor 2x90 mg or prasugrel 10 mg (reduced dose of 5 mg for BW &lt; 60 kg or age > 75) or clopidogrel 75 mg At the end of the mentioned period, the P2Y12 inhibitor is discontinued, and the patient continues to take acetylsalicylic acid permanently. Depending on the individually assessed risk of bleeding and risk of ischaemic events, the period of dual therapy can be shortened or extended in certain patients.

## Initial assessment and managment of a patient with acute coronary syndrome

Along with a targeted medical history and 12-lead ECG, the approach to each patient with a working diagnosis of acute coronary syndrome emphasises the assessment and treatment of cardiac and circulatory disorders (C from circulation in ABCDE) and must include:

- Setting up venous access,
- Measuring vital parameters (blood pressure, heart rate, Sp0' levels),
- Short physical examination focused on the cardiovascular and respiratory systems,
- Continuous ECG monitoring with an available defibrillator
- Taking blood samples for laboratory analysis (in emergency hospital admission),
- Suppression of pain and dyspnoea,
- Application of antiplatelet therapy.

### TREATMENT OF NSTE-ACS

As previously mentioned, there are different categories of NSTEACS patients. The correct choice of treatment strategy is based on an early assessment of the risk of ischaemia and bleeding in each patient. NSTE-ACS patients are divided into three categories according to the risk of ischaemia, which also determines the degree of urgency and time of invasive cardiac assessment (Table 1):

1. Very high risk - emergency coronary angiography (&lt; 2 hours from admission),
2. High risk - early invasive assessment (&lt; 24 hours from admission),
3. Low risk - selective invasive assessment (only in patients in whom non-invasive assessment shows signs of inducible ischaemia). In very high-risk patients, the treatment strategy is the same as in STEMI patients. Before referring to urgent invasive assessment, we should try to suppress the patient's pain and administer acetylsalicylic acid 300 mg as early as possible. In NSTE-ACS, it is no longer recommended to routinely administer P2Y12 inhibitors before coronary angiography and insight into the anatomy of the coronary arteries. Studies have shown that pretreatment with P2Y12 inhibitors does not improve outcomes compared with their use during PCI and can significantly endanger the patient and increase the risk of bleeding in the case of indication for urgent surgical revascularisation due to multivessel coronary artery disease. In other NSTE-ACS patients, initial drug therapy includes anti-thrombotic and anti-ischaemic therapy. Antithrombotic therapy includes:
- Acetylsalicylic acid 300 mg initially, then in a maintenance dose of 100 mg.
- Anticoagulant: fondaparinux 2.5 mg SC or enoxaparin 2 6 1 mg/kg. Beta-blockers are indicated for reducing myocardial oxygen demand (if the patient is haemodynamically stable and without signs of acute heart failure). In persistent pain, we should also administer nitrates sublingually or intravenously. Most NSTE-ACS patients undergo invasive assessment in the aforementioned time intervals, except for patients who do not have high or very high-risk criteria, with normal troponin levels, in whom non-invasive testing excluded inducible ischaemia.

### PERIPROCEDURAL AND POSTPROCEDURAL TREATMENT OF NSTE-ACS

A patient with an indication for PCI after a coronary angiography should receive a potent P2Y12 inhibitor in a loading dose - prasugrel 60 mg or ticagrelor 180 mg and additionally a bolus of unfractionated heparin (70-100 U/kg) or enoxaparin (0.5 mg/kg BW) if the patient has previously received it. If there is no other indication, further use of anticoagulant therapy after PCI is not indicated even in NSTE-ACS patients, except for thromboprophylaxis. Recommendations regarding dosage and duration of dual antiplatelet therapy in NSTE-ACS patients are the same as in STE-ACS patients.
