## INTRAVASCULAR ACCESS

Adequate intravascular access is one of the main preconditions of adequate care for acutely ill patients. It ensures fast and direct administration of medication and fluid therapy, including blood products and other intravenous fluids. Intravascular access can be established via a peripheral vein, a central vein, or intraosseous access. In practice, one or more peripheral venous accesses are most often placed. In cases when that is not possible, alternatives for peripheral intravascular access include intraosseous access (in out-of-hospital conditions) and central venous access (in hospital conditions). This chapter focuses on techniques for establishing intravenous (IV) and intraosseous (IO) access, competencies that every medical doctor should have after graduation. As with every other skill, it is important to point out that the technique requires practice and experience. Intravenous access is established by placing a cannula in the peripheral vein. There are several sizes of cannulas available, each size having its specific colour. The flow through the cannula depends on its size, i.e., diameter, which is measured in Gauges (G) and written on the cannula packaging. The larger the Gauge, the smaller the diameter of the catheter and the slower the infusion flow. In an adult acutely ill patient, the standard is to place at least two large-bore IV cannulas with a diameter greater than 18 G, colloquially "green, white, or grey."

## INTRAOSSEOUS ACCESS

Furthermore, the procedure can be difficult in certain groups of patients, such as children (due to subcutaneous fatty tissue that makes it difficult to palpate the vein

If possible, the procedure needs to be explained in detail to the patient before the beginning. Compliance and, therefore, the success of the procedure is higher in that case. It is also necessary to check the equipment with which the procedure is performed (for example, whether the infusion system is completely filled with infusion fluid). The procedure begins with palpation of the desired venepuncture site; standard positions are the branches of the dorsal venous arch of the basilic vein, the basilic vein located cubically, the beginning of the cephalic vein located on the hand, and the cephalic vein in the upper arm. There are facilitating manoeuvres that can be used: tying the limb cranially to the venepuncture site (tourniquet), instructing the patient to lower their hand below the level of the heart and squeeze the hand into a fist several times, rubbing with an alcohol antiseptic solution, after which the vein is more clearly visible, and choosing a vein that has a straight course in the length of several centimetres. It is often more important to palpate the vein well rather than to see it well. Placing an IV cannula is performed in several steps: the skin 3 to 4 cm distal to the venepuncture site is stretched (especially in elderly patients, in whom placement may be significantly more difficult due to reduced skin turgor) and then disinfected. The patient is warned to expect a puncture. The vein is punctured at an angle from 10° and 30° from the surface of the patient's skin. After obtaining blood return in the flashback chamber, the cannula is placed another millimetre into the lumen of the vein and then laid horizontally next to the patient's skin. The tourniquet is released, and the cannula should then be "swiped" over the needle into the vein while simultaneously pulling the needle out. The distal part of the cannula is then connected to the infusion system, and flow is started. The patient may feel a sensation of coldness, but pain proximal to the site of the catheter placement, as well as swelling of the surrounding tissue may indicate a blown vein due to improper needle insertion and/or a fragile vein with an escape of the infusion paravascularly. In that case, the IV cannula should be removed and the procedure repeated on another, more proximally located vein. Once the cannula has been successfully placed, secure its position on the skin using the stickers.

In children, there are some special considerations when choosing a place of insertion. One should take into account the epiphyseal cartilage and bone growth zones. The distal femur can be considered an alternative to the previously mentioned sites. Contraindications for intraosseous access placement are bone fracture, osteomyelitis, infection of the skin and soft tissues over the bone, the patient with an artificial joint or bone in that place, intraosseous access already placed on that bone in the last 48 hours, and the inability to determine typical palpation sites. The equipment consists of the following: a drill or a gun with a single shot on which the needle is (magnetically) inserted, a system for connecting to a classic set for infusion solutions, and a cuff for applying the infusion solution under pressure. Once the correct position for insertion is selected and aseptic preparation of the skin at the site of the planned insertion is made, the drill with the attached needle is oriented perpendicularly to the patient's bone. Piercing the skin and subcutaneous tissue is done with a needle, without drilling, and after contact with the periosteum, the drilling can start. The goal is to pass through the compact part of the bone and reach the spongious tissue of bone, which can be felt as a loss of resistance. Once the cancellous bone is reached, the drilling should be stopped. The stylet is removed from the catheter, and the remaining part is fixed to the skin. Aspiration of the content (bone marrow) confirms the adequate position. Prior to fluid administration, breakage of the fibrin mesh in the spongious part of the bone should be done, usually with the application of a fluid bolus (volume of 5-10 mL). The procedure itself is painful, therefore, if the patient reacts to pain, it is necessary to apply 2-4 mL of 2% lidocaine before the bolus to anesthetise the septal area. According to the guidelines for the management of acutely ill patients, two failed attempts to establish intravenous access are an indication for the establishment of intraosseous access. There are typical places for establishing intraosseous access in adults:

- Proximal part of the humerus: the patient's arm is adjusted in adduction and internal rotation, and the hand is fixed under the lower back. The greater tubercle and the surgical neck of the humerus are palpated. The ideal insertion site is on the greater tubercle, 1 cm above the surgical neck of the humerus.
- Proximal part of the tibia: the patient's leg is placed in extension. The ideal insertion point is 2 cm distal and 2 cm medial from the tibial tuberosity, on the bone surface.
- Distal part of the tibia: the ideal place of insertion is 3 cm proximal to the most prominent part of the medial malleolus. Palpation of the anterior and posterior borders of the tibia is necessary in order to perform the insertion on a flat part of the bone.

KEY POINTS:

- Two failed attempts of intravenous access are an indication for intraosseous access in acutely ill patients.
- Typical placement positions: proximal humerus, proximal tibia, distal tibia.
