The advanced life support (ALS) algorithm is a standardised approach for the management of patients in cardiac arrest. It is extremely useful because it allows starting the treatment without losing time for discussion, thus increasing the team's efficiency in resuscitation. Heart rhythms associated with cardiac arrest can be divided into two groups:

1. shockable rhythms, which include pulseless ventricular tachycardia (pVT) and ventricular fibrillation (VF) and
2. non-shockable rhythms, which include pulseless electrical activity (PEA) and asystole (Figure 1). The main difference in the management of the two mentioned groups is that patients with pVT/VF need to be defibrillated as early as possible, i.e., receive an electrical shock to the heart. Other cardiopulmonary resuscitation (CPR) actions, such as heart compressions, airway management and ventilation, intravenous/intraosseal access, administration of adrenaline, and identification and management of reversible causes, are common to both groups. Other additional procedures may be indicated if cardiac arrest is caused by special circumstances. It is essential to note that procedures that improve survival after cardiac arrest include prompt and effective bystander CPR, high-quality chest compressions with minimal interruptions and early defibrillation.

## MANAGEMENT OF SHOCKABLE RHYTHMS (PVT, VF)

6. Confirm cardiac arrest by simultaneously checking the pulse and breathing for 10 seconds. Call the resuscitation team. Initiate chest compressions 30:2 while simultaneously placing self-adhesive defibrillation/monitoring pads - one below the right clavicle and the other at the V6 position in the midaxillary line. Stop chest compressions to avoid artefacts on the monitor. Confirm VF/pVT on ECG. Immediately continue chest compressions. At the same time, the person on the defibrillator should choose the appropriate energy (150-200 J biphasic for the first defibrillation and 150-360 J biphasic for the following defibrillations) and press the charge button. While the defibrillator is charging, warn all present team members, except the one performing CPR, to stand back and move the oxygen source at least one meter away from the defibrillator.

## TREATMENT OF NON2SHOCKABLE RHYTHMS

### (PEA, ASYSTOLE)

6. Begin CPR 30:2. As soon as the IV/IO access is established, give adrenaline 1 mg IV/IO. Continue CPR 30:2 until the airway is secured (then continue with chest compressions without stopping during ventilation) for 2 minutes. After 2 minutes, check the rhythm: If organised electrical activity is shown, the pulse and/or signs of life should be checked.
- **a)** If there is a pulse and/or signs of life, start post-resuscitation care. b) If there is no palpable pulse and/or no signs of life (PEA pulseless electrical activity), continue CPR. While performing CPR, think about reversible causes of death (4H and 4T). After 2 minutes, check the rhythm and continue depending on the assessed rhythm (in the case of persistent cardiac arrest, once given, repeat adrenaline 1 mg IV every 3-5 minutes - every other 2-minute cycle of CPR):
- If the monitor shows VF/pVT, follow the other side of the algorithm that includes defibrillation.
- If asystole is shown on the monitor, continue CPR.
8. When the defibrillator is charged, tell the person performing the massage to "step back". When the person moves away, deliver the shock. Immediately after shock delivery, proceed with CPR (30:2) without checking the rhythm or pulse, starting with chest compressions. Continue CPR for 2 minutes and secure the airway with an advanced device during that time. After 2 minutes, stop briefly and check the monitor. If the rhythm is VF/pVT, repeat steps 6-10 and deliver a second shock. Continue CPR for 2 minutes. If the airway is secured with an advanced device (e.g., laryngeal mask or endotracheal tube), perform CPR continuously without a break for ventilation with chest compressions at the rate of 100-120/min and respiratory rate of 10/min. Think about reversible causes of death (4H and 4T). If a reversible cause is detected, begin correcting it. If VF/pVT persists, repeat steps 6-8 and deliver a third shock. Immediately resume CPR and give adrenaline 1 mg IV/IO and amiodarone 300 mg IV/IO while performing CPR for the next 2 minutes. If VF/pVT persists, repeat the 2-minute sequence of CPR

During the treatment of cardiac arrest, you should keep in mind the possible reversible causes that led to it, which are divided into two groups (4H and 4T) to make them easier to remember (Table 1). Hypoxia: reduce the risk of hypoxia by oxygenating with 100% oxygen, adequate ventilation, and securing the airway by placing an i-gel or an endotracheal tube. Hypovolaemia: pulseless electrical activity is often caused by hypovolaemia, which can result from, for example, bleeding or sepsis. The intravascular volume should be replaced immediately with intravenous fluids and blood through a wide-lumen peripheral venous cannula (min. green IV cannula, diameter 18 G and wider). Hyperkalaemia, hypokalaemia, hypoglycaemia, hypocalcaemia, and other metabolic disorders can be detected by laboratory tests or from medical history (e.g., kidney failure). Each of these conditions requires specific therapy. Hypothermia: hypothermia should be suspected in all drowning victims and patients found outdoors. When measuring temperature, use a thermometer with a low-temperature reading option. Such patients should be warmed by passive (blankets, aluminium foil) and active methods (heating the air, giving warm IV fluids, rinsing cavities with warm fluids). Tension pneumothorax: may be the primary cause of pulseless electrical activity. The most important causes of tension pneumothorax are trauma, barotrauma in patients on positive pressure ventilation, and asthma. The treatment is rapid thoracostomy or needle thoracocentesis. Tamponade: can be diagnosed by placing an ultrasound probe below the xiphoid. If tamponade is the cause of cardiac arrest, it is necessary to perform an emergency thoracotomy or pericardiocentesis as soon as possible. Drug poisoning: can be detected later by laboratory tests. If available, use appropriate antidotes. The most common cause of thromboembolic or mechanical obstruction is massive pulmonary embolism (PE) or coronary artery thrombosis. If it is suspected that the cardiac arrest is caused by a pulmonary embolism, consider immediate administration of thrombolytics. Furthermore, the suspicion of a thromboembolic incident of the coronary arteries can be established from the patient's history (chest pain) or by pre-arrest 12-lead ECG (with documented ischaemic changes).
