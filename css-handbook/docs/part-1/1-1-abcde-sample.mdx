title: "1.1 Initial assessment of acutely ill patients (ABCDE and SAMPLE)"
description: "Structured initial assessment of acutely ill patients using the ABCDE and SAMPLE approaches."
---

An acutely ill patient is one with an immediate threat of deterioration of vital functions, such as the cardiovascular, respiratory, and neurological systems, with a risk for subsequent cardiac arrest. This can result from their initial illness or a combination of chronic comorbidities and an acute state. Handling these patients requires standardised, systematic, and simple initial assessment and management. Simplified and focused examinations bring enough information about the patient's current status and need for therapeutic interventions. The accidental or deliberate omission of one or more vital parameters may delay recognition of the problem and potentially further worsen the patient's condition. With a structured examination, in most situations, we obtain enough information to form a differential diagnosis and plan for the initial management of the acutely ill patient. One of the tools for standardised and systematic initial patient assessment is the ABCDE algorithm. It consists of five categories, where each letter represents one category during the assessment. The assessment is performed in alphabetical order. If a deviation/problem is encountered during the inspection, immediate therapeutic intervention is performed. In other words, if there is a problem on "B", the recommendation is to solve that problem, stabilise it if possible, and only then move on to "C". Lifethreatening conditions in patients are most often manifested through changes in cardiocirculatory, respiratory and neurological systems; therefore, they have the most important place in the rapid assessment.

"A" stands for airway and evaluates problems with airway obstruction. Obstruction can be partial or complete and caused by various reasons such as decreased tone of soft tissues in the upper airway, food, blood, gastric content, oedema of the soft tissues of the tongue, pharynx, and larynx, laryngospasm, bronchospasm, and others. Complete airway obstruction can quickly cause cardiac arrest. Sometimes after complete airway obstruction, a see-saw phenomenon can be seen in the form of paradoxical inverse movement of the chest cavity and abdomen. If the respiratory drive is still present, the diaphragm and intercostal muscles contract and prepare for air to enter. If there is no inspired air, a reverse movement of the chest and abdomen can be seen (instead of the regular simultaneous abdomen and chest movement). Partial obstruction is a common problem in clinical practice. In that case, sound phenomena can usually be heard as snoring, gargling, and inspiratory stridor. Depending on which of these are detected, one can estimate an approximate location of obstruction (upper or lower respiratory tract). A common problem is partial airway obstruction caused by acute deterioration in the level of consciousness. It is expected that patients with a Glasgow Coma Score (GCS) of 8 or fewer points don't have protective reflexes, so protection of the airway against gastric reflux and regurgitation of content with endotracheal tube is required in that case!

### HOW TO KEEP THE AIRWAY OPEN,

Opening the airway is one of the key points of patient treatment whatsoever. If airway obstruction is visible, it is necessary to act immediately. One of the basic approaches is the head-tilt chin-lift manoeuvre in its original form, which can be supplemented with adjutant manoeuvres. The upper hand is put on the patient's forehead, and the lower hand pushes the bony part of the mandible until resistance is felt. If a fracture of the cervical vertebrae is suspected (for example, fall from height, traffic accident, or jump into a shallow pool or beach), the manoeuvre should be modified. Instead of head tilt, fixation of the skull is made with pressure on zygomatic arches on both sides, followed by regular chin lift. If there is suspicion of spinal fracture combined with visible trauma of the facial bones, the head is fixated in the area of temporal bone bilaterally with regular chin lift movement. Once the airway is opened, it should be kept that way, which is achieved with oro- or nasopharyngeal airway or supraglottic (SGA) or infraglottic devices. Usually, we start with the lowest level device (e.g., nasopharyngeal airway) and escalate according to the patient's state. Commonly used are the oropharyngeal airway and nasopharyngeal airway. For the airway tube to function appropriately, the right size should be placed in the patient's oral cavity. The appropriate size is estimated by placing it in a position in which it stands in the oral cavity, placing the lower edge at the mandibular angle. Tube of proper size should have the upper edge at the level of incisors. If the proper size is unavailable, a bigger size is more appropriate. As with every other tube, there can occur practical problems. Firstly, the tube is near the oropharyngeal aperture and base of the tongue, which are areas with significant vagal innervation. This can provoke a vomiting reflex if one is still present in the patient (in that case, the airway must be removed immediately). Secondly, the trauma of the oral cavity and trismus could make insertion difficult, maybe even impossible. The nasopharyngeal airway is inserted into the pharynx through the nasal cavity. It is cut diagonally on its lower edge, and a shorter margin should be placed medially when inserting. Rotating movements are done by prospering through the lower nasal cavity perpendicularly to the patient's head. The appropriate size of the airway is estimated by placing the lower edge on the earlobe, and the upper edge should be at the nostril opening. Some of the commercially available nasopharyngeal tubes come with adjustable flange to mark and secure the airway in optimal depth (for a particular patient) after the insertion. The nasopharyngeal airway is better tolerated than the oropharyngeal in patients that still have protective reflexes (pharyngeal, laryngeal, cough reflex). There can be difficulties placing the airway if there is a nose fracture present. On top of that, if there is a suspected skull base fracture (Brillen hematoma, otorrhea, rhinorrhoea, mastoid hematoma), it is contraindicated to place nasopharyngeal airway because potentially fractured lamina cribrosa of ethmoidal bone can be additionally damaged by placing the device. One of the essential supraglottic devices is a laryngeal mask (LMA). The most commonly used version is i-gel, which got its value by having thermoplastic elastomers on its lower edge that ensure better placement on the laryngeal aditus. This provides a better seal and less probability of aspiration of gastric content into the lungs. Insertion is made by holding LMA in the hand like a pencil, with the progression of the mask through the oral cavity. It is useful to push the soft tissues of the tongue aside to place it more easily. Its upper edge has a connector that enables bag valve mask placement and assists ventilation of the patient if needed. ALS algorithm for cardiac arrest states that LMA is an adequate airway protection device for providers who are not highly skilled in intubation. However, research shows that, although it does provide some protection, it does not completely eliminate the risk of aspiration of gastric content in the case of regurgitation. The second category is "B", which stands for the breathing parameters. Firstly, the respiratory rate is assessed. It is measured by observing breathing mechanics in parallel with placing a hand on the lower part of the patient's chest for 30 seconds. Normal respiratory rate is between 10 and 20 breaths per minute (eupnoea). Bradypnoeic patient (&lt; 10/min) has, despite the appropriate tidal volume, inappropriate exchange of oxygen and carbon dioxide in the lungs, so the patient needs assisted ventilation (for example, if the patient breaths 6/min, 6 additional ones should be added to have appropriate frequency and, therefore, adequate minute volume). An apnoeic patient requires constant assisted ventilation. Tachypnoeic patient (> 20/min) has significantly higher energy expenditure on work of breathing, which eventually leads to muscle fatigue and use of accessory respiratory muscles (intercostal muscles, muscles of the neck). Normal breathing mechanics also implies a symmetric movement of both sides of the thorax synchronously with the abdomen. In the case of asymmetric movement, further examination should be performed to distinguish potential pathology. Auscultation of the lungs can bring a lot of information that can help in the clinical examination: inspiratory stridor can point to a problem in the upper respiratory tract, and inspiratory and expiratory crackles can point to a problem in the lower respiratory tract. If lung auscultation shows abnormalities, percussion of the thorax can distinguish whether air is trapped in the thorax (pneumothorax - hyperresonance) or there is effusion (dull, flat sound). Lung ultrasound and radiogram are additional points in examination that can help in further diagnosis and are commonly used in these cases. In the case of tension pneumothorax (PTX), urgent decompression of the compressed lung is necessary. Furthermore, pulse oximetry is a vital parameter in ABCDE assessment. It is a non-invasive measurement of a patient's peripheral blood oxygen saturation. In most patients, normal saturation is between 94 and 98%, while 88-92% is tolerated in patients with chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD). If there is a lack of oxygen, it can be compensated with a nasal cannula, simple face mask, or face mask with a reservoir. The mixture of air that is normally inhaled (fraction of inspired oxygen - FiO') contains 21% of oxygen. The fraction of inspired oxygen can be enriched through nasal cannulas and masks. - assisted ventilation "C" stands for circulation. Cardiovascular disturbances are often a sign of general deterioration in a patient. There are several parameters included in the "C" assessment: characteristics of a patient's pulse, blood pressure, skin colour, capillary refill time, and diuresis. The individual parameter might not sometimes give much information, while the combined analysis of all the parameters and their trends often paints a good general picture of the patient's haemodynamic profile and risk for deterioration and cardiac arrest. Pulse is characterised by its rate, rhythm, and strength. Normal heart rate ranges from 60 to 100 beats per minute, while a rate below 60/min is called bradycardia, and above 100/min is called tachycardia. Bradycardic pulse can sometimes be found in young, healthy individuals without cardiac pathology. A typical example of a completely arrhythmic pulse can be found in patients with atrial fibrillation, a commonly found pathological rhythm in elderly patients. Heart rhythm and its pathology are explained in more detail in chapter 2.2. Pulse is regularly equally palpable centrally (e.g., carotid artery) and peripherally (radial artery). If the pulse strength is weaker on the radial artery, the estimated systolic blood pressure is less than 80 mmHg (hypotensive state). The focus in the assessment of skin is on its warmth and wetness. Normal skin is dry and warm, while in the case of haemodynamic disturbances, it is often clammy, sweaty, and cold. Pale skin can point to anaemia and/or centralisation of blood flow. Cyanotic skin can indicate hypoxia, and red and swollen skin can indicate anaphylaxis. Capillary refill time is a parameter that indicates the adequacy of peripheral blood flow; as the skin is the organ with the lowest level of autoregulation, it will also be the first organ from which the blood will be centralised in the case of reduced cardiac output, e.g., in hypovolaemia. Measurement is performed by applying pressure on the skin near bony tissue (typically sternum or forehead). If it takes longer than 2 seconds for a distal capillary bed to regain its colour, blood flow through the skin and other peripheral tissues is insufficient. The longer the capillary refill time, the stronger the centralisation (a typical example is an actively bleeding patient). Centralisation of blood flow is an adaptive response to haemodynamic disturbance. It consists of vasoconstriction in the "peripheral" organ system (skin, muscles) in order to preserve adequate blood flow through vital organs (brain, heart, lungs). Clinically, centralisation is seen in characteristic manifestations: pale and clammy skin, capillary refill

time (CRT) longer than 2 seconds, and tachycardia. Blood pressure measurement in these cases might show hypotension with systolic blood pressure below 90 mmHg. Besides clinical examination, there are several interventions that have to be performed during the assessment of the cardiovascular system. An acutely ill patient should be put on continuous electrocardiographic (ECG) monitoring. Basic monitoring includes at least three Einthoven leads: the red one is put on the upper right side of the thorax, the yellow one is put on the upper left part of the thorax, and the green electrode is put on the lower left part of the thorax. After the patient is put on ECG monitoring, the cardiac rhythm strip should be analysed. Depending on the patient's medical history and clinical examination, a 12-lead ECG should be recorded. Placing intravenous access is one of the basic interventions in acutely ill patients. Once IV access is placed, blood samples can be analysed, and intravenous medications can be administered. State of consciousness, pupillary response, and blood glucose levels are regularly monitored. Basic quantitative assessment of the state of consciousness is checked through the AVPU score. "A" stands for an alert patient, "V" represents a patient who reacts to verbal stimuli (corresponds to somnolence). The patient in the "P" category does not react to verbal stimuli but reacts to painful stimuli (corresponds to stupor). "U" stands for patient unresponsive to any stimuli, which corresponds to coma. AVPU roughly shows categories measured more thoroughly by the GCS, a scoring system that should be considered if the patient has a primary neurological disorder. Pupils are typically equally wide and respond to light with constriction bilaterally. Asymmetry of pupils can imply increased intracranial pressure. In acutely ill patients, bilaterally dilated pupils that don't respond to light stimuli imply global cerebral circulation disorder. ### MEDICAL HISTORY

In order to help with differential diagnosis and decide on the plan for initial management of the acutely ill patient, target medical history should be taken from the patient or their caregiver. A short medical history can be sampled through six basic categories that form the acronym SAMPLE (Table 1). Symptoms are very often connected to the circumstances that led to them. Multiple comorbidities of the patient and numerous chronic medications can direct the therapeutic approach and examination (e.g., due to polyneuropathy in acute coronary syndrome, people with diabetes do not necessarily have to have chest pain and usually present with non-specific symptoms, which leads to the conclusion that a 12-lead ECG must be recorded). The time passed since the last meal indicates stomach fullness with food, which impacts the strategy for potential endotracheal intubation. The stomach is considered empty 6 hours after the consumption of solid food, 4 hours after the consumption of liquid food, and 2 hours after the consumption of water. ### IN HOSPITAL CONDITIONS

Abnormal physiology is common in general wards, yet research shows that observation and measurement of vital parameters occur less than recommended. Assessment of simple vital signs may help with the prevention of cardiopulmonary arrest. One of the ways to increase the success of early identification of critical patients in the wards is to use the Early Warning Score (EWS) protocol. EWS enables repeated assessment of the patient's vital parameters in defined time intervals. The assessment is mainly carried out by a medical technician. If the patient scores 3-5, they are observed once every 4 hours. If the sum is greater than 6 or more, it is necessary to alert the ward doctor, who then assesses the need for more frequent observation (for example, once every hour). Patients with a score of 9 or more require continuous monitoring, which is most often carried out in intensive care units, emergency room observation rooms, and other appropriate places. The most important is the recognition of major changes for specific parameters in time intervals. If the patient's condition deteriorates, a decision is made on further treatment and the need to refer the critical patient to treatment services.
