*Authors: Electrolytes are electrically charged substances commonly found*

in blood and other bodily fluids. They are essential for various physiological processes, such as the maintenance of proper membrane potential and the generation and propagation of the action potential in nerve and muscle cells. Sodium, potassium, and chloride are the most significant among them, along with magnesium, calcium, and phosphates. Electrolyte disorders are most commonly classified according to the change in their (usual) plasma concentration and can have increased (hyper-) and decreased (hypo-) values. They can lead to potentially life-threatening conditions, so it is important to recognise and treat them as soon as possible.

## HYPOKALAEMIA AND HYPERKALAEMIA

Potassium is an electrolyte vital for maintaining normal body functions. Changes in its concentration can cause the sudden onset of potentially life-threatening conditions. Potassium is primarily an intracellular positively charged ion (cation). The sodium-potassium pump (Na-/K--ATPase, Na-/K- pump) found in the membrane of all cells is responsible for maintaining potassium homeostasis in the body. It maintains the normal resting membrane potential through the activity of the enzyme Na/K-ATPase, which transports three sodium ions out of the cell in exchange for two potassium ions, thus removing one positive charge carrier from inside the cell. ### HYPERKALAEMIA

Hyperkalaemia is defined as the elevation of potassium concentration above its upper limit, usually above 5.0 to 5.5 mmol/L. It can be classified as mild, moderate, or severe, depending on the plasma potassium concentration. Mild hyperkalaemia is defined as a potassium concentration of

5. 5 to 5.9 mmol/L. It doesn't usually cause any symptoms and is typically detected accidentally while performing routine laboratory tests. This condition can also be chronic, usually in patients with a certain degree of decreased kidney function, also often without a clinical manifestation. The need for treatment depends on the presence of symptoms and is usually done by stimulating the elimination of potassium through either oral or rectal suspensions, as well as modifying the diuretic therapy, if present. Moderate hyperkalaemia is defined as a potassium concentration of 6.0 to 6.4 mmol/L. Symptoms can be present, and more worrying, changes in heart rhythm can also occur. In severe hyperkalaemia (potassium concentration of 6.5 mmol/L or higher), these changes are almost always present, making this a potentially life-threatening condition requiring emergency treatment. These patients should be examined using the ABCDE approach and their history should be taken according to the SAMPLE acronym. It is of utmost importance to monitor these patients' vital signs using a standard 3-lead ECG monitor, regularly measure blood pressure, and continuously monitor SpO-. Serial ECG readings should be recorded to monitor changes in the ECG pattern or to gauge the effectiveness of therapy. Symptoms are non-specific and depend on the overall level of potassium. They vary from diarrhoea, nausea, vomiting, thirst, muscle

weakness, muscle pain or numbness, and heart palpitations with or without chest pain. The expected ECG changes include first-degree AV block, the loss of P waves, broadened QRS complex, ST denivelation, the fusion of S and T waves, formation of the so-called sine-wave pattern, and tall T waves (tenting T waves). Patients can present with bradycardia and ventricular tachycardia, and if left untreated, it can progress to cardiac arrest. Treatment of moderate or severe hyperkalaemia depends on the clinical presentation (see Figure 1). The three basic steps of treatment are to protect the heart muscle, stimulate the potassium flux into the cells, and remove excess potassium from the body. If ECG changes are present, start the treatment with the application of intravenous calcium in the form of calcium gluconate or calcium chloride in a slow IV infusion. This protects the myocardium from the deleterious effects of high potassium concentrations. However, this does not lower the actual potassium concertation. Therefore, treatment is continued by trying to move the excess potassium inside the cells by administering intravenous glucose solution with short-acting insulin and nebulised salbutamol. In cases of severe hyperkalaemia or in cases of moderate therapy-resistant hyperkalaemia with pronounced ECG changes and/or cardiac arrhythmias, it is important to seek immediate expert help from a nephrology specialist and consider emergency dialysis. Furthermore, it is important to recognise the cause of hyperkalaemia. HYPERKALAEMIA CAN BE CAUSED BY:

- acidaemia, i.e., lowering of serum pH - this leads to the removal of potassium from the cells in exchange for the influx of hydrogen ions
- diabetes - the insulin-regulated transport of glucose inside the cells is impaired (co-transport with potassium)
- diuretics - potassium-sparing diuretics: aldosterone-receptor antagonists: spironolactone - Aldactone, eplerenone - Inspra®, Diuron®), thiazide diuretics (indapamide, hydrochlorthiazide)
- chronic kidney failure or acute kidney injury
- various other medications - ACE inhibitors, beta-blockers, non-steroid anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs)
- tissue breakdown - rhabdomyolysis, tumour necrosis, haemolysis
- potassium-rich diet
- pseudohyperkalaemia - caused by haemolysis due to inadequate blood sampling or storage of blood samples.

Kidneys are responsible for the removal of roughly 90% of potassium, the remainder being secreted through the digestive tract and other bodily fluids like sweat and saliva. In kidneys, the reabsorption of potassium happens in the proximal convoluted tubule and the ascending limb of the loop of Henle, while its secretion happens in the distal convoluted tubule. Changes in serum pH, insulin, various medications (e.g., diuretics), as well as various hormones, can cause changes in extracellular potassium concentration, either hyperkalaemia or hypokalaemia. What makes potassium especially dangerous is its effect on myocardial cells, where it can cause the development of cardiac arrhythmias that can progress to malignant arrhythmias and cause cardiac arrest. Therefore, it is vital to recognise potassium disorders and their clinical presentation, even before laboratory tests are completed.

Hypokalaemia can usually be discerned from an adequately taken patient history even before laboratory results are in. It can manifest in the musculoskeletal system and, more dangerously, in the cardiovascular system. Symptoms can vary due to the patient's response to the lowering of potassium levels. Mild forms of hypokalaemia are characterised by the development of muscle cramps. Symptoms progress further with the development of paralytic ileus or nausea and vomiting, possible rhabdomyolysis, and cardiac arrhythmias. Patients can also present with various ECG changes like the lowering of T waves, ST depression, and prominent U waves (especially in precordial V4-C6 leads). The most common cardiac manifestation in practice is the sudden onset of atrial fibrillation. Treatment depends on the severity of the presentation. It is most often treated by administering oral potassium supplements like potassium citrate in a dilute aqueous solution (Kalinor®), which is usually effective within 1-2 hours. In cases of inability to administer potassium via the oral route or in severe cases, potassium can be supplemented via a peripheral vein. Application of intravenous potassium is limited to 20 mmol/h, with 10 mmol/h being the recommended dose. In very severe cases, potassium can be supplemented with 10 mmol/10 minutes and an additional 10 mmol/5-10 minutes, with mandatory ECG monitoring.

### HYPONATRAEMIA AND HYPERNATRAEMIA

Sodium is the main extracellular positively charged ion, i.e., cation. Its plasma concentration varies from 135 to 145 mmol/L and usually stays within this range despite daily variations in salt and fluid intake. Along with chloride and bicarbonate anions, it maintains roughly 90% of extracellular osmolarity. Sodium concentration is primarily dependent on the overall volume of extracellular fluid, i.e., fluid depletion or overload that results in hyper- or hyponatraemia. Mild disorders are usually asymptomatic and self-limiting, while more severe disorders can lead to death.

- Reduced potassium dietary intake - viewed in isolation, it rarely causes hypokalaemia due to the kidney's ability to compensate by reducing the secretion of potassium. However, it can still cause symptomatic hypokalaemia in severe malnutrition combined with certain medications.

## INITIAL PATIENT ASSESSMENT

### A (AIRWAY)

- Loss of consciousness due to CNS-related symptoms or the progression of cardiac presentations can result in sound phenomena like snoring due to upper airway soft tissue obstruction.
- In the case of seizures, fluid contents can be found in the oral cavity (foam, vomit, etc.) with resulting sound phenomena like gargling.

### B (BREATHING)

- Respiratory rate can be increased due to irritability or psychomotor agitation in milder electrolyte disorders or as compensation for metabolic acidosis. In more severe cases, the respiratory rate can be reduced resulting in bradypnea or apnoea as symptoms progress or cardiac arrest occurs.
- It is important to consider possible injuries that can lead to pathological chest wall motions (e.g., pneumothorax).
- Auscultatory phenomena, e.g., rhonchi or rales due to secretions in the respiratory tract or aspiration of gastric contents.
- With SpO- &lt; 94% (&lt; 88% with COPD) - apply oxygen therapy.

### C (CIRCULATION)

- Due to possible life-threatening cardiac manifestations, it is important to monitor the patient: 3-lead ECG cardiac rhythm monitoring, SpO-, blood pressure monitoring, and regular standard 12-lead ECG recording. Also, wide-diameter peripheral venous access is important.
- Pulse can be altered in line with various arrhythmias connected with electrolyte disorders (bradycardic, tachycardic, regular, or irregular pulse, loss of pulse in cardiac arrest!).
- Hypotension can be linked to various arrhythmias.
- In the case of arrhythmias, hypotension, or altered level of consciousness, a state of shock can occur with pale, cold, dry skin and prolonged capillary refill time.
- Urinary catheter should be placed to monitor urine output (consider the prior history of diuretic therapy when suspecting hypokalaemia) or response to diuretic therapy.
