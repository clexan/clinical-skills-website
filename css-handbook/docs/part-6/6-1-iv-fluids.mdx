## INTRODUCTION

Intravenous (IV) fluid is a drug administered when a person is, due to the nature of a disease, unable to sustain their own basal metabolic needs. This happens in a couple of situations. Human metabolic needs are met with water and food intake. If the level of consciousness is altered, that becomes impossible. Also, when there is a severe gastrointestinal abnormality, even if the patient is theoretically able to swallow food and drink water, absorption is impaired, and deficit develops. There are some states in which IV fluids are administered to compensate for the losses (blood transfusion product in the case of bleeding). To understand indications, contraindications, and for proper choice of an IV fluid, some basic physiological facts need to be stated. Approximately 60% of the human body weight is water. It varies with age, sex, and body composition. Total body water is located intracellularly and extracellularly. Extracellular fluid can be divided into the following compartments:

- interstitial fluid (lymph, protein-poor fluid between the cells),
- intravascular fluid (plasma volume),
- transcellular fluid (gastrointestinal fluid, bile, urine, vitreous body, joint fluid, pleural, peritoneal, and pericardial fluid),
- fluid in bones and dense connective tissue (anatomically significant portion, but does not form functional extracellular fluid because of the slow flow between this and other compartments).

## BASAL METABOLISM

Urine, gastrointestinal fluid production, perspiration, and invisible losses (from skin and lungs) are responsible for basal metabolic needs. If a patient is unable to maintain their basal metabolic requirements through oral intake, fluid and electrolyte disorders will occur. The 4-2-1 formula is used to calculate hourly basal metabolic needs. In some conditions, the retrograde deficit should also be calculated into the deficit (e.g., a patient with a prolonged fasting period) and calculated extra losses should be added. Some losses are impossible to determine precisely (vomiting, diarrhoea). As always, clinical presentation and ABCDE assessment findings guide us to optimise fluid administration (for example, but not limited only to, heart rate, blood pressure, CRT, and urine output). ### UNBALANCED SOLUTIONS

Saline (0.9% NaCl) has a higher chloride ion (strong ion difference the sum of strong cation concentrations (Na, Mg, K) minus the sum of strong anion concentrations [Cl, lactate]). SID of extracellular fluid is approx. 40 mEq/L and SID of saline is 0, meaning that infusing a

0. 9% NaCl solution reduces SID and causes metabolic acidosis. It has a clinically significant effect at doses higher than 1,000 mL when it causes hyperchloraemic metabolic acidosis.

### BALANCED SOLUTIONS

They have chloride concentrations similar to human plasma concentrations. They have added anions such as acetate, malate, and gluconate. They behave as buffers and create bicarbonates. Acetate is metabolised throughout the body, is not dependent on liver metabolism, and metabolises faster than lactates. It does not interfere with glucose or insulin levels. Lactate is metabolised in the liver and can become glucose through gluconeogenesis. In severe shock, there is a disturbance in liver metabolic function, and acetate metabolism remains unaffected. Gluconate metabolism is not fully explained. Besides additional buffers, these solutions contain added different cation concentrations (Na, K, Ca, Mg).

### 2. COLLOIDS

Colloids have higher colloid-osmotic pressure and reduce transcapillary filtration rate in the event of low capillary hydrostatic pressure. Therefore, they expand the plasma volume. Their half-life is longer compared with crystalloids. However, they tend to have a nasty side-effect profile, which limits their indications and allowed volumes to be infused. In emergency settings, the most commonly used colloid is HES 6% (hydroxyethyl starch), which is notorious for coagulopathy, anaphylactoid reactions (&lt; 0.6%), and renal toxicity, making it contraindicated in patients with renal failure. Nevertheless, it is indicated in severe haemorrhagic shock when blood product availability is limited.
