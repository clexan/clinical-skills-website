Haemorrhage results from blood loss from the intravascular space, which leads to a decrease in oxygen delivery to the cells. When oxygen levels decrease to levels insufficient to maintain aerobic metabolism, a state of shock occurs, irrespective of the initial cause. If the state of generalised ischaemia persists, cell death with organ damage and the development of multiorgan failure occurs. Ultimately, this leads to the death of the organism. Oxygen delivery to cells (DO') depends on two factors:

1. cardiac output (CO), which is the product of stroke volume and heart rate,
2. oxygen content in the arterial blood, which is dependent on the haemoglobin concentration (Hb) in the blood, saturation of the arterial blood with oxygen (SaO2), and oxygen partial pressure in the arterial blood (PaO2). ## CLASSIFICATION OF BLEEDING

1. STAGE: &lt; 15% blood volume loss: usually without significant changes in vital signs, IV fluid resuscitation is generally not required.
2. STAGE: 15-30% blood volume loss: the patient becomes tachycardic, and the difference between systolic and diastolic pressures decreases. Compensatory mechanisms are activated, peripheral vasoconstriction occurs, and the skin and mucosa are pale and cold to the touch. The patient may become agitated. Resuscitation of circulating volume with crystalloids and colloids is required. In patients with cardiovascular diseases, consider blood products.
3. STAGE: 30-40% blood volume loss: blood pressure drops (systolic pressure &lt; 90 mmHg), heart rate increases (> 110/min), visible signs of peripheral hypoperfusion/shock - capillary refill time is prolonged, urine output decreases, and consciousness is impaired as cerebral blood flow becomes insufficient. Transfusion of blood products is required. *Haematocrit and haemoglobin findings are not objective and possibly false due to haemoconcentration. The decision on the transfusion of blood products is based on clinical assessment.
4. STAGE: > 40% blood volume loss; compensatory mechanisms are reached.

### BLOOD VOLUME LOSS IS DIFFICULT TO ASSESS IN THE

FOLLOWING PATIENTS:

1. ELDERLY PATIENTS have a reduced ability to compensate in the case of acute bleeding due to their reduced cardiorespiratory reserve. Their blood pressure will decrease more rapidly even in the case of minor bleeding compared with the general population. Similar can be seen in patients who have a so-called low fixed cardiac output, e.g., patients with aortic stenosis that prevents an increase in cardiac output. Their heart is already using compensatory mechanisms for their steady state and further compensation is not possible.

### PATIENTS TAKING CHRONIC THERAPY AND PATIENTS WITH A

PACEMAKER. In patients taking beta-blockers, bleeding will not lead to an increase in heart rate and will also cause a sooner drop in blood pressure. Assessing bleeding based on heart rate in these patients will lead to a significant underestimation of blood loss (no compensatory tachycardia), and the same applies to patients whose heart rate is defined by their pacemakers.

3. PREGNANT WOMEN under normal circumstances have a higher heart rate, increased circulating blood volume, and increased respiratory rate, which may complicate the assessment of blood loss.
4. ATHLETES under normal circumstances and at rest may have a heart rate of &lt; 50/min. In cases of bleeding, significant relative tachycardia in this group of patients may already be present well under 100/min.

## CAUSES OF SEVERE BLEEDING

- traumatic injury (e.g., car accident, fall from a great height, stab or gunshot wound, etc.)
- bleeding from the gastrointestinal tract (e.g., bleeding gastric or duodenal ulcer, intestinal obstruction with necrosis, bleeding from haemorrhoids, etc.)
- massive bleeding in gynaecology and obstetrics (e.g., placental abruption, placenta previa, extrauterine pregnancy, etc.)
- bleeding from large blood vessels (e.g., aortic dissection, ruptured thoracic/abdominal aortic aneurysm)
- tumour infiltrating large blood vessels (e.g., thoracic aorta infiltration by a lung or oesophageal cancer).

### C (CIRCULATION)

- Connect the patient to ECG monitoring, measure the heart rate simultaneously on the central and peripheral arteries, blood pressure (repeat the measurement every 3-5 minutes), and capillary refill time. Place a urinary catheter, and monitor hourly urine output.
- Obtain at least two wide-bore intravenous lines (. 18 G) 8 alternative vascular access if the above is not possible (intraosseous access, central venous access).
- Try to assess the source of bleeding and blood loss:
- Do not forget about possible "hidden" bleeding in the thorax, abdomen, pelvis, retroperitoneal space, and thigh muscles.
- If bleeding is > 15% of total blood volume, administer crystalloids (saline, Ringer's lactate, Plasma-Lyte, etc.) and colloids (6% HES, human albumin).
- Start targeted fluid resuscitation only in the case of hypotension until blood products are ready. Target pressures of controlled/targeted hypotension are presented in more detail below (damage control resuscitation).
- Whatever intravenous fluid you choose, make sure it is warmed to body temperature (hypothermia further worsens coagulopathy).
- Aggressive resuscitation with large volumes of intravenous fluids will lead to an increase in blood pressure, which carries the risk of displacement/damage of an already-formed thrombus at the bleeding site and can cause dilutional coagulopathy. This leads to further bleeding, which creates the need for even greater volume resuscitation - thus creating a vicious cycle. The priority is to provide the patient with emergency surgical/ radiological/endovascular/other haemostasis. During transport to the hospital, prior to haemostasis, systolic blood pressure should be maintained at a minimum level sufficient for the short-term maintenance of organ viability, however low enough not to stimulate bleeding further. According to the latest recommendations, the target pressure values for traumatic bleeding in patients with stab wounds without suspected brain or spinal cord injury and no other risk factors are MAP > 65 mmHg or systolic pressure 80-90 mmHg. In patients with blunt injury and/or brain injury, spinal cord injury, or other risk factors, the pressure values are MAP > 85-90 mmHg or systolic pressure 110-120 mmHg. Adjust the volume of intravenous fluids administered according to the target pressure values, i.e., do not administer intravenous fluids until blood pressure drops below target. Typically, intravenous fluid boluses of 500-1000 mL are administered until the target pressure is reached. As soon as we are able to give the patient blood products and significant bleeding is present, thromboelastography should be monitored and targeted therapy should be applied according to the guidelines. In case the above is not possible, the massive transfusion protocol is 1:1:1 in the form of erythrocyte concentrate, fresh frozen plasma, and platelets with additional clotting factors as needed. Tranexamic acid is an antifibrinolytic agent indicated in massive bleeding that preserves the fibrin plaque at the bleeding site. It is indicated to administer it within three hours of the onset of bleeding, with the most commonly used scheme of 1 g in 100 mL of saline for 10 minutes and continuous administration of 1 g in 500 mL of saline during the next 8 hours. No significant side effects have been reported, but caution should be exercised in patients with existing thromboembolic events, epilepsy, and visual disturbances. Consider commercially available clotting factors:
- recombinant factor VII, prothrombin complex (coagulation factors II, VII, IX, X), antithrombin III, and factor XIII
- cryoprecipitate Vasoconstrictors:
- administer in the case of traumatic brain injury and spinal cord injury to maintain MAP in addition to fluids.

CLINICAL PRESENTATION OF HAEMORRHAGIC SHOCK:

- increase in heart rate (due to catecholamine effect on the heart)
- tachypnoea
- flat jugular veins
- circulation centralisation - vasoconstriction in the "less important tissues":
- cold, mottled skin
- decreased urine output
- consciousness impairment

### VITAL SIGNS TO BE REPEATED EVERY FEW MINUTES IN

PATIENTS WITH HAEMORRHAGIC SHOCK:

- respiratory rate and SpO2
- heart rate, arterial pressure, pulse strenght (central and peripheral)
- ECG (rhythm, rate, and shape of QRS)
- capillary refill time.

### D (DISABILITY)

- Examine the patient's state of consciousness (GCS), pupils, and blood glucose.
- Compensatory mechanisms in bleeding are aimed at maintaining sufficient cerebral blood flow. When bleeding causes consciousness impairment, it usually means > 30% of circulating blood volume is lost and compensatory mechanisms become insufficient.

### E (EXPOSURE)

- Measure the patient's body temperature and warm them.
- Examine the body in detail for the source of bleeding:
- In traumatic bleeding, look for direct and indirect signs of long bone or pelvic fractures, rib fractures, or abdominal distension.
- In women of childbearing age with abdominal pain, do not forget about the possibility of extrauterine pregnancy. Significant bleeding in pregnant women in the later stages of pregnancy is one of the most common causes of maternal mortality.
- In the case of suspected bleeding from the digestive system, do not forget to do a digital rectal examination (a trace of black stool indicates bleeding from the oesophagus or stomach, and a trace of fresh blood may be the result of bleeding from any part of the digestive system). SAMPLE HISTORY: S (Signs / symptoms): adjust the questions to the potential cause and source of bleeding. A (Allergies): check if the patient has allergies. M (Medications): examine the patient for chronic medications; check especially for antiplatelets (acetylsalicylic acid, clopidogrel, prasugrel), anticoagulant drugs (warfarin, NOACs, and beta-blockers). P (Past illnesses): check if the patient has significant comorbidities and whether they have had surgery in the past. L (Last oral intake / last menstrual cycle): check when the patient last ate (possibility of aspiration of gastric contents); in women of childbearing age, check the date of the last menstruation. E (Events leading up to present illness): check for significant events that preceded the current condition.
