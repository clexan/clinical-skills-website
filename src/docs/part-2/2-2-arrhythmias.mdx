## Introduction

Cardiac rhythm disorder, or arrhythmia, refers to any abnormality in the generation and/or conduction of a cardiac impulse. Arrhythmias include a spectrum of disorders, from harmless and haemodynamically insignificant to those leading to haemodynamic collapse and cardiac arrest. We often encounter patients with various cardiac rhythm disorders in daily clinical practice, especially in outpatient emergency services and emergency hospital admissions. Therefore, it is essential to know how to approach such a patient and assess whether their condition is life-threatening and requires urgent treatment. For this to be possible, it is necessary to be familiar with the most common arrhythmias and their potential complications.

## Classification of arrhythmias

Cardiac rhythm and conduction disorders are usually classified according to heart rate into tachyarrhythmias (> 100/min) and bradyarrhythmias (< 60/min).

Depending on the place of origin, tachyarrhythmias are divided into supraventricular and ventricular, and in everyday practice, a more practical classification is into narrow (QRS ≤ 120 ms) and broad (QRS > 120 ms) QRS complex tachycardias. In the case of broad QRS complex tachycardia, cardiac rhythm is most often of ventricular origin, but it can also be a supraventricular disorder, for example SVT with bundle branch block or aberrancy, or SVT in pre-excitation syndrome. Ventricular tachycardia always represents a serious diagnosis and a greater risk of haemodynamic collapse and/or transition to ventricular fibrillation with consequent cardiac arrest.

Bradyarrhythmias occur as a result of disorders in the generation or conduction of impulses at the level of the sinus node or lower parts of the conduction system (AV node, bundle of His). They can be manifested by various disorders, from sinus bradycardia, sinoatrial block, sinus arrest, bradycardia-tachycardia syndrome, or varying degrees of atrioventricular block. After the initial assessment of the patient's condition and, if necessary, emergency treatment, we must distinguish between a reversible disorder due to the effects of drugs, electrolyte disorders, hypothyroidism, hypothermia, or myocardial infarction, and an irreversible condition resulting from an intrinsic conduction system disorder. It is also important to recognise physiological bradycardia caused by excess vagal tone or in athletes, when a sinus rhythm of 40–50/min at rest or up to 30/min during sleep is considered a normal finding.

The haemodynamic effect of a cardiac rhythm disorder in each patient will depend on the type of arrhythmia, its frequency, duration, and the presence of structural heart disease. Thus, a person with a healthy myocardium will tolerate extreme heart rate well (> 150/min, < 40/min), while in people with heart failure or coronary disease, even significantly smaller heart rate deviations will cause symptoms and unfavourable haemodynamic effects.

## Initial approach to the patient with a cardiac arrhythmia

In patients with cardiac arrhythmia, two procedures are essential:

- assessment of the clinical condition
- recognition of arrhythmia and awareness of its potential haemodynamic effects

Assessment of the clinical condition implies the detection of adverse signs or symptoms, the presence of which indicates a serious rhythm disorder that haemodynamically threatens the patient and requires immediate treatment. Adverse signs include:

1. Hypotension (systolic blood pressure < 90 mmHg). If it is accompanied by symptoms of hypoperfusion of the target organs (pale skin, cold sweat, confusion, inappropriate urine output), we are talking about shock.
2. Heart failure. Signs of pulmonary congestion can vary from the auscultatory finding of late inspiratory crackles over the lung bases to the presentation of acute pulmonary oedema.
3. Myocardial ischaemia. This can present with symptoms such as chest pain and/or ECG changes (ST depression, negative T waves).
4. Syncope. This is a short-term, temporary loss of consciousness with complete recovery that occurs due to a sudden drop in cardiac output and thus brain hypoperfusion.

A very efficient and rapid method for the initial assessment and detection of adverse signs in a patient is the ABCDE (airway, breathing, circulation, disability, exposure) approach and patient care. Special emphasis is placed on the intravenous route, recording a 12-lead ECG, and continuous ECG monitoring, blood pressure, and SpO₂.

## Treatment

Initial treatment in the outpatient emergency department or emergency hospital admission will depend primarily on the patient's haemodynamic stability and, of course, the type of arrhythmia. Treatment options are drugs, electroshock delivery in the form of synchronised cardioversion or defibrillation for tachycardia disorders, and external electrostimulation for bradycardic rhythm disorders. In some patients, we can use vagal manoeuvres. It is also important to remove possible triggers (electrolyte disorders, acid-base disturbances, hypoxia, medications).

### Treatment of tachycardia in haemodynamically unstable patients

In patients who are haemodynamically unstable due to tachycardia, it is necessary to perform synchronised cardioversion, which involves shock delivery during the R wave to prevent delivery during the T wave and consequent ventricular fibrillation. The patient needs to be sedated beforehand, usually with propofol in a dose of 1 mg/kg. The initial energy of the biphasic defibrillator is 120–150 J for broad QRS complex tachycardias and atrial fibrillation, and 70–120 J for regular narrow QRS complex tachycardias, including atrial flutter. In the case of failure, increasing the energy level in the next shock is recommended, with a maximum of three attempts.

If conversion does not occur after the third shock, administer amiodarone 300 mg IV over 10–20 minutes, then repeat synchronised cardioversion.

### Treatment of tachycardia in haemodynamically stable patients

If the patient has no adverse symptoms, treatment will depend on the type of arrhythmia. Therefore, the 12-lead ECG should be analysed in detail, and a differential diagnosis should be made to detect the disorder. In that sense, the classification of tachycardias according to QRS complex broadness and RR interval regularity is useful.

### Regular broad QRS complex tachycardia

Regular broad QRS complex tachycardia can be classified as ventricular tachycardia (80%) or supraventricular tachycardia (SVT) with bundle branch block (15%) or with anterograde conduction over the accessory pathway (5%). Any regular broad QRS complex tachycardia is considered ventricular tachycardia until proven otherwise. The procedure is the following:

- Administer amiodarone 300 mg IV over 20–60 minutes or procainamide 10–15 mg/kg IV over 20 minutes (currently not available in our country).
- If there is a previous certain diagnosis of SVT with bundle branch block, treat as for narrow complex tachycardia.

### Irregular broad QRS complex tachycardia

In stable patients, it is usually atrial fibrillation with bundle branch block, while other options are atrial fibrillation with pre-excitation (Wolff–Parkinson–White syndrome) and polymorphic ventricular tachycardia, which are more often accompanied by haemodynamic instability.

In the case of atrial fibrillation with bundle branch block, the approach to the patient is described in detail in the section on narrow QRS complex tachycardias.

Atrial fibrillation with pre-excitation represents an emergency and, most often, requires urgent synchronised cardioversion due to the high risk of transition to ventricular fibrillation. Another option is the use of antiarrhythmic drugs, of which procainamide has proven to be the safest. Among the drugs available in our country, propafenone IV in a dose of 1.5–2 mg/kg can be considered. It is contraindicated to use drugs that slow down AV conduction (beta-blockers, calcium channel blockers, adenosine) and thus stimulate conduction through the accessory pathway, which can lead to a further increase in heart rate and occurrence of ventricular fibrillation.

Polymorphic VT with a prolonged QT interval on the baseline ECG (*torsade de pointes*) also usually requires urgent treatment because of the high incidence of transition to ventricular fibrillation. In such patients, it is necessary to administer magnesium sulphate 2 g IV, correct electrolyte disorders, and stop using drugs that prolong the QT interval. If the arrhythmia persists, the option is overdrive pacing, for which it is necessary to place temporary transvenous pacing.

### Regular narrow QRS complex tachycardia

Paroxysmal supraventricular tachycardias (AVRT, AVNRT), atrial tachycardia, or atrial flutter with regular AV conduction most often present as regular narrow QRS complex tachycardias.

It is initially recommended to apply a vagal manoeuvre (pressure on the carotid sinus or the Valsalva manoeuvre by blowing into a 10 mL syringe), which can terminate PSVT in 54% of cases if performed correctly. On the other hand, in cases of atrial flutter or atrial tachycardia, by slowing AV conduction, the vagal manoeuvre can unmask the flutter waves or P waves. Sometimes it can stop atrial tachycardia, depending on the mechanism of its origin.

If the vagal manoeuvre does not stop the tachycardia, adenosine should be administered in a rapid IV bolus dose of 6 mg, then 12 mg and 18 mg if the previous administration was unsuccessful.

If adenosine does not stop the tachycardia nor unmask atrial flutter, metoprolol 2.5–5 mg IV can be administered up to a maximum of 15 mg or verapamil 2.5–10 mg IV. If pharmacological treatment fails, we can perform synchronised cardioversion, which is rarely necessary for PSVT.

In the case of atrial flutter, the treatment strategy is the same as in patients with atrial fibrillation, including the prevention of thromboembolic events.

### Irregular narrow QRS complex tachycardia

It most often presents as atrial fibrillation or, less often, atrial flutter with variable AV conduction. It is essential to know when the arrhythmia occurred and to assess the risk of thromboembolic complications.

If arrhythmia lasts > 48 h or the time of onset is unknown, a heart rate control strategy is indicated using beta-blockers (bisoprolol 1.25–10 mg orally, metoprolol 2.5–5 mg IV up to a total dose of 15 mg or 25–200 mg orally) or, very rarely, calcium channel blockers (verapamil 2.5–10 mg IV). The target ventricular rate is < 110/min at rest, and if it is not achieved by one of the mentioned drugs, we can consider digoxin (0.2–0.4 mg IV up to a total saturation dose of 1 mg in 24 h; oral maintenance dose 0.1 mg). In such cases, conversion to sinus rhythm without prior anticoagulation for at least 3 weeks is contraindicated due to the risk of thromboembolic complications. If the patient has been regularly taking oral anticoagulant therapy, it is possible to proceed with the cardioversion procedure.

If atrial fibrillation occurs within 48 hours, pharmacological cardioversion can be attempted; if this is unsuccessful, synchronised electrocardioversion can be attempted. Choosing antiarrhythmic drugs depends on the presence of structural heart disease. In patients without structural heart disease, the option is propafenone 1.5–2 mg/kg IV over 10 minutes, and in patients with known heart disease, the option is amiodarone 5–7 mg/kg over 1–2 hours. Depending on the estimated long-term risk of thromboembolism (CHA2DS2-VASc score), some patients will need permanent oral anticoagulant therapy.

### Treatment of bradyarrhythmia in haemodynamically unstable patients

In patients with haemodynamic instability due to bradyarrhythmia, we often encounter third-degree AV block, second-degree Mobitz type II AV block or AV block 2:1, and sinus arrest. Treatment options include the use of atropine and cardiac electrostimulation. Atropine is administered in a dose of 0.5 mg IV, which can be repeated at intervals of 3–5 minutes up to a total of 3 mg. The effect of atropine will depend on the mechanism and localisation of conduction disorders. Thus, atropine will improve conduction in patients with increased vagal tone or an AV node disorder. However, if the disorder is localised below the AV node, it will worsen impulse conduction and the patient's condition. For this reason, the better option is to apply transcutaneous external cardiac electrostimulation and urgently call the cardiology service for further treatment.

### Bradyarrhythmia in haemodynamically stable patients

Immediate treatment is unnecessary in patients who do not have adverse symptoms. We should try to find reversible causes and remove them. It is also important to know which disorders inevitably require further cardiological assessment and treatment due to possible progression to more severe forms and, thus, an increased mortality risk. The following disorders are most often encountered:

- First-degree AV block and second-degree Mobitz type I AV block: mostly benign and do not require active treatment.
- Second-degree Mobitz type II AV block and third-degree AV block: high risk of progression and mortality and represent an indication for implantation of a permanent pacemaker, except in the case of a clear reversible cause.
- Sinus bradycardia: indication for implantation of a permanent pacemaker only when it is symptomatic and after reversible causes are excluded (sick sinus syndrome).
