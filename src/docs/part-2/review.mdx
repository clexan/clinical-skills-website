Part 2 covers the two highest-priority cardiac emergencies: acute coronary syndrome and cardiac arrhythmias. The chapters share a diagnostic structure — 12-lead ECG early, ABCDE throughout — but differ sharply in treatment decisions once the diagnosis is established. This review draws those distinctions together.

## Chapter connections

**Chapter 2.1 — Acute coronary syndrome** covers the recognition and initial management of ACS. The central diagnostic split — STE-ACS versus NSTE-ACS — determines the reperfusion strategy and the timing of antiplatelet therapy.

**Chapter 2.2 — Cardiac arrhythmias** covers the recognition and management of tachycardias and bradycardias. The central clinical split — haemodynamically stable versus unstable — determines whether treatment is immediate (cardioversion, atropine) or can be planned (pharmacological options, monitoring).

Both chapters start with ABCDE and require early 12-lead ECG interpretation.

## STE-ACS versus NSTE-ACS

This is the first clinical decision in every ACS presentation.

| Feature | STE-ACS | NSTE-ACS |
|---|---|---|
| **ECG finding** | ST elevation ≥ 2 adjacent leads (or LBBB) | ST depression, T-wave changes, or normal ECG |
| **Likely pathology** | Complete coronary occlusion | Partial or intermittent occlusion |
| **Reperfusion strategy** | Primary PCI as soon as possible (within 12 h of symptom onset) | Risk-stratified: urgent, early, or selective invasive |
| **P2Y12 inhibitor timing** | Give in loading dose (ticagrelor or prasugrel) before PCI | Do not give routinely before coronary angiography |
| **Aspirin** | 300 mg immediately | 300 mg immediately |
| **Oxygen** | Only if SaO₂ < 90% | Only if SaO₂ < 90% |

The aspirin and oxygen rules are identical in both. The P2Y12 timing is not — this is the most common clinical error in NSTE-ACS management.

## ACS: common treatment steps (both types)

Regardless of ST classification, the following apply to every ACS patient:

- Establish IV access
- Continuous ECG monitoring with defibrillator available
- Measure vital signs including SpO₂
- Give aspirin 300 mg orally
- Treat pain with sublingual nitroglycerin (unless contraindicated) and morphine if needed
- Give oxygen only if SaO₂ < 90%

## Arrhythmia management: stable versus unstable

The critical question in any arrhythmia is whether the patient is haemodynamically stable. Instability is defined by the presence of any of these features: shock, syncope, myocardial ischaemia, or severe heart failure.

| Feature | Unstable | Stable |
|---|---|---|
| **Clinical signs** | Shock, syncope, ischaemia, severe heart failure | None of the above |
| **Tachycardia treatment** | Synchronised cardioversion immediately | Pharmacological options based on mechanism |
| **Bradycardia treatment** | Atropine 0.5 mg IV → repeat to 3 mg → pacing | Monitor; treat only if deteriorating or high asystole risk |
| **Pacing indication** | Haemodynamic compromise unresponsive to atropine | Mobitz II, complete heart block with broad QRS, pauses > 3 s, recent asystole |

## Tachycardia escalation summary

If a tachycardia with life-threatening features does not respond to synchronised cardioversion:

1. Up to three synchronised shocks at appropriate energy
2. Amiodarone 300 mg IV over 10–20 minutes
3. Repeat shock

If the rhythm is regular and the QRS is narrow, vagal manoeuvres or adenosine may be attempted first in a stable patient — but not if there is haemodynamic compromise.

## Bradycardia escalation summary

1. **Atropine 0.5 mg IV** — repeat to maximum 3 mg total
2. If inadequate response: **transcutaneous pacing** (preferred escalation over drugs)
3. Alternative drugs (isoprenaline, adrenaline infusion) while arranging definitive pacing

Even a bradycardia without immediate life-threatening features may still require treatment if the patient has risk factors for asystole: recent asystole, Mobitz II AV block, complete heart block with broad QRS, or ventricular pauses longer than 3 seconds.
