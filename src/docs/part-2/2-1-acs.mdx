# Initial assessment and management of a patient with acute coronary syndrome

*Gordana Bašić*

## Introduction

Acute coronary syndrome (ACS) is a clinical condition caused by sudden, critical myocardial ischaemia. It most often occurs due to atherosclerotic plaque rupture or erosion and the consequent clot formation in the coronary artery, leading to partial or complete luminal obstruction. This results in acute derangement in myocardial perfusion, which, depending on the severity and duration, leads to cardiomyocyte necrosis and myocardial infarction.

ACS is one of the leading causes of death in developed countries and is considered one of the greatest emergencies in modern cardiology and medicine in general. Therefore, it is crucial to promptly recognise a patient with ACS and start treatment as early as possible to reduce the risk of complications and mortality.

## Diagnosis

Since this clinical condition is assigned the highest level of emergency, making the diagnosis as early as possible is crucial. It is essential to recognise the clinical signs and analyse the ECG, and then make a working diagnosis of acute coronary syndrome without waiting for laboratory results to confirm or exclude cardiomyocyte injury.

### Clinical presentation

In most patients, ACS is manifested by the sudden onset of chest discomfort that lasts more than 20 minutes. It usually occurs behind the sternum as burning, discomfort, pressure, or dull pain. Recurrent, shorter episodes of chest pain are also possible. The pain can spread to the neck, arms, most often to the left shoulder and arm, lower jaw, or back.

In the case of inferior wall myocardial infarction, epigastric pain can occur with or without spreading to the retrosternal area. The pain often occurs at rest, without being provoked, typically early in the morning, and it usually awakens the patient from sleep. The onset of symptoms can also be triggered by physical exertion or emotional stress.

When ischaemia affects a large part of the myocardium, it can lead to acute heart failure, the presentation of which can vary from mild signs of pulmonary congestion to acute pulmonary oedema or cardiogenic shock. Due to the increased electrical excitability of the ischaemic myocardium, various rhythm disorders are possible, from paroxysmal atrial fibrillation, ventricular extrasystoles, and short-term episodes of unstable ventricular tachycardia to persistent ventricular tachycardia accompanied by haemodynamic instability or ventricular fibrillation and consequent cardiorespiratory arrest.

Conduction disorders at different levels are also possible, for example varying degrees of AV block due to injury to the conduction system or accentuated vagal tone, usually associated with inferior myocardial infarction. In some patients, such as the elderly or people with long-standing diabetes, the clinical presentation of ACS can be atypical in the sense of undefined chest discomfort, shortness of breath, nausea, or generalised weakness.

After the targeted medical history, a 12-lead ECG should be recorded, if possible, within 10 minutes of first contact.

### Classification of acute coronary syndromes

Based on the initial ECG findings, ACS is classified into two forms:

1. **ST-segment elevation ACS (STE-ACS)** lasting more than 20 minutes.
2. **Non-ST-segment elevation ACS (NSTE-ACS)**, characterised by signs of myocardial ischaemia in the form of ST-segment depression, transient ST-segment elevation, and negative T waves, although the ECG findings may also be normal.

This classification correlates with the pathophysiology of a critical coronary lesion and, at the same time, determines the treatment method. STE-ACS is usually caused by complete atherothrombotic occlusion of one of the coronary arteries and results in ST-segment-elevation myocardial infarction (STEMI). The aim in such patients is to enable reperfusion as soon as possible, that is, to re-establish flow in the endangered myocardium area using percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) or, very rarely nowadays, using fibrinolytic drugs.

On the other hand, NSTE-ACS is usually caused by partial or intermittent coronary artery occlusion, which in some patients results in non-ST-segment-elevation myocardial infarction (NSTEMI), while in others cardiomyocyte necrosis is absent, with normal cardiac troponin levels, which is called unstable angina pectoris. In a small number of patients, NSTE-ACS can manifest as persistent myocardial ischaemia and consequent recurrent or refractory chest pain, acute heart failure, haemodynamic instability, or malignant ventricular arrhythmias.

Accordingly, the approach to the treatment of NSTE-ACS depends on the estimated risk of adverse events. In certain patients, as well as in STEMI patients, emergency invasive approach and revascularisation are necessary, while in others it is possible to start with drug therapy.

## Management of acute coronary syndrome

Despite the aforementioned classification, ACS is actually a spectrum of clinical conditions with a common aetiology and pathogenesis and therefore entails numerous overlaps in approach and treatment, especially in the early prehospital phase or at emergency hospital admission.

Along with a targeted medical history and 12-lead ECG, the approach to each patient with a working diagnosis of acute coronary syndrome emphasises the assessment and treatment of cardiac and circulatory disorders, that is, the **C** from the ABCDE approach, and must include:

- setting up venous access
- measuring vital parameters such as blood pressure, heart rate, and SpO2
- a short physical examination focused on the cardiovascular and respiratory systems
- continuous ECG monitoring with an available defibrillator
- taking blood samples for laboratory analysis during emergency hospital admission
- suppression of pain and dyspnoea
- application of antiplatelet therapy

### Treatment of acute coronary syndrome

#### Suppression of pain and dyspnoea

Reducing pain is one of the important initial steps in treating ACS patients since pain is associated with activation of the sympathetic nervous system, consequent vasoconstriction and tachycardia, and thus additional oxygen demand in an already ischaemic myocardium.

A dose of **0.4 mg sublingual nitroglycerin** is most often administered, which can be repeated up to three times at intervals of 5 to 10 minutes. Nitrates should be avoided in patients with systolic blood pressure below 90 mmHg and in suspected right ventricular infarction.

In the case of persistent pain, intravenous morphine should be considered in single doses of **2 to 4 mg every 5 minutes** until the pain stops, that is, until reaching the highest dose of **20 mg**. To prevent nausea and vomiting, antiemetic drugs should always be used with opioids. Morphine should be administered slowly by intravenous injection to prevent respiratory depression, hypotension, or bradycardia. Besides acting as an analgesic, morphine also has a desirable haemodynamic effect due to vasodilation of the venous and arterial system, thus reducing myocardial oxygen demand.

In hypoxic patients, oxygen should be administered if **SaO2 is below 90%**, while routine oxygen administration is not recommended due to the possible harmful effect of hyperoxia on the injured myocardium. In the case of signs and symptoms of pulmonary congestion, the patient should be given an intravenous diuretic, such as **furosemide 20 to 40 mg** or the dose the patient takes at home, and depending on the severity of the accompanying respiratory insufficiency, non-invasive or invasive ventilation may be indicated.

### Treatment of STE-ACS

In STE-ACS patients in whom symptoms appeared within 12 hours, urgent coronary angiography and primary PCI are indicated to mechanically open the occluded coronary artery as early as possible. Therefore, when diagnosing STE-ACS on site or in health institutions that do not have a PCI centre, the patient should urgently be transported to a centre with a primary PCI program.

Since the activation of platelets and the coagulation cascade play a key role in the pathophysiology of ACS, it is essential to give dual antiplatelet therapy as early as possible, that is, a combination of acetylsalicylic acid and a potent P2Y12 receptor inhibitor in a loading dose:

- **oral acetylsalicylic acid 300 mg**
- **oral P2Y12 inhibitor**: ticagrelor **180 mg** or prasugrel **60 mg**; clopidogrel **600 mg** may be used if ticagrelor or prasugrel is unavailable or contraindicated

Compared with clopidogrel, ticagrelor and prasugrel lead to faster, more reliable, and stronger inhibition of platelet aggregation, with onset of action of about 30 minutes versus 2 to 6 hours for clopidogrel. This is why clopidogrel is used only if the other two drugs are not available or are contraindicated.

### Periprocedural and postprocedural treatment of STE-ACS

During coronary angiography and PCI, patients also receive an anticoagulant drug, that is, **unfractionated heparin 70 to 100 U/kg** or, less often, **enoxaparin 0.5 mg/kg IV**. If there are no other indications, further anticoagulant therapy after PCI is not indicated except for thromboprophylaxis, that is, prevention of deep-vein thrombosis and pulmonary thromboembolism.

Intravenous administration of GPIIb/IIIa inhibitors, which belong to the group of antiplatelet drugs, should be considered in patients with angiographically detected extensive coronary artery thrombosis or in patients with no-reflow or slow-flow phenomenon.

After PCI, patients continue with dual antiplatelet therapy in a maintenance dose for the next 12 months:

- **acetylsalicylic acid 75 to 100 mg**
- **ticagrelor 2 × 90 mg** or **prasugrel 10 mg**; a reduced prasugrel dose of **5 mg** is used for body weight below 60 kg or age above 75 years; **clopidogrel 75 mg** is an alternative

At the end of that period, the P2Y12 inhibitor is discontinued and the patient continues acetylsalicylic acid permanently. Depending on the individually assessed risk of bleeding and risk of ischaemic events, the period of dual therapy can be shortened or extended in certain patients.

### Fibrinolytic therapy

If the estimated time from STEMI diagnosis to PCI reperfusion is longer than 120 minutes, it is recommended to administer intravenous fibrinolytic therapy with a fibrin-specific agent such as **alteplase**. Thanks to the developed network of primary PCI, it is rarely indicated today.

Fibrinolysis is also followed by dual antiplatelet therapy in loading doses, **acetylsalicylic acid 300 mg** and **clopidogrel 300 mg**, or **75 mg in patients older than 75 years**, and the patient must then be transported to the PCI centre to perform routine early coronary angiography and PCI within **2 to 24 hours**.

### Treatment of NSTE-ACS

As previously mentioned, there are different categories of NSTE-ACS patients. The correct choice of treatment strategy is based on an early assessment of the risk of ischaemia and bleeding in each patient. NSTE-ACS patients are divided into three categories according to the risk of ischaemia, which also determines the degree of urgency and timing of invasive cardiac assessment:

1. **Very high risk** — emergency coronary angiography within **2 hours** of admission.
2. **High risk** — early invasive assessment within **24 hours** of admission.
3. **Low risk** — selective invasive assessment only in patients in whom non-invasive assessment shows signs of inducible ischaemia.

In very high-risk patients, the treatment strategy is the same as in STEMI patients. Before referring to urgent invasive assessment, the patient’s pain should be suppressed and **acetylsalicylic acid 300 mg** administered as early as possible.

In NSTE-ACS, it is no longer recommended to routinely administer P2Y12 inhibitors before coronary angiography and insight into the anatomy of the coronary arteries. Studies have shown that pretreatment with P2Y12 inhibitors does not improve outcomes compared with their use during PCI and can significantly endanger the patient and increase the risk of bleeding if urgent surgical revascularisation is indicated due to multivessel coronary artery disease.

In other NSTE-ACS patients, initial drug therapy includes antithrombotic and anti-ischaemic therapy. Antithrombotic therapy includes:

- **acetylsalicylic acid 300 mg initially**, then a maintenance dose of **100 mg**
- **fondaparinux 2.5 mg SC** or **enoxaparin 1 mg/kg twice daily**

Beta-blockers are indicated for reducing myocardial oxygen demand if the patient is haemodynamically stable and without signs of acute heart failure. In persistent pain, nitrates should also be administered sublingually or intravenously. Most NSTE-ACS patients undergo invasive assessment within the aforementioned time intervals, except for patients who do not have high-risk or very high-risk criteria, have normal troponin levels, and in whom non-invasive testing excluded inducible ischaemia.

### Periprocedural and postprocedural treatment of NSTE-ACS

A patient with an indication for PCI after coronary angiography should receive a potent P2Y12 inhibitor in a loading dose, **prasugrel 60 mg** or **ticagrelor 180 mg**, and additionally a bolus of **unfractionated heparin 70 to 100 U/kg** or **enoxaparin 0.5 mg/kg body weight** if the patient has previously received it.

If there is no other indication, further use of anticoagulant therapy after PCI is not indicated even in NSTE-ACS patients, except for thromboprophylaxis. Recommendations regarding dosage and duration of dual antiplatelet therapy in NSTE-ACS patients are the same as in STE-ACS patients.
