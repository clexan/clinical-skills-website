An acutely ill patient is one with an immediate threat of deterioration of vital functions, such as the cardiovascular, respiratory, and neurological systems, with a risk for subsequent cardiac arrest. This can result from the initial illness or from a combination of chronic comorbidities and an acute state.

Handling these patients requires a standardised, systematic, and simple initial assessment and management approach. Simplified and focused examinations provide enough information about the patient's current status and need for therapeutic interventions. The accidental or deliberate omission of one or more vital parameters may delay recognition of the problem and further worsen the patient's condition.

With a structured examination, in most situations we obtain enough information to form a differential diagnosis and plan the initial management of the acutely ill patient. One of the tools for standardised and systematic initial patient assessment is the **ABCDE algorithm**. It consists of five categories, where each letter represents one category during the assessment.

The assessment is performed in alphabetical order. If a deviation or problem is encountered during the inspection, immediate therapeutic intervention is performed. In other words, if there is a problem at **B**, the recommendation is to solve that problem, stabilise it if possible, and only then move on to **C**. Life-threatening conditions in patients are most often manifested through changes in cardiocirculatory, respiratory, and neurological systems; therefore, these systems have the most important place in the rapid assessment.

## A - Airway

**A** stands for airway and evaluates problems with airway obstruction. Obstruction can be partial or complete and may be caused by decreased tone of soft tissues in the upper airway, food, blood, gastric content, oedema of the soft tissues of the tongue, pharynx, and larynx, laryngospasm, bronchospasm, and others.

Complete airway obstruction can quickly cause cardiac arrest. Sometimes, after complete airway obstruction, a **see-saw phenomenon** can be seen in the form of paradoxical inverse movement of the chest cavity and abdomen. If the respiratory drive is still present, the diaphragm and intercostal muscles contract and prepare for air to enter. If there is no inspired air, reverse movement of the chest and abdomen can be seen instead of regular simultaneous abdomen and chest movement.

Partial obstruction is a common problem in clinical practice. In that case, sound phenomena can usually be heard as snoring, gargling, and inspiratory stridor. Depending on which of these are detected, one can estimate an approximate location of obstruction in the upper or lower respiratory tract.

A common problem is partial airway obstruction caused by acute deterioration in the level of consciousness. It is expected that patients with a Glasgow Coma Score (**GCS**) of 8 or fewer points do not have protective reflexes, so protection of the airway against gastric reflux and regurgitation of content with an endotracheal tube is required in that case.

### How to keep the airway open

Opening the airway is one of the key points of patient treatment. If airway obstruction is visible, it is necessary to act immediately. One of the basic approaches is the **head-tilt chin-lift manoeuvre** in its original form, which can be supplemented with adjunct manoeuvres. The upper hand is placed on the patient's forehead, and the lower hand pushes the bony part of the mandible until resistance is felt.

If a fracture of the cervical vertebrae is suspected, for example after a fall from height, a traffic accident, or a jump into a shallow pool or beach, the manoeuvre should be modified. Instead of head tilt, fixation of the skull is made with pressure on the zygomatic arches on both sides, followed by a regular chin lift. If there is suspicion of spinal fracture combined with visible trauma of the facial bones, the head is fixated in the area of the temporal bones bilaterally with a regular chin lift movement.

Once the airway is opened, it should be kept that way. This is achieved with an oro- or nasopharyngeal airway or with supraglottic (SGA) or infraglottic devices. Usually, the process starts with the lowest-level device, for example a nasopharyngeal airway, and escalates according to the patient's state. Commonly used devices are the **oropharyngeal airway** and **nasopharyngeal airway**.

The **oropharyngeal airway** is inserted through the mouth. Its concave side slides along the hard palate. Once the top of the airway reaches the soft palate, a change of resistance is felt, and the tube should be rotated by 180° and advanced so that air can freely pass from outside through the oral cavity and pharynx into the larynx.

For the airway tube to function appropriately, the right size should be used. The appropriate size is estimated by placing it in a position in which it stands in the oral cavity, with the lower edge at the mandibular angle. A tube of proper size should have the upper edge at the level of the incisors. If the proper size is unavailable, a larger size is more appropriate.

As with every other tube, practical problems can occur. First, the tube is near the oropharyngeal aperture and base of the tongue, which are areas with significant vagal innervation. This can provoke a vomiting reflex if one is still present in the patient; in that case, the airway must be removed immediately. Second, trauma of the oral cavity and trismus can make insertion difficult or impossible.

The **nasopharyngeal airway** is inserted into the pharynx through the nasal cavity. It is cut diagonally on its lower edge, and the shorter margin should be placed medially during insertion. Rotating movements are performed while advancing through the lower nasal cavity perpendicularly to the patient's head.

The appropriate size of the nasopharyngeal airway is estimated by placing the lower edge on the earlobe, while the upper edge should be at the nostril opening. Some commercially available nasopharyngeal tubes come with an adjustable flange to mark and secure the airway at an optimal depth for a particular patient after insertion.

The nasopharyngeal airway is better tolerated than the oropharyngeal airway in patients who still have protective reflexes, including pharyngeal, laryngeal, and cough reflexes. Difficulties can arise if there is a nasal fracture. In addition, if there is a suspected skull base fracture, such as Brillen hematoma, otorrhoea, rhinorrhoea, or mastoid hematoma, placement of a nasopharyngeal airway is contraindicated because a potentially fractured lamina cribrosa of the ethmoidal bone can be additionally damaged by the device.

One of the essential supraglottic devices is a **laryngeal mask airway (LMA)**. The most commonly used version is the **i-gel**, which has thermoplastic elastomers on its lower edge that ensure better placement on the laryngeal aditus. This provides a better seal and lowers the probability of aspiration of gastric content into the lungs.

Insertion is performed by holding the LMA in the hand like a pencil and advancing the mask through the oral cavity. It is useful to push the soft tissues of the tongue aside to place it more easily. Its upper edge has a connector that enables bag-valve-mask placement and assists ventilation if needed.

The ALS algorithm for cardiac arrest states that an LMA is an adequate airway protection device for providers who are not highly skilled in intubation. However, research shows that although it does provide some protection, it does not completely eliminate the risk of aspiration of gastric content in the case of regurgitation. Moreover, it can even provoke vomiting if vagal reflexes are present or if ventilation is performed under higher pressure with a leak into the oesophagus and subsequent gastric distention. **Endotracheal intubation remains the gold standard for airway protection.**

## B - Breathing

The second category is **B**, which stands for the breathing parameters. First, the respiratory rate is assessed. It is measured by observing breathing mechanics while placing a hand on the lower part of the patient's chest for 30 seconds.

Normal respiratory rate is between **10 and 20 breaths per minute** (*eupnoea*). A bradypnoeic patient (< 10/min) has, despite appropriate tidal volume, inappropriate exchange of oxygen and carbon dioxide in the lungs, so the patient needs assisted ventilation. For example, if the patient breathes 6/min, 6 additional breaths should be added to reach an appropriate frequency and therefore an adequate minute volume. An apnoeic patient requires constant assisted ventilation.

A tachypnoeic patient (> 20/min) has significantly higher energy expenditure on the work of breathing, which eventually leads to muscle fatigue and the use of accessory respiratory muscles, including intercostal muscles and neck muscles.

Normal breathing mechanics also implies symmetric movement of both sides of the thorax synchronously with the abdomen. In the case of asymmetric movement, further examination should be performed to distinguish potential pathology.

Auscultation of the lungs can provide a lot of information that helps in the clinical examination. Inspiratory stridor can point to a problem in the upper respiratory tract, whereas inspiratory and expiratory crackles can point to a problem in the lower respiratory tract. If lung auscultation shows abnormalities, percussion of the thorax can distinguish whether air is trapped in the thorax (**pneumothorax** - hyperresonance) or whether there is an effusion (dull, flat sound). Lung ultrasound and radiography are additional examination methods that can help in further diagnosis and are commonly used in these cases. In the case of tension pneumothorax (**PTX**), urgent decompression of the compressed lung is necessary.

Furthermore, pulse oximetry is a vital parameter in ABCDE assessment. It is a non-invasive measurement of a patient's peripheral blood oxygen saturation. In most patients, normal saturation is between **94 and 98%**, while **88-92%** is tolerated in patients with chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (**COPD**). If there is a lack of oxygen, it can be compensated with a nasal cannula, simple face mask, or face mask with reservoir.

The mixture of air that is normally inhaled (**fraction of inspired oxygen - FiO₂**) contains 21% oxygen. The fraction of inspired oxygen can be enriched through nasal cannulas and masks. The **nasal cannula** can supplement oxygen through **1-4 L/min** flow, thus increasing the inspiratory fraction to **40%**. A **face mask** supplements **4-10 L/min** flow (**FiO₂ 35-60%**). A **face mask with reservoir** supplements **10-15 L/min** (**FiO₂ 60-95%**). Which device to use depends entirely on the patient's clinical state and is discussed further in the chapter on acute respiratory insufficiency.

### Key points

- Respiratory rate, symmetry of chest movement, lung auscultation, pulse oximetry
- Supplementation of oxygen: nasal cannula, simple face mask, face mask with reservoir
- Assisted ventilation

## C - Circulation

**C** stands for circulation. Cardiovascular disturbances are often a sign of general deterioration in a patient. Several parameters are included in the **C** assessment: characteristics of the patient's pulse, blood pressure, skin colour, capillary refill time, and diuresis.

An individual parameter may not always provide much information, while combined analysis of all parameters and their trends often gives a good general picture of the patient's haemodynamic profile and risk of deterioration and cardiac arrest.

Pulse is characterised by its rate, rhythm, and strength. Normal heart rate ranges from **60 to 100 beats per minute**, while a rate below **60/min** is called **bradycardia**, and above **100/min** is called **tachycardia**. A bradycardic pulse can sometimes be found in young, healthy individuals without cardiac pathology. A typical example of a completely arrhythmic pulse can be found in patients with **atrial fibrillation**, a commonly found pathological rhythm in elderly patients. Heart rhythm and its pathology are explained in more detail in Chapter 2.2.

Pulse is regularly equally palpable centrally, for example in the carotid artery, and peripherally, for example in the radial artery. If pulse strength is weaker in the radial artery, the estimated systolic blood pressure is less than **80 mmHg**, indicating a hypotensive state.

The focus of skin assessment is on warmth and wetness. Normal skin is dry and warm, while in haemodynamic disturbances it is often clammy, sweaty, and cold. Pale skin can point to anaemia and/or centralisation of blood flow. Cyanotic skin can indicate hypoxia, while red and swollen skin can indicate anaphylaxis.

**Capillary refill time (CRT)** is a parameter that indicates adequacy of peripheral blood flow. Because the skin is the organ with the lowest level of autoregulation, it is also the first organ from which blood will be centralised in the case of reduced cardiac output, for example in hypovolaemia. Measurement is performed by applying pressure to the skin near bony tissue, typically the sternum or forehead. If it takes longer than **2 seconds** for a distal capillary bed to regain its colour, blood flow through the skin and other peripheral tissues is insufficient. The longer the capillary refill time, the stronger the centralisation, as typically seen in an actively bleeding patient.

Centralisation of blood flow is an adaptive response to haemodynamic disturbance. It consists of vasoconstriction in the **peripheral** organ system, such as skin and muscles, in order to preserve adequate blood flow through vital organs such as the brain, heart, and lungs. Clinically, centralisation is seen in characteristic manifestations: pale and clammy skin, capillary refill time longer than 2 seconds, and tachycardia. Blood pressure measurement in these cases may show hypotension with systolic blood pressure below **90 mmHg**.

Besides clinical examination, several interventions should be performed during assessment of the cardiovascular system. An acutely ill patient should be placed on continuous **electrocardiographic (ECG) monitoring**. Basic monitoring includes at least three Einthoven leads: the red lead on the upper right side of the thorax, the yellow lead on the upper left side of the thorax, and the green electrode on the lower left part of the thorax. After the patient is placed on ECG monitoring, the cardiac rhythm strip should be analysed. Depending on the patient's history and clinical examination, a **12-lead ECG** should be recorded.

Placing **intravenous (IV) access** is one of the basic interventions in acutely ill patients. Once IV access is placed, blood samples can be analysed and intravenous medications can be administered.

### Key points

- Basic examination: pulse (rate, rhythm, strength), skin (colour, wetness, warmth), blood pressure, capillary refill time, diuresis
- ECG monitoring, IV access

## D - Disability

A short neurological assessment is performed during the **D** category (*disability*). State of consciousness, pupillary response, and blood glucose levels are regularly monitored.

Basic quantitative assessment of the state of consciousness is checked through the **AVPU** score. **A** stands for an alert patient. **V** represents a patient who reacts to verbal stimuli and corresponds to somnolence. The patient in the **P** category does not react to verbal stimuli but reacts to painful stimuli, corresponding to stupor. **U** stands for a patient unresponsive to any stimuli, which corresponds to coma.

AVPU roughly corresponds to categories measured more thoroughly by the **GCS**, a scoring system that should be considered if the patient has a primary neurological disorder.

Pupils are typically equally wide and respond to light with bilateral constriction. Asymmetry of pupils can imply increased intracranial pressure. In acutely ill patients, bilaterally dilated pupils that do not respond to light imply global cerebral circulation disorder.

**Hypoglycaemia** is one of the most common causes of acutely deteriorated neurological status, which makes glucose measurement an essential part of neurological assessment.

### Key points

- AVPU scale
- Pupillary response, blood glucose measurement

## E - Exposure and environment

The last category in the assessment includes **exposure and environment (E)**. It includes examination of other organ systems not previously included and a detailed examination of the skin of the whole body in search of further clinical signs that can be connected to already identified pathology.

Body temperature should also be measured. Depending on the symptoms observed, the examination can include abdominal examination, rectal examination, neurological orientation examination, **FAST** (*Focused Assessment with Sonography in Trauma*) ultrasound examination, and others.

### Key points

- Temperature
- FAST ultrasound, last menstrual cycle

## Medical history

In order to help with differential diagnosis and decide on the plan for initial management of the acutely ill patient, a targeted medical history should be taken from the patient or caregiver. A short medical history can be sampled through six basic categories that form the acronym **SAMPLE**.

Symptoms are very often connected to the circumstances that led to them. Multiple comorbidities and numerous chronic medications can direct the therapeutic approach and examination. For example, due to polyneuropathy in acute coronary syndrome, people with diabetes do not necessarily have chest pain and often present with non-specific symptoms, which leads to the conclusion that a **12-lead ECG** must be recorded.

The time passed since the last meal indicates stomach fullness, which affects the strategy for possible endotracheal intubation. The stomach is considered empty **6 hours** after consumption of solid food, **4 hours** after consumption of liquid food, and **2 hours** after consumption of water.

In women of reproductive age, indicative medical-history information can be the time of the last menstruation, which is important in the differential diagnosis of abdominal pain.

If it is not possible to obtain data from the patient, history from a caregiver or next of kin plays a significant role. Acutely ill patients may deteriorate during examination, so it is important to gather as much anamnestic data as possible while it is still possible. In patients able to communicate, it is essential first to inquire about basic data and then continue the examination according to the ABCDE approach, so that if the condition deteriorates, at least some anamnestic data are already available.

### SAMPLE history

| Letter | Component | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **S** | Symptoms | duration, intensity, character (e.g. pain) |
| **A** | Allergies | primarily to medications |
| **M** | Medications | therapy for chronic diseases |
| **P** | Past medical history | in correlation with the previous section (M) |
| **L** | Last oral intake | the time elapsed since the last meal or drink |
| **E** | Events leading to the incident | circumstances in which symptoms happened |

## Appendix 1: Recognition of an acutely ill patient in hospital conditions

Abnormal physiology is common in general wards, yet research shows that observation and measurement of vital parameters occur less than recommended. Assessment of simple vital signs may help prevent cardiopulmonary arrest.

One way to increase the success of early identification of critical patients in hospital wards is to use the **Early Warning Score (EWS)** protocol. EWS enables repeated assessment of the patient's vital parameters at defined time intervals. The assessment is mainly carried out by a medical technician.

If the patient scores **3-5**, they are observed once every **4 hours**. If the sum is **6 or more**, the ward doctor should be alerted and then assesses the need for more frequent observation, for example once every hour. Patients with a score of **9 or more** require continuous monitoring, which is most often carried out in intensive care units, emergency room observation rooms, and other appropriate settings.

The most important aspect is recognising major changes in specific parameters over time. If the patient's condition deteriorates, a decision is made about further treatment and the need to refer the critical patient to treatment services.
