Acute deterioration is easier to recognise when the first assessment is deliberately structured.
The ABCDE approach gives the team a shared sequence, a common language, and a clear rule:
identify the immediate threat, intervene, and then reassess before progressing.

In simulation and in practice, the value of the method is not only diagnostic. It prevents the
team from missing reversible problems while trying to build a perfect differential too early. A
patient with airway obstruction, critical hypoxaemia, or profound hypotension needs correction of
those problems first; only then does the rest of the history become useful.

## Why ABCDE remains the safest starting point

The sequence follows physiology. Loss of airway patency can progress to cardiac arrest within
minutes, and severe breathing failure or circulatory collapse quickly produces secondary
neurological decline. Working in order helps clinicians focus on the cause most likely to kill the
patient first.

The framework also supports teamwork. One person can verbalise the current step, another can
prepare the likely intervention, and the rest of the team can anticipate what comes next. In a
busy simulation room this reduces duplicated work and makes escalation easier to justify.

Practical reassessment is built into the method. If the patient improves after oxygen, suction,
fluids, bronchodilators, or glucose, that response is part of the clinical information. If the
patient does not improve, the team knows it has to escalate or widen the differential.

## Airway and breathing come first

During **A**, the first question is simple: is air moving freely to the lungs? Snoring, gurgling,
stridor, silence with respiratory effort, or reduced consciousness should trigger immediate airway
action. Open the airway manually, clear visible obstruction when safe, suction secretions if
available, and prepare adjuncts early if the patient cannot maintain patency.

During **B**, move rapidly from observation to intervention. Count the respiratory rate, inspect the
pattern of breathing, look for cyanosis, listen for added sounds, and correlate what you see with
oxygen saturation and the patient’s work of breathing. Severe fatigue, accessory muscle use, or a
falling level of consciousness means the patient may not sustain ventilation without help.

Common first-line actions include high-concentration oxygen when indicated, positioning the patient
to improve ventilation, using inhaled bronchodilators for bronchospasm, and preparing bag-mask
ventilation if spontaneous breathing is inadequate. Every intervention should be followed by
another look at the chest, the monitor, and the patient’s overall effort.

## Circulation, disability, and exposure complete the rapid survey

The **C** assessment asks whether the patient is perfusing vital organs. Check pulse rate and
quality, blood pressure, capillary refill, skin temperature, and evidence of external bleeding.
Attach ECG monitoring early and establish intravenous or intraosseous access when the clinical
picture suggests shock, sepsis, arrhythmia, or the need for urgent medication.

During **D**, perform a brief neurological screen rather than a lengthy examination. AVPU or GCS,
pupil findings, and bedside glucose are usually enough to identify the next urgent action. A low
glucose concentration is common, easy to miss, and rapidly reversible, so it deserves explicit
attention in every unstable patient.

During **E**, expose only as much as necessary to identify hidden bleeding, rashes, trauma, burns,
or signs of infection while protecting the patient from heat loss and loss of dignity. Temperature
measurement, targeted abdominal or limb inspection, and a quick search for medical devices, scars,
or medication patches often explain deterioration that was not obvious at the door.

## A focused SAMPLE history adds context after immediate threats are addressed

Once the patient is more stable, the team should collect a brief **SAMPLE** history:
symptoms, allergies, medications, past medical history, last oral intake, and events leading to
the illness. This is usually enough to direct the next investigation or treatment pathway.

The goal is not a perfect history on the first pass. It is to gather the pieces that change
management immediately. Anticoagulant use matters in trauma, insulin or oral hypoglycaemics matter
in altered mental status, and the last meal matters if airway interventions or anaesthesia may be
needed.

The most effective teams keep cycling between treatment and reassessment. After every major
intervention, run the relevant part of ABCDE again and decide whether the patient is improving,
static, or worsening. That loop is what turns the framework from a checklist into a clinical tool.
