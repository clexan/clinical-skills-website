# Advanced life support (ALS) algorithm

*Ivan Vuksan, Erika Šuper-Petrinjac*

The advanced life support (ALS) algorithm is a standardised approach for the management of patients in cardiac arrest. It is extremely useful because it allows treatment to begin without losing time for discussion, thereby increasing the team’s efficiency during resuscitation.

Heart rhythms associated with cardiac arrest can be divided into two groups:

1. **Shockable rhythms**, which include pulseless ventricular tachycardia (**pVT**) and ventricular fibrillation (**VF**).
2. **Non-shockable rhythms**, which include pulseless electrical activity (**PEA**) and asystole.

The main difference in the management of these two groups is that patients with pVT/VF need to be defibrillated as early as possible. Other cardiopulmonary resuscitation (CPR) actions, such as chest compressions, airway management and ventilation, intravenous/intraosseous access, administration of adrenaline, and identification and management of reversible causes, are common to both groups. Additional procedures may be indicated if cardiac arrest is caused by special circumstances. It is essential to note that measures that improve survival after cardiac arrest include prompt and effective bystander CPR, high-quality chest compressions with minimal interruptions, and early defibrillation.

## Management of shockable rhythms (pVT, VF)

1. Confirm cardiac arrest by simultaneously checking the pulse and breathing for 10 seconds.
2. Call the resuscitation team.
3. Initiate chest compressions in a 30:2 ratio while simultaneously placing self-adhesive defibrillation/monitoring pads — one below the right clavicle and the other at the V6 position in the midaxillary line.
4. Stop chest compressions to avoid artefacts on the monitor. Confirm VF/pVT on ECG.
5. Immediately continue chest compressions. At the same time, the person operating the defibrillator should choose the appropriate energy (**150–200 J biphasic** for the first defibrillation and **150–360 J biphasic** for subsequent defibrillations) and press the charge button.
6. While the defibrillator is charging, warn all present team members, except the one performing CPR, to stand back and move the oxygen source at least one meter away from the defibrillator.
7. When the defibrillator is charged, tell the person performing chest compressions to “step back”. When that person moves away, deliver the shock.
8. Immediately after shock delivery, proceed with CPR (30:2) without checking the rhythm or pulse, starting with chest compressions.
9. Continue CPR for 2 minutes and secure the airway with an advanced device during that time.
10. After 2 minutes, stop briefly and check the monitor.
11. If the rhythm is VF/pVT, repeat steps 6–10 and deliver a second shock. Continue CPR for 2 minutes. If the airway is secured with an advanced device (for example, a laryngeal mask or endotracheal tube), perform CPR continuously without pauses for ventilation, with chest compressions at **100–120/min** and a respiratory rate of **10/min**. Think about reversible causes of death (4H and 4T). If a reversible cause is detected, begin correcting it.
12. If VF/pVT persists, repeat steps 6–8 and deliver a third shock. Immediately resume CPR and give **adrenaline 1 mg IV/IO** and **amiodarone 300 mg IV/IO** while continuing CPR for the next 2 minutes.
13. If VF/pVT persists, repeat the 2-minute sequence of **CPR → rhythm/pulse check → defibrillation**.
14. Give another **1 mg of adrenaline IV** after every other shock (that is, every **3–5 minutes**).

## Treatment of non-shockable rhythms (PEA, asystole)

1. Begin CPR 30:2.
2. As soon as IV/IO access is established, give **adrenaline 1 mg IV/IO**.
3. Continue CPR 30:2 until the airway is secured. Once an advanced airway is secured, continue chest compressions without stopping during ventilation for 2 minutes.
4. After 2 minutes, check the rhythm. If organised electrical activity is shown, check for a pulse and/or signs of life.
   a. If there is a pulse and/or signs of life, start post-resuscitation care.
   b. If there is no palpable pulse and/or no signs of life (**PEA**), continue CPR.
5. While performing CPR, think about reversible causes of death (4H and 4T).
6. After 2 minutes, check the rhythm again and continue according to the assessed rhythm. In persistent cardiac arrest, once given, repeat **adrenaline 1 mg IV every 3–5 minutes**, which corresponds to every other 2-minute CPR cycle:
   - If the monitor shows **VF/pVT**, follow the shockable side of the algorithm that includes defibrillation.
   - If the monitor shows **asystole**, continue CPR.

## Reversible causes of death (4H and 4T)

| 4H | 4T |
| --- | --- |
| Hypoxia | Tension pneumothorax |
| Hypovolaemia | Tamponade |
| Hyperkalaemia, hypokalaemia, and metabolic disorders | Thrombosis (coronary thrombosis, pulmonary embolism) |
| Hypothermia | Toxic agents |

During the treatment of cardiac arrest, you should keep in mind the possible reversible causes that led to it, which are divided into two groups (4H and 4T) to make them easier to remember.

**Hypoxia:** Reduce the risk of hypoxia by oxygenating with 100% oxygen, providing adequate ventilation, and securing the airway by placing an i-gel or an endotracheal tube.

**Hypovolaemia:** Pulseless electrical activity is often caused by hypovolaemia, which can result from, for example, bleeding or sepsis. Intravascular volume should be replaced immediately with intravenous fluids and blood through a wide-lumen peripheral venous cannula (minimum green IV cannula, diameter 18 G or wider).

**Hyperkalaemia, hypokalaemia, hypoglycaemia, hypocalcaemia, and other metabolic disorders:** These can be detected by laboratory testing or from the medical history (for example, kidney failure). Each requires specific therapy.

**Hypothermia:** Hypothermia should be suspected in all drowning victims and in patients found outdoors. When measuring temperature, use a thermometer with a low-temperature reading option. Such patients should be warmed by passive methods (blankets, aluminium foil) and active methods (heated air, warm IV fluids, rinsing cavities with warm fluids).

**Tension pneumothorax:** This may be the primary cause of pulseless electrical activity. The most important causes are trauma, barotrauma in patients on positive-pressure ventilation, and asthma. Treatment is rapid thoracostomy or needle thoracocentesis.

**Tamponade:** Tamponade can be diagnosed by placing an ultrasound probe below the xiphoid. If tamponade is the cause of cardiac arrest, an emergency thoracotomy or pericardiocentesis should be performed as soon as possible.

**Drug poisoning:** This can be detected later by laboratory tests. If available, use appropriate antidotes.

**Thromboembolic or mechanical obstruction:** The most common causes are massive pulmonary embolism (PE) or coronary artery thrombosis. If pulmonary embolism is suspected as the cause of cardiac arrest, consider immediate administration of thrombolytics. Suspicion of coronary thrombosis can be supported by the patient’s history (for example, chest pain) or by a pre-arrest 12-lead ECG showing ischaemic changes.
