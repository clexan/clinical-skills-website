Part 1 covers the foundational habits that underpin every acute clinical encounter: a structured first assessment, systematic ECG reading, reliable vascular access, and the ALS algorithm when arrest is present. This review draws those threads together.

## Chapter connections

**Chapter 1.1 — Initial assessment** establishes the ABCDE sequence and SAMPLE history. The core rule is treat before advancing, and reassess after every intervention. Nothing in the chapters that follow overrides this: ALS begins with ABCDE, sepsis begins with ABCDE, every chapter in the handbook opens with ABCDE.

**Chapter 1.2 — ECG interpretation** gives two related but distinct skills: rhythm analysis using a six-question approach (electrical activity, rate, regularity, QRS width, atrial activity, AV relationship), and systematic 12-lead review (rhythm, rate, axis, ST and T changes). Both are needed in practice. Rhythm strips appear on monitors; 12-leads appear in ACS, arrhythmia, and electrolyte chapters.

**Chapter 1.3 — Vascular access** covers peripheral IV insertion and intraosseous access as the alternative when IV fails or is too slow. Both are routes to the same goal: reliable drug and fluid delivery.

**Chapter 1.4 — ALS algorithm** applies everything above to cardiac arrest: rhythm recognition drives the shockable or non-shockable pathway, CPR quality determines perfusion during the cycle, and the 4H and 4T framework structures the search for reversible causes.

## First assessment and SAMPLE

- Always use the **ABCDE approach** for the initial assessment of an acutely ill patient. Do not skip steps. The goals are a patent airway, adequate oxygenation and ventilation, haemodynamic stability, recognition and correction of neurological derangement, and identification of likely causes.
- Include a **SAMPLE history** whenever possible. If the patient cannot provide it, ask family members, caregivers, or witnesses.
- After every intervention, run the relevant ABCDE steps again and decide whether the patient is improving, static, or worsening. Reassessment is not a separate task — it is built into the method.

## ECG: rhythm strip versus 12-lead

Both ECG skills are in Chapter 1.2, but they answer different questions.

| Feature | Rhythm strip analysis | 12-lead ECG review |
|---|---|---|
| **Primary question** | What is the rhythm? | What is happening to the myocardium? |
| **Steps** | Electrical activity → rate → regularity → QRS width → atrial activity → AV relationship | Rhythm → rate → axis → ST segments → T waves |
| **Typical context** | Monitor in any emergency | Suspected ACS, arrhythmia with haemodynamic significance, electrolyte disorder |
| **Key output** | Rhythm identification (e.g. AF, VT, complete heart block) | STEMI localisation, ischaemia pattern, axis deviation |

In practice both are used together. The monitor rhythm strip gives a continuous view; the 12-lead provides regional detail.

## Vascular access: IV versus IO

| Feature | Peripheral IV | Intraosseous (IO) |
|---|---|---|
| **First choice** | Yes, in most situations | No — used when IV fails or is impractical |
| **Preferred sites** | Antecubital fossa, forearm, dorsum of hand | Proximal tibia, distal tibia, proximal humerus |
| **Key advantages** | Familiar, lower risk | Rapid, reliable in circulatory collapse |
| **Drug delivery** | Immediate | Immediate — same effect as IV |
| **Common errors** | Attempting too many failed sites before switching | Incorrect needle placement, failure to flush under pressure |

The clinical rule: if peripheral IV cannot be established quickly in a deteriorating patient, switch to IO rather than persisting with multiple failed attempts.

## ALS: shockable versus non-shockable

Both pathways of the ALS algorithm share the same structure: a 2-minute CPR cycle followed by rhythm recognition. The difference lies in what happens at the rhythm check.

| Feature | Shockable (VF / pVT) | Non-shockable (PEA / asystole) |
|---|---|---|
| **Immediate action** | Defibrillate | Continue CPR |
| **Adrenaline timing** | After 3rd shock, then every 3–5 min | Immediately, then every 3–5 min |
| **Amiodarone** | 300 mg after 3rd shock, 150 mg after 5th | Not indicated |
| **CPR quality** | Same — essential in both pathways | Same — essential in both pathways |
| **Reversible causes** | Search for 4H and 4T throughout | Search for 4H and 4T throughout |

After return of spontaneous circulation, reassess using ABCDE again. Post-resuscitation care begins at that point.

## Reversible causes: the 4H and 4T framework

Every ALS cycle should include an active search for treatable causes.

| 4H | 4T |
|---|---|
| Hypoxia | Tension pneumothorax |
| Hypovolaemia | Tamponade (cardiac) |
| Hypo- or hyperkalaemia (and other metabolic) | Toxins |
| Hypothermia | Thrombosis (pulmonary or coronary) |
