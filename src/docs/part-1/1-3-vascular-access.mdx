## Intravascular access

Adequate intravascular access is one of the main preconditions for adequate care of acutely ill patients. It ensures fast and direct administration of medication and fluid therapy, including blood products and other intravenous fluids. Intravascular access can be established via a peripheral vein, a central vein, or intraosseous access. In practice, one or more peripheral venous accesses are most often placed.

When peripheral venous access cannot be obtained, alternatives include intraosseous access in out-of-hospital conditions and central venous access in hospital conditions. This chapter focuses on techniques for establishing intravenous (IV) and intraosseous (IO) access, competencies that every medical doctor should have after graduation. As with every other skill, the technique requires practice and experience.

Intravenous access is established by placing a cannula in a peripheral vein. Several cannula sizes are available, each with its own standard colour coding. Flow through the cannula depends on its size, that is, its diameter, which is measured in Gauges (G) and written on the cannula packaging. The larger the Gauge, the smaller the catheter diameter and the slower the infusion flow. In an adult acutely ill patient, the standard is to place at least two large-bore IV cannulas with a diameter greater than 18 G, colloquially referred to as “green, white, or grey”.

If possible, the procedure should be explained in detail to the patient beforehand. Compliance, and therefore procedural success, is greater when this is done. It is also necessary to check all equipment before starting, for example whether the infusion system is fully primed with infusion fluid.

The procedure begins with palpation of the desired venepuncture site. Standard sites are branches of the dorsal venous arch, the basilic vein in the cubital region, the beginning of the cephalic vein on the hand, and the cephalic vein in the upper arm. Several facilitating manoeuvres can be used: applying a tourniquet proximal to the puncture site, instructing the patient to lower the hand below heart level and repeatedly clench the fist, rubbing the area with an alcohol antiseptic solution to improve vein visibility, and choosing a vein with a straight course over several centimetres. In practice, good palpation is often more important than visibility.

IV cannulation is performed in several steps. The skin 3 to 4 cm distal to the venepuncture site is stretched, particularly in elderly patients in whom reduced skin turgor may make placement more difficult, and the area is disinfected. The patient is warned to expect a puncture. The vein is punctured at an angle of 10° to 30° to the skin surface. After blood return appears in the flashback chamber, the cannula is advanced another millimetre into the vein lumen and then lowered so that it lies horizontally next to the patient’s skin.

The tourniquet is released, and the cannula is then advanced over the needle into the vein while the needle is simultaneously withdrawn. The distal part of the cannula is connected to the infusion system and flow is started. The patient may feel a sensation of coldness, but pain proximal to the insertion site or swelling of surrounding tissue may indicate a blown vein caused by improper needle insertion or a fragile vein with paravasal leakage of fluid. In that case, the IV cannula should be removed and the procedure repeated on another, more proximal vein. Once the cannula has been placed successfully, secure it to the skin using adhesive dressings.

## Intraosseous access

Circulatory shock and cardiac arrest significantly disturb a patient’s haemodynamics, and establishing intravenous access in such patients may present a major challenge. The procedure can also be difficult in certain groups, such as children, because subcutaneous fatty tissue can make the vein difficult to palpate and cannulate; elderly patients, who often have fragile atherosclerotic blood vessels; and intravenous drug users. According to guidelines for management of acutely ill patients, two failed attempts to establish intravenous access are an indication for establishing intraosseous access.

Typical sites for establishing intraosseous access in adults are:

- **Proximal humerus:** The patient’s arm is placed in adduction and internal rotation, and the hand is fixed under the lower back. The greater tubercle and surgical neck of the humerus are palpated. The ideal insertion site is on the greater tubercle, 1 cm above the surgical neck of the humerus.
- **Proximal tibia:** The patient’s leg is placed in extension. The ideal insertion point is 2 cm distal and 2 cm medial to the tibial tuberosity, on the bone surface.
- **Distal tibia:** The ideal insertion site is 3 cm proximal to the most prominent part of the medial malleolus. Palpation of the anterior and posterior borders of the tibia is necessary to ensure insertion on a flat part of the bone.

In children, there are special considerations when choosing an insertion site. The epiphyseal cartilage and bone growth zones must be taken into account. The distal femur can be considered an alternative to the previously mentioned sites.

Contraindications to intraosseous access placement are bone fracture, osteomyelitis, infection of the skin and soft tissues over the selected bone, the presence of an artificial joint or bone at that site, intraosseous access already placed in that bone within the previous 48 hours, and inability to identify the usual palpable landmarks.

The equipment consists of a drill or single-shot gun to which the needle is magnetically attached, a connection system for a standard infusion set, and a cuff for administering infusion solution under pressure. After the correct insertion site is chosen and the skin is prepared aseptically, the drill with the attached needle is oriented perpendicularly to the bone. The skin and subcutaneous tissue are pierced with the needle without drilling. Once the periosteum is reached, drilling can begin.

The aim is to pass through the compact bone and reach the spongious bone tissue, which is felt as a loss of resistance. Once cancellous bone is reached, drilling should stop. The stylet is removed from the catheter and the remaining catheter is fixed to the skin. Aspiration of bone marrow confirms correct positioning.

Before fluid administration, the fibrin mesh in the spongious bone should be disrupted, usually by applying a fluid bolus of 5–10 mL. The procedure is painful, so if the patient reacts to pain, 2–4 mL of 2% lidocaine should be administered before the bolus to anaesthetise the septal area. After the bolus, the intraosseous line can be connected to the infusion system, but infusion solution must be administered under pressure higher than atmospheric pressure, most often by using a cuff or pump.

All solutions and drugs that are regularly administered intravascularly, except cytostatics, can be given through intraosseous access. Possible complications are infection, compartment syndrome, and fluid extravasation. The line should be removed within 48 hours of placement, or earlier if possible.
