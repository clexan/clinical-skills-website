# 1.2 Essentials for ECG interpretation in medical emergencies

*Ivan Ševeljević, Ana Pišuljan, Matej Bura*

An electrocardiogram (ECG) is a recording of the heart’s electrical activity. Depending on the indication, it can be continuously monitored on an ECG monitor or recorded on graph paper in the form of a 12-lead ECG. The recording and interpretation of ECG is an indispensable part of the assessment of an acutely ill patient, and it is a basic skill that every doctor must acquire. It is important to point out that the ECG shows the electrical activity of the heart but not the mechanical activity of the heart (heart contraction). Hence, it is necessary to palpate the pulse for the assessment if electrical activity ultimately produces a mechanical one.

## 1.2.1 The conduction system of the heart and the normal cardiac cycle on the ECG

In order for the heart to perform its basic role as a pump, two steps are necessary:

1. Generation and conduction of the electrical signal through the heart.
2. Cardiac contraction and relaxation.

The first step requires the so-called conductive heart fibres and the second the contractile heart fibres. The conductive heart fibres represent the part of the heart specialised for initiating and efficiently conducting the electrical impulse to the cells that are capable of contraction. In a healthy patient, the electrical signal (in the form of an action potential) originates in the SA node (“cardiac pacemaker”), from where it spreads through the atria to the AV node. The signal is briefly “slowed down” while passing through the AV node and then quickly transmitted through the bundle of His, its right and left branches, and the network of Purkinje fibres towards the contractile ventricular fibres. The mentioned slowing of the signal in the AV node allows the atria to contract first during the cardiac cycle and expel the blood towards the ventricles, after which the filled ventricles contract.

In order to better understand the ECG recording, it is necessary to note that each action potential consists of depolarisation and repolarisation, whereby depolarisation stimulates cardiac contraction (systole), while repolarisation enables cardiac relaxation (and filling of chambers with blood during diastole).

“Translation” of the mentioned process into one cardiac cycle on the ECG (Figure 1): atrial depolarisation is represented by the P wave, conduction through the atria and the AV node is represented by the PR interval (from the beginning of the P wave to the beginning of the QRS complex; normally 0.12–0.20 s), where deceleration through the AV node is the PR segment (from the end of P to the beginning of the QRS), ventricular depolarisation is represented by the QRS complex (normally up to 0.12 s), while ventricular repolarisation is represented by the T wave.

## 1.2.2 ECG monitoring

ECG monitoring is used for continuous monitoring of the heart’s electrical activity, which enables the assessment of rhythm and rate in real time. Using ECG monitoring, it is not possible to analyse the ST junction and the duration of individual segments (PR, QT, QRS). If such an analysis is necessary, it is indicated to record a 12-lead ECG.

Indications for patient monitoring are rhythms during cardiac arrest and other significant arrhythmias, chest pain, cardiac decompensation, collapse/syncope, shock/hypotension, palpitations, and any other condition in which the patient is acutely endangered (e.g., sepsis, polytrauma, etc.).

Types of monitoring are:

1. **3-lead monitoring:** it is recorded using electrodes attached according to the “traffic light principle” (red electrode above the right clavicle, yellow above the left clavicle, green in the projection of the apex of the heart). This enables the monitoring of leads I, II, and III.
2. **Monitoring using self-adhesive electrodes or defibrillator pads:** it is recorded by placing one electrode/pad under the right clavicle and the other in the middle axillary line to the left (anteroposterior placement is also possible). This is also the method of choice in the arrest and post-resuscitation period. It enables only monitoring of lead II.

In practice, we use the following six questions for quick ECG analysis in medical emergencies:

1. **Is there any electrical activity?** If there is no electrical activity, the rhythm is asystole. If electrical activity is present but disorganised (“bizarre”, without identifiable QRS complexes), the rhythm is ventricular fibrillation (VF). Asystole and VF are rhythms incompatible with life because they cannot produce a pulse and cardiopulmonary resuscitation (CPR) should be started immediately. If we see those while monitoring a living patient (patient with a pulse), it is probably artefacts and/or wrong position/connection of the electrodes. If organised electrical activity is seen, the rhythm is further analysed according to questions 2–6. It is important to point out the case in which electrical activity is seen, but the patient has no pulse, which is called pulseless electrical activity (PEA). It does not require further analysis of the rhythm, and it is extremely important to start CPR without delay.
2. **What is the ventricular (QRS) rate?** The normal heart rate is 60–100/min. A rate slower than 60/min is called bradycardia, and a rate faster than 100/min is called tachycardia. The rate reading is usually presented on the monitor, but it can also be calculated from a longer printout on graph paper.
3. **Is the QRS rhythm regular or irregular?** We compare the lengths of the R–R intervals, and we can also use sound signals from the monitor. A regular rhythm with intermittent irregularities (i.e., premature complexes) most often indicates extrasystoles (they can be of atrial or ventricular origin). If the rhythm is completely irregular (“irregularly irregular”) and all QRS complexes are morphologically equal, it is most likely atrial fibrillation (AF).
4. **Is the QRS complex normal or prolonged?** QRS complexes normally last up to 0.12 s (up to three “small squares” on the printout). A normal/narrow QRS complex indicates that the rhythm is “supraventricular” (above the bifurcation of the bundle of His). An extended QRS (prolonged duration) indicates that the rhythm originates from the ventricle (e.g., ventricular tachycardia) or might be supraventricular but transmitted with aberrant conduction (branch block, accessory pathway conduction, etc.).
5. **Is atrial activity present?** It is necessary to look for P waves: sinus P waves are most often clearly visible in lead II as upright waves. The absence of atrial activity, i.e., its disorganisation, indicates AF. Regular atrial activity in the form of fast “saw-tooth” waves indicates atrial undulation. When possible, interpretation of atrial activity should be facilitated (especially in fast rhythms) by recording a 12-lead ECG and analysing all leads (usually, atrial activity is easiest to determine in V1).
6. **How does atrial activity relate to ventricular activity?** Does a QRS follow each P wave, and is there a P wave before each QRS? What is the length of the PR interval?

The most precise answer is obtained by analysing a longer printout. Conduction between atria and ventricles is normal if PR < 0.20 s. Each P wave is followed by a QRS complex, and the P wave is located before each QRS complex. First-degree AV block is characterised by prolongation of PR (> 0.20 s). Second-degree AV block Mobitz Type I (Wenckebach AV block) is characterised by a progressive prolongation of the PR interval until there is no QRS, after which the cycle starts from the beginning. Second-degree AV block Mobitz Type II is characterised by a constant PR interval with occasional individual QRS absences. In the third-degree AV block (complete AV block, “AV dissociation”), there is no conduction through the AV node, so the frequencies of the atria and ventricles are independent of each other (the ratio of P and QRS waves is irregular and unrelated).

## 1.2.3 Interpretation of 12-lead ECG

A more detailed analysis of the heart’s electrical activity is not possible without a 12-lead ECG, and there are numerous causes of changes in the ECG (ischaemia, pericarditis, arrhythmias, pulmonary embolism, electrolyte disorders, drugs, implanted pacemaker, etc.). A standard 12-lead ECG is usually recorded by placing 10 electrodes, which results in a recording of six peripheral (I, II, III, aVR, aVL, aVF) and six precordial leads (V1–V6). Peripheral leads are projected in the vertical plane and the precordial leads are projected in the horizontal plane, which enables a three-dimensional analysis (see Figure 7). In addition to the standard ones, recording can be supplemented by placing additional leads (Figure 2), for example, posterior leads (to confirm posterior myocardial infarction) or right leads (to confirm right ventricular infarction).

The basic analysis consists of four parts:

1. **Rhythm** — we determine it using the “six questions for rhythm analysis” discussed earlier. Sinus rhythm (SR) is a normal rhythm and is the result of regular activity of the SA node and normal conduction of the electrical signal to the contractile heart muscles (P waves are upright in leads I and II, each QRS is preceded by P waves, the PR interval is constant). There are many irregular rhythms (AF, VT, heart blocks, arrhythmias with bundle branch blocks, and many others), but we will not go into a more detailed analysis here.
2. **Rate** — we can use the “300 method”. We do this by finding the R wave that coincides with the thick line on the ECG recording and, depending on which following thick line the R wave falls on, we calculate the rate. The first line represents 300, the second 150, the third 100, the fourth 75, the fifth 60, and the sixth 50/min. This rule only applies to regular rhythms. For irregular and slow rhythms, take 25 cm of recording paper (represents 10 s), count the QRS complexes, and multiply the number by 6 to get a heart rate expressed as beats/min.
3. **Electrical axis** — represents the direction of depolarisation of the ventricles and is normally between −30° and +90°. Axis deviation can indicate pathology (left axis deviation is from −30° to −90°, right axis deviation is from +90° to +180°, and extreme axis deviation is from +180° to +270°). The simplest method is to determine the axis by quadrants. We observe the QRS complexes in leads I and aVF and determine whether they are predominantly positive or negative. A positive QRS in lead I means that the electrical axis is going to the left, and a negative QRS means that the axis is going to the right. Likewise, a positive QRS in aVF means that the axis is going down, and a negative QRS means that the axis is going up. By combining these values in the coordinate system, we determine the electrical axis of the heart. In the case of a positive QRS in lead I and a negative QRS in lead aVF, to confirm whether it is a left axis deviation or a physiological axis, look at lead II — a negative QRS in the same lead indicates axis deviation.
4. **Analysis of the ST segment and T wave** — normally, the ST segment is in the isoelectric line (the J point — the beginning of the ST segment is compared with the TP interval, which represents the isoelectric line) (Figure 1), and the T waves are positive (except in aVR and V1). Changes that suggest myocardial ischaemia are inverted (negative) T waves (Figure 6) and ST-segment depression. ST-segment elevation can be a sign of acute myocardial infarction and some other conditions (pericarditis, vasospastic angina, benign early repolarisation, left bundle branch block, etc.). It is necessary to look at all 12 leads and analyse these segments, and it is also useful to monitor the dynamics of these changes over time by periodically repeating the ECG recording.

## 1.2.4 ST-segment-elevation myocardial infarction (STEMI) on ECG

ECG criteria for the diagnosis of STEMI are ST elevation in two or more contiguous leads. Contiguous leads are leads that “look at” the same myocardial wall, i.e., the vascularisation area of the same coronary artery (Figure 7 and Figure 8), with the elevation having to be ≥ 1 mm (0.1 mV, “one small square”), except in V2 and V3 where more strict criteria apply, e.g., ≥ 2.5 mm for men < 40 years, ≥ 2.0 mm for men > 40 years, and ≥ 1.5 mm for women. We determine the location of the infarction according to the leads in which the ST elevation is present. In patients with inferior STEMI (ST elevation in leads II, III, aVF), it is recommended to record the right leads (V3R–V6R) for possible confirmation of right ventricular infarction (Figure 9B).

ST elevation is often “paired” with a reciprocal ST depression in the “opposite” leads (Figure 9A, Figure 9B, and Figure 9C). Thus, ST depression in V1–V3 may suggest a “mirror image” of posterior STEMI, so it is necessary to record the posterior leads (V7–V9). ST elevation ≥ 0.5 mm is sufficient to confirm posterior STEMI (Figure 8). New-onset left bundle branch block (LBBB) in the clinical presentation of acute coronary syndrome (ACS) is considered equivalent to STEMI. ST depression ≥ 1 mm in eight or more leads followed by ST elevation in aVR and/or V1 suggests obstruction of the left coronary artery (“left main”) or severe “triple-vessel coronary artery disease/ischaemia” (Figure 10).

Figure 7 represents the largest coronary arteries: the left anterior descending artery (LAD), the circumflex branch of the left coronary artery (LCx), and the right coronary artery (RCA). Contiguous leads “look at” the same wall, i.e., the vascularisation area of the same coronary artery, and the location of the infarction is determined according to the leads in which the ST elevation is present (notice how the peripheral leads are projected in the vertical plane, and the precordial leads in the horizontal plane, which enables 3D analysis). We distinguish the following:

- **Septal STEMI (LAD occlusion):** ST elevation in V1–V2.
- **Anterior STEMI (LAD occlusion):** ST elevation in V3–V4.
- **Lateral STEMI (LCx occlusion):** ST elevation in V5, V6, I, aVL.
- **Posterior STEMI (RCA or LCx occlusion):** ST elevation in V7–V9, reciprocal ST depression in V1–V3.
- **Inferior STEMI (RCA occlusion):** ST elevation in II, III, aVF, reciprocal ST elevation in lateral leads (right leads V3R–V6R should also be recorded to confirm/exclude right ventricular infarction).
- **Combinations of the previously mentioned** are also possible, e.g., anteroseptolateral STEMI (also called “extensive anterior infarction”, ST elevation in V1–V6, I + aVL) and inferoposterior STEMI (ST elevation in II, III, aVF, and V7–V9, reciprocal ST depression in V1–V3).
