This review keeps Part 7 focused on the first practical decisions: make the scene safe, control major bleeding early, complete the trauma survey efficiently, and recognise which burn patterns need rapid escalation.

## Trauma approach

- Stick to the **ABCDE** approach.
- **Trauma patients require a modified protocol** to enable efficient assessment and treatment. Learn the trauma algorithm well and use formal trauma training where possible.
- Assess the **scene first**. It may be dangerous, and it may also provide essential clues about the likely injuries.

## Bleeding control and rapid survey

- If you see **significant bleeding**, try to stop it immediately. In practice, this often means direct pressure to the wound.
- Perform a **rapid trauma survey** whenever the mechanism of injury is significant or the primary assessment is abnormal.
- **Immobilise the patient properly.** Spinal and pelvic stabilisation are not minor details; they are part of definitive early care.

## Burns priorities

- In a burned patient, always consider the **mechanism of injury** and possible **associated trauma**. Not all burns carry the same risk. Special concern is required for:
  - airway or inhalation burns,
  - chemical burns,
  - electrical burns,
  - burns in anatomically critical areas,
  - large TBSA burns.
- Airway and lung burns may progress to **complete airway obstruction** and/or **pulmonary oedema**. Early intubation is often required. If you are not trained to intubate, give oxygen and transport the patient rapidly to trained personnel.
- Estimate **TBSA**, calculate the **Parkland formula** if time allows, establish **IV access** preferably through unburned skin, begin **fluid resuscitation**, and provide **adequate analgesia**.
