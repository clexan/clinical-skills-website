## Introduction

Large burns are challenging and potentially life-threatening injuries commonly encountered in emergency medicine. Early management has a major effect on outcome.

Burns are more common in men than in women, and flame and scald injuries are the most common causes. Children under 5 years of age are at particularly high risk. Approximately 10% of burned patients require hospital admission, and about 10–15% of admitted patients require treatment in specialised burn centres.

The incidence of severe burns in Europe is reported to be about **0.2–2.9 per 10,000**.

## Risk Factors for Severe Complications and Poor Outcome

The following burn patterns or patient factors are associated with worse outcomes:

- second-degree burns involving **more than 10% TBSA** in patients **under 10** or **over 50 years** of age,
- second-degree burns involving **more than 20% TBSA**, 
- third-degree burns involving **more than 5% TBSA**, 
- burns involving the **face, hands, feet, genitalia, perineum, or major joints**, 
- **chemical burns**, 
- **electrical burns**, including lightning injury,
- burns associated with **other trauma**, 
- important pre-existing conditions such as **heart failure, diabetes, immunosuppression, or pregnancy**.

Patients with one or more of these features benefit from management in a specialised burn unit.

## Initial Patient Assessment and Management: ABCDE + SAMPLE

Before patient contact:

- ensure rescuer and patient safety,
- identify hazards such as electricity, gas, traffic, or ongoing fire,
- stop the burning process if still ongoing.

Every burned patient should also be managed as a trauma patient because burns may be associated with head trauma, inhalation injury, falls, or other trauma mechanisms.

If the patient does not respond, call for help, open the airway, and check breathing and pulse for 10 seconds according to advanced life support principles.

If the patient is alive, proceed with **ABCDEF**.

### A (Airway), B (Breathing), and Cervical Spine Control

Check whether the airway is patent and open it if obstructed. Apply cervical spine protection if trauma is suspected.

Assess breathing using a **look, listen, and feel** approach:

- respiratory rate and effort,
- added breath sounds,
- symmetry of chest movement,
- subcutaneous emphysema,
- tracheal deviation,
- jugular venous distention,
- cyanosis,
- pulse oximetry.

Apply **high-flow oxygen** via a non-rebreathing reservoir mask.

Signs suggesting inhalation injury include:

- deep burns of the lower face or neck,
- burned nasal hairs,
- blisters or oedema of the lips or tongue,
- history of fire in a closed space,
- hoarseness,
- carbonaceous sputum,
- hypoxia,
- stridor,
- wheezing,
- respiratory distress,
- use of accessory muscles.

These findings may indicate airway or lung inhalation injury and are an indication to strongly consider **early tracheal intubation with a cuffed endotracheal tube**, ideally by an experienced clinician.

Prepare a range of endotracheal tube sizes because airway oedema may require a smaller tube. Secure the tube carefully once placed.

Remember that airway oedema may worsen after fluid resuscitation and become more obvious during transport.

Always consider **carbon monoxide** or **cyanide poisoning** in a patient exposed to fire.

### C (Circulation)

Any burn involving **more than 10% TBSA in children** or **more than 15% TBSA in adults** requires careful intravenous fluid resuscitation to reduce the risk of hypovolaemic shock.

Assess:

- heart rate,
- blood pressure,
- capillary refill time,
- skin colour,
- urine output.

Reassess frequently, typically every **3–5 minutes** in unstable patients.

Insert **two large-bore intravenous cannulas** where possible, ideally in unburned skin. If this is not possible, use **intraosseous access**.

Hypotension immediately after the burn is unusual and should prompt a search for another cause such as **bleeding**.

Use continuous **ECG monitoring**, especially after **electrical injury** or in **hypothermia**.

Check peripheral pulses in all limbs because circumferential burns can lead to **compartment syndrome**.

Insert a urinary catheter to monitor hourly urine output. Target values are:

- **adults:** > 1 mL/kg/h
- **children:** > 2 mL/kg/h

After the initial assessment, provide adequate analgesia. For example, **morphine 4–6 mg IV** may be given initially, followed by **1–2 mg IV every 10 minutes** until adequate analgesia is achieved.

### D (Disability)

Assess:

- AVPU or Glasgow Coma Scale,
- blood glucose,
- pupil size and symmetry,
- pupil reactivity.

Give special attention to toxicological causes of altered consciousness if carbon monoxide or cyanide poisoning is suspected.

### E (Exposure and Environmental Control)

Cooling may help limit thermal injury and reduce pain, but it must be used cautiously.

- Cool burned areas with **clean water at about 12–15 °C** for **2–3 minutes**.
- Do not use excessively cold water, as this may worsen hypothermia.
- Irrigate chemical burns thoroughly.
- Remove wet or dirty clothing, but do not pull away burned clothing that is stuck to the skin.
- Remove all jewellery.

Estimate the percentage of **total body surface area (TBSA)** burned.

- Use the **rule of nines** where appropriate.
- If burns are patchy, the patient’s own hand represents roughly **1% TBSA**.

Cover burned areas with:

- sterile gauze soaked in sterile saline, or
- another clean commercially available burn covering.

Do **not** apply ointments, powders, or creams in the acute setting.

Watch for **hypothermia**. Once the patient has been assessed, cover them with a clean blanket, warm the vehicle, and use warmed IV fluids.

Check the patient’s tetanus immunisation status.

### F (Early Fluid Therapy)

For significant burns:

- **Adults with burns > 20% TBSA:** begin with **500 mL** of warmed intravenous balanced crystalloid in the first hour.
- **Children with burns > 10% TBSA:** begin with **50–100 mL** of warmed intravenous balanced crystalloid, depending on body weight.

Use a **balanced crystalloid**, ideally:

- **Ringer lactate**, or
- **Ringer acetate**,

prewarmed to body temperature.

Record the volume of fluid already given. Excessive fluid resuscitation can worsen tissue oedema, delay wound healing, and aggravate airway swelling.

Once the patient reaches hospital, ongoing fluid replacement during the first 24 hours is commonly estimated using the **Parkland formula**:

The total calculated volume is then split: half in the first 8 hours after injury, and the remainder over the next 16 hours.

Inform the receiving hospital early, because severe burns require major resources and multidisciplinary care.
