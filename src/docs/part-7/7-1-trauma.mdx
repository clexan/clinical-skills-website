## Introduction

Polytrauma, together with acute coronary syndrome and stroke, is a time-dependent condition. Recognition of life-threatening injuries, initial stabilisation, and definitive care are directly linked to survival during the so-called golden period, from the moment of injury to definitive treatment.

In prehospital trauma care, the goal is to spend as little time as possible at the scene, ideally about 10 minutes, while identifying and managing immediately life-threatening conditions, immobilising the patient, and transporting them to the appropriate trauma centre.

Two approaches are commonly used in Europe:

- **International Trauma Life Support (ITLS)** for prehospital trauma care.
- **European Trauma Course (ETC)** for multidisciplinary in-hospital trauma care.

The trauma examination itself remains fundamentally the same.

## Management of Trauma Patients

We use the ITLS algorithm for the structured assessment of trauma patients:

- **ITLS primary survey:** scene size-up, initial assessment, and rapid trauma survey or focused survey.
- **ITLS ongoing exam:** monitoring for changes in the patient’s condition.
- **ITLS secondary survey:** full examination and identification of all injuries.

## Scene Size-Up

Before approaching the patient, assess the scene and remove or control hazards. If the scene is unsafe, for example because of electrical risk, explosion, fire, building collapse, or road traffic, it must first be secured by other emergency services or the patient must be removed without endangering the team.

Personal protective equipment is mandatory because of possible exposure to blood or other potentially infectious material. Gloves should be changed when examining multiple casualties.

Scene size-up also includes assessment of:

- mechanism of injury,
- number of victims,
- required equipment,
- need for additional medical assistance.

As a practical rule, one ambulance team and one vehicle are needed for each severely injured patient.

## Mechanism of Injury

The mechanism of injury may be:

- **generalised**, when major forces act on the whole body and severe internal injury is likely, or
- **focused**, when the force acts on one region only.

Significant mechanisms of injury include:

- ejection from a vehicle,
- falls from more than 4.5 metres,
- vehicle rollover,
- high-speed vehicle collision,
- pedestrian-versus-car collision,
- motorcycle accident.

In these situations, assume serious injury even if the external findings appear limited.

A motor vehicle collision implies three sequential collisions:

1. vehicle collision,
2. body collision,
3. organ collision.

Additional injury may be caused by unsecured objects acting as projectiles or by secondary impact with other objects or vehicles. Vehicle safety systems may also contribute to injury of the abdomen, cervical and lumbar spine, shoulders, and clavicles.

### Basic Mechanisms of Injury

**Blunt injuries** include:

- sudden forward deceleration,
- sudden vertical deceleration,
- energy transfer from blunt impact.

**Penetrating injuries** include:

- missiles,
- knives,
- falls onto a fixed object.

### Potential Injuries by Trauma Mechanism

**Frontal impact** may cause:

- cervical spine fracture,
- flail chest,
- myocardial contusion,
- pneumothorax,
- aortic disruption,
- liver or spleen laceration,
- posterior hip dislocation,
- knee dislocation.

**Lateral impact** may cause:

- cervical spine fracture,
- flail chest,
- pneumothorax,
- aortic disruption,
- diaphragmatic rupture,
- laceration of the spleen, liver, or kidney,
- pelvic fracture.

**Rear impact** may cause:

- cervical spine injury.

**Pedestrian versus car** may cause:

- head injury,
- abdominal visceral injury,
- fractures of the lower extremities and pelvis,
- spinal fracture.

## Initial Assessment

The team leader performs the primary ITLS assessment and should not interrupt it unless there is cardiopulmonary arrest, airway obstruction, uncontrolled external bleeding, or a new danger at the scene.

In a critically injured patient, the goal is to complete the primary examination within 2 minutes and keep scene time below 10 minutes.

Interventions are delegated using the **“fix it”** principle. Major external bleeding changes the usual ABC priority to **C-ABC**.

Before direct examination, assess the general impression, obvious deformities, major breathing problems, and whether the patient belongs to a higher-risk group such as children, older adults, or pregnant patients.

The level of consciousness is rapidly assessed using **AVPU**:

- **A – Alert:** awake, oriented, obeys commands.
- **V – Verbal:** responds to verbal stimulus.
- **P – Pain:** responds to painful stimulus.
- **U – Unresponsive:** no response to any stimulus.

If the patient is **P** or **U**, airway compromise is likely.

### A (Airway)

If the patient responds, the airway is open.

If consciousness is impaired, listen for abnormal airway sounds:

- **Snoring** suggests partial airway obstruction and requires airway opening manoeuvres or placement of an oropharyngeal or nasopharyngeal airway.
- **Gurgling** suggests liquid in the mouth and requires suction.
- The **see-saw phenomenon** with asynchronous chest and abdominal movement suggests complete airway obstruction.

Manual cervical spine stabilisation is maintained from the start.

### B (Breathing)

Assess:

- respiratory rate,
- symmetry of chest rise,
- use of accessory muscles,
- pulse oximetry.

Give high-flow oxygen at **12–15 L/min** through a reservoir mask to every critically injured patient.

If the patient is apnoeic, provide bag-mask ventilation with a self-inflating bag. Assist ventilation if respiratory rate or tidal volume is inadequate.

### C (Circulation)

Assess:

- pulse rate, rhythm, and strength,
- skin colour, temperature, and sweating,
- capillary refill time,
- visible external bleeding.

In early shock, the patient is typically pale, cold, and sweaty, with a rapid weak pulse and prolonged capillary refill time.

Attempt IV access, but do not delay life-saving interventions or transport for vascular access.

### D (Disability)

Assess consciousness initially with AVPU. If the patient has altered consciousness, perform a more detailed neurological examination with:

- Glasgow Coma Scale,
- pupil size and symmetry,
- pupil reactivity to light.

Measure blood glucose in patients with impaired consciousness or known diabetes.

### E (Exposure)

Look for clues in the environment and on the patient, such as:

- needles,
- pills,
- hidden wounds,
- body temperature abnormalities.

### SAMPLE

Because trauma patients may lose consciousness before reaching hospital, the prehospital team may be the only team able to obtain history.

- **S:** signs and symptoms
- **A:** allergies
- **M:** medications
- **P:** past medical history
- **L:** last oral intake
- **E:** events preceding the incident

If the patient is unconscious, obtain heteroanamnesis from family or witnesses.

## Rapid Trauma Survey

Rapid trauma survey is a systematic **head-to-toe** examination used in patients with a generalised mechanism of injury or any abnormality on the initial assessment. In patients with isolated or minor trauma, a focused examination of the affected region may be sufficient.

Each region is examined by **inspection first** and then **palpation**. The chest may also require auscultation and percussion.

### Regions to Examine

#### Head

Assess for:

- visible injuries,
- haematoma,
- deformity,
- tenderness,
- crepitation,
- instability.

#### Neck

Assess for:

- visible injuries,
- jugular venous distention,
- tenderness,
- tracheal deviation,
- subcutaneous emphysema.

Maintain cervical spine immobilisation.

#### Chest

Assess for:

- visible injuries,
- unstable segments,
- open wounds,
- tenderness,
- crepitation,
- instability,
- symmetry of breath sounds,
- heart sounds.

Use percussion if auscultatory findings are unequal.

#### Abdomen

Assess for:

- visible injuries,
- seat-belt marks,
- tenderness,
- rigidity,
- distension.

#### Pelvis

Assess for:

- visible injury,
- deformity,
- tenderness,
- crepitation,
- instability.

If the pelvis is unstable, apply a pelvic splint.

#### Extremities

Assess for:

- visible injuries,
- deformity,
- tenderness,
- crepitation,
- instability,
- bleeding,
- distal pulses,
- motor function,
- sensation.

#### Posterior Surface

Assess for:

- visible injuries,
- tenderness,
- crepitation,
- instability.

Use spinal motion restriction and examine the back with a log roll unless contraindicated.

### Important Practical Points

Do not remove foreign bodies unless they obstruct the airway or are located in the cheek. Stabilise them in place.

Do not return eviscerated abdominal contents to the abdomen. Cover them with sterile gauze soaked in isotonic saline and then with an impermeable cover.

Amputated parts should be placed in a dry bag, which is then placed in a second bag containing ice and water. The amputated part must not be in direct contact with ice or water.

Palpate the spine for pain, deformity, and paravertebral muscle tension. Palpate the abdomen in four quadrants. If abdominal pain is present, suspect intra-abdominal injury and risk of shock.

Examine the pelvis gently. If it is already painful or unstable, stop further pelvic testing and apply a pelvic splint.

When examining the extremities, check for distal pulses below any suspected fracture.

In patients with altered consciousness, facial grimacing may be the only sign of pain during palpation.

Auscultate the chest bilaterally in the midaxillary line. If breath sounds are unequal, percussion may help distinguish between **pneumothorax** and **haemothorax**.

Examine the back by **log roll** unless contraindicated. Contraindications include:

- unstable pelvis,
- bilateral femoral fractures,
- foreign body in the chest or abdomen,
- obvious signs of spinal injury.

In such cases, use a **scoop stretcher** instead.

In patients at risk of shock, obtain **two large-bore IV lines** and start fluid replacement. In suspected internal bleeding, the target systolic blood pressure is around **90 mmHg**.

Excessive fluid resuscitation may worsen bleeding, dilute clotting factors, and aggravate hypothermia.

## Immobilisation

### Cervical Spine Immobilisation with a Cervical Collar

Spinal immobilisation begins with manual stabilisation of the head and neck and continues with placement of a cervical collar and other immobilisation equipment.

Before placing the collar, examine the entire neck, because inspection and palpation become difficult afterwards.

Choose the correct collar size by measuring the distance between:

- the upper border of the trapezius where it meets the side of the neck, and
- the point where a line dropped from the chin meets the neck.

Transfer that measurement to the collar and adjust it before application.

### Spinal Immobilisation with a Long Backboard (Log Roll)

Complete immobilisation requires:

- cervical collar,
- long backboard,
- lateral head stabilisers,
- two adhesive headbands,
- straps,
- ideally three or four team members.

The backboard is placed parallel to the patient. While one person maintains in-line manual stabilisation, the others support the shoulders, hips, and legs and roll the patient as one unit.

Once the patient is on the board, secure them in this order:

1. strap under the arms and above the chest,
2. strap across the pelvis,
3. strap above the knees,
4. figure-of-eight strap around the feet,
5. lateral head stabilisers and headbands.

This order matters because an incompletely secured patient may shift if vomiting occurs and side-turning becomes necessary.

A **vacuum mattress** is an increasingly preferred alternative for longer transport.

## ETC Approach to Trauma Patients

The European Trauma Course was developed for multidisciplinary in-hospital trauma care.

On arrival in the emergency department, perform a rapid **5-second round**:

- Are there signs of airway obstruction?
- Is there massive external bleeding?
- Are there signs of arrest?

After immediate threats are excluded, the senior clinician gathers information, performs a rapid trauma survey and FAST ultrasound, and decides on further laboratory and imaging tests.

## Specific Injuries

### Chest Injuries

The thorax protects the lungs, heart, great vessels, liver, spleen, transverse colon, and spinal cord. Chest trauma can therefore be rapidly life-threatening.

Chest injuries may be:

- **blunt (closed)**,
- **open (penetrating)**.

The main symptoms are **dyspnoea** and **chest pain**. Tenderness, instability, and crepitation may be found on palpation. Use pulse oximetry, capnography, ECG monitoring, oxygen therapy, and IV access as needed.

Multiple rib fractures may produce respiratory compromise and are commonly associated with pulmonary contusion.

#### Tension Pneumothorax

Tension pneumothorax is progressive air accumulation in the pleural space without exit, producing a one-way valve effect. Intrathoracic pressure rises, mediastinal structures shift, venous return falls, cardiac output drops, and obstructive shock develops.

Typical findings include:

- dyspnoea,
- tachypnoea,
- possible tracheal deviation,
- distended neck veins,
- reduced chest movement on the affected side,
- absent or reduced breath sounds,
- hyperresonance on percussion.

Needle decompression is a temporary life-saving intervention. It may be performed in:

- the **second intercostal space, midclavicular line**, or
- the **fourth or fifth intercostal space, anterior or midaxillary line**.

Definitive treatment is thoracic drainage in hospital.

#### Open Pneumothorax

Open pneumothorax occurs when an open chest wound allows air to enter the pleural space. If the wound is wider than about two-thirds of the tracheal diameter, air preferentially enters through the wound.

Cover the wound with an **occlusive dressing taped on three sides**, creating a flutter valve.

#### Massive Haemothorax

Massive haemothorax may follow blunt or penetrating trauma. It is defined as at least **1,500 mL** of blood in the pleural cavity.

Thoracotomy is indicated when:

- blood loss exceeds **1,500 mL** or about one-third of total blood volume, or
- drainage exceeds **200 mL/hour** over **2–4 hours**.

#### Flail Chest

Flail chest is caused by fracture of **three or more adjacent ribs in two or more places**, creating an unstable segment with paradoxical movement.

The most important associated pathology is **lung contusion**, with risk of respiratory insufficiency, atelectasis, and pneumothorax.

Management includes:

- stabilisation,
- adequate analgesia,
- intubation and assisted ventilation when required.

#### Cardiac Tamponade

Pericardial tamponade usually follows penetrating chest trauma. Rapid accumulation of blood in the pericardium compresses the ventricles, impairs filling, reduces cardiac output, and causes obstructive shock.

**Beck’s triad** consists of:

- hypotension,
- jugular venous distention,
- muffled heart sounds.

Without ultrasound, tamponade may be difficult to distinguish clinically from tension pneumothorax.

Treatment is emergency **pericardiocentesis with drainage** by an experienced operator.

#### Diaphragmatic Rupture

Diaphragmatic rupture is uncommon but occurs in a minority of polytrauma patients, most often after major traffic trauma. It is more common on the **left side**.

Blunt trauma may produce large lacerations with herniation of abdominal organs into the thorax. Penetrating injuries may create smaller defects that present later.

Suspect it in severe blunt thoracic trauma, especially after compression or rapid deceleration.

### Abdominal Injuries

Abdominal trauma is dangerous because of the high risk of **haemorrhagic shock**. It may be difficult to diagnose even in hospital, so prehospital care should emphasise mechanism of injury, rapid recognition, and minimal scene time.

Common findings include:

- abdominal pain,
- tenderness,
- abdominal wall tension,
- bruising,
- seat-belt marks.

A horizontal seat-belt mark strongly suggests mesenteric or bowel injury.

#### Blunt Abdominal Injuries

Blunt abdominal trauma is often caused by:

- traffic accidents,
- falls,
- bicycle handlebar injury,
- sports trauma,
- assault.

Possible consequences include:

- intra-abdominal bleeding,
- hollow-organ injury,
- retroperitoneal injury.

Common bleeding sources are:

- spleen,
- liver,
- mesenteric vessels.

Jejunal or terminal ileal rupture may lead to peritonitis. Retroperitoneal haematoma may result from renal rupture. Pancreatic and duodenal injuries may occur after compression against the spine.

#### Penetrating Abdominal Injuries

Penetrating abdominal injuries are usually caused by stabbing or gunshot wounds. They may injure the abdominal wall vessels and internal organs such as the liver, spleen, stomach, intestines, and mesentery.

If bowel protrudes, do not push it back into the abdomen. Cover it with warm saline-soaked gauze.

### Pelvic Injuries

Pelvic injuries usually reflect **high-energy trauma** and may cause severe bleeding and rapid haemorrhagic shock. They are often associated with injuries to the chest or abdomen.

Pelvic stabilisation with a pelvic splint has four main goals:

- prevent further tissue damage,
- reduce pelvic volume,
- minimise bleeding from bone and soft tissue,
- reduce pain.

Use a pelvic splint in:

- a haemodynamically unstable patient with a mechanically unstable pelvis,
- a haemodynamically unstable patient with suspected pelvic fracture,
- a haemodynamically stable patient with a mechanically unstable pelvis.

Check the **4 Ps** before and after splint application:

- pain,
- pockets,
- penis,
- pulse.

The splint is placed at the level of the **greater trochanters**.
