# 6.2 Management of electrolyte disorders

*Lara Valenčić Seršić, Berislav Barbalić*

Electrolytes are electrically charged substances commonly found in blood and other bodily fluids. They are essential for multiple physiological processes, including maintenance of membrane potential and generation and propagation of action potentials in nerve and muscle cells. The most clinically important electrolytes are sodium, potassium, and chloride, along with magnesium, calcium, and phosphates.

Electrolyte disorders are usually classified according to a change in plasma concentration and may be increased (**hyper-**) or decreased (**hypo-**). They can lead to potentially life-threatening conditions, so early recognition and treatment are important.

## 6.2.1 Hypokalaemia and hyperkalaemia

Potassium is vital for normal body function. Changes in its concentration can lead to the sudden onset of potentially life-threatening conditions.

Potassium is primarily an intracellular positively charged ion (**cation**). The sodium–potassium pump (`Na+/K+-ATPase`) in the membrane of all cells is responsible for maintaining potassium homeostasis. Through the activity of this enzyme, three sodium ions are transported out of the cell in exchange for two potassium ions, thereby maintaining the normal resting membrane potential.

When fully balanced, the normal intracellular potassium concentration is approximately `130–150 mmol/L`, while plasma concentration usually varies between `3.5 and 5.0 mmol/L`, depending on laboratory reference values.

The kidneys eliminate roughly 90% of potassium, with the remainder secreted through the digestive tract and other body fluids such as sweat and saliva. In the kidneys, potassium is reabsorbed in the proximal convoluted tubule and the ascending limb of the loop of Henle, while secretion occurs in the distal convoluted tubule.

Changes in serum pH, insulin, medications such as diuretics, and various hormones can all alter extracellular potassium concentration, resulting in either hyperkalaemia or hypokalaemia. Potassium disorders are particularly dangerous because of their effect on myocardial cells, where they can trigger arrhythmias that may progress to malignant arrhythmias and cardiac arrest. For that reason, potassium disorders must be recognised clinically even before laboratory results are available.

### Hyperkalaemia

Hyperkalaemia is defined as elevation of potassium concentration above its upper limit, usually above `5.0–5.5 mmol/L`. It can be classified as mild, moderate, or severe according to plasma concentration.

- **Mild hyperkalaemia:** `5.5–5.9 mmol/L`
- **Moderate hyperkalaemia:** `6.0–6.4 mmol/L`
- **Severe hyperkalaemia:** `≥ 6.5 mmol/L`

Mild hyperkalaemia often causes no symptoms and is commonly detected incidentally on routine laboratory testing. It may also be chronic, especially in patients with reduced kidney function, often without clear clinical manifestation. Treatment depends on symptoms and may include stimulation of potassium elimination, for example with oral or rectal preparations, and adjustment of diuretic therapy.

Moderate hyperkalaemia may produce symptoms and, more importantly, changes in cardiac rhythm. Severe hyperkalaemia is a potentially life-threatening emergency in which ECG changes are commonly present.

These patients should be assessed using the **ABCDE** approach and a **SAMPLE** history. Continuous monitoring is essential: standard 3-lead ECG monitoring, repeated blood pressure measurements, and continuous `SpO2` monitoring. Serial 12-lead ECGs should be recorded to follow ECG changes and response to therapy.

Symptoms are non-specific and depend on potassium level. They may include diarrhoea, nausea, vomiting, thirst, muscle weakness, muscle pain or numbness, and palpitations with or without chest pain.

Expected ECG changes include:

- first-degree AV block
- loss of P waves
- broadened QRS complex
- ST-segment abnormalities
- fusion of S and T waves
- sine-wave pattern
- tall, tented T waves

Patients may present with bradycardia or ventricular tachycardia, and untreated hyperkalaemia may progress to cardiac arrest.

Treatment of moderate or severe hyperkalaemia depends on the clinical presentation. The three basic principles are:

1. **Protect the heart**
2. **Shift potassium into cells**
3. **Remove excess potassium from the body**

If ECG changes are present, start treatment with intravenous calcium, either **calcium gluconate** or **calcium chloride**, given slowly IV. This protects the myocardium from the harmful effects of high potassium concentration, but it does **not** lower serum potassium itself.

Treatment is then continued by shifting potassium into cells using intravenous glucose with short-acting insulin and nebulised salbutamol. In severe hyperkalaemia, or in moderate hyperkalaemia that is resistant to therapy and accompanied by marked ECG changes and/or arrhythmias, seek urgent nephrology input and consider emergency dialysis.

#### Causes of hyperkalaemia

- acidaemia, with potassium leaving cells in exchange for hydrogen ions
- diabetes, due to impaired insulin-regulated cellular glucose transport and associated potassium shift
- diuretics, especially potassium-sparing diuretics and aldosterone-receptor antagonists such as spironolactone and eplerenone; thiazide diuretics are also listed in the source chapter
- chronic kidney failure or acute kidney injury
- medications such as ACE inhibitors, beta-blockers, and non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs)
- tissue breakdown, including rhabdomyolysis, tumour necrosis, and haemolysis
- potassium-rich diet
- pseudohyperkalaemia due to haemolysis from poor blood sampling or sample storage

Initial steps:

- assess the patient using the **ABCDE** approach
- obtain a **12-lead ECG** and monitor cardiac rhythm, especially if serum potassium is `> 6.5 mmol/L`
- exclude **pseudohyperkalaemia**
- if hyperkalaemia is strongly suspected and arrhythmia is present, treat empirically while awaiting confirmation
- seek expert help early

If **ECG changes** are present, give:

- **Calcium chloride 10%:** `10 mL IV`, or
- **Calcium gluconate 10%:** `30 mL IV`

Use a large-bore IV line and administer over `5 minutes`. Repeat the ECG. Consider a further dose after `5 minutes` if ECG changes persist.

To **shift potassium into cells**, use:

- **Insulin–glucose IV infusion:** `25 g glucose` with `10 units soluble insulin` over `15–30 minutes IV`
  - examples given in the source text: `50 mL of 50% glucose`, `125 mL of 20% glucose`, or `250 mL of 10% glucose`
- If pre-treatment blood glucose is `< 7.0 mmol/L`, start **10% glucose infusion at 50 mL/hour for 5 hours** (`25 g`)
- **Nebulised salbutamol:** `10–20 mg`

Monitor both **serum potassium** and **blood glucose**, and be alert for hyperglycaemia or hypoglycaemia.

To **remove potassium from the body**, the source chapter lists:

- **Sodium zirconium cyclosilicate:** `10 g` orally `3 times/day for 72 hours`
- **Patiromer:** `8.4 g/day` orally
- **Calcium resonium:** `15 g` orally `3 times/day`

Follow local practice. Consider **dialysis** and urgent specialist help in life-threatening hyperkalaemia or if potassium remains around or above `6.5 mmol/L` despite medical therapy.

### Hypokalaemia

Hypokalaemia is one of the most frequent electrolyte disorders in everyday practice. It is more common than hyperkalaemia and usually presents with milder symptoms. It is defined as a potassium concentration **below `3.5 mmol/L`** and can be classified as:

- **Mild:** `3.0–3.4 mmol/L`
- **Moderate:** `2.5–2.9 mmol/L`
- **Severe:** `< 2.4 mmol/L`

It is especially common in inpatients, particularly in cardiac, nephrology, and paediatric populations.

Hypokalaemia can often be suspected from a well-taken history even before laboratory results are available. It may present with musculoskeletal symptoms and, more dangerously, cardiovascular manifestations. Mild forms commonly cause muscle cramps. As severity increases, patients may develop paralytic ileus, nausea and vomiting, possible rhabdomyolysis, and cardiac arrhythmias.

Typical ECG findings include:

- flattened T waves
- ST depression
- prominent U waves, especially in precordial leads `V4–V6`

In practice, one of the most common cardiac manifestations is sudden-onset atrial fibrillation.

Treatment depends on severity. It is most often treated with oral potassium supplementation, for example **potassium citrate** in dilute aqueous solution (`Kalinor®`), which is usually effective within `1–2 hours`. If oral replacement is not possible or the deficit is severe, potassium may be supplemented intravenously through a peripheral vein.

The source chapter states:

- recommended IV rate: `10 mmol/hour`
- upper routine IV limit: `20 mmol/hour`
- in very severe cases: `10 mmol over 10 minutes`, then an additional `10 mmol over 5–10 minutes`

Continuous ECG monitoring is mandatory in severe IV replacement.

#### Common causes of hypokalaemia

- reduced dietary potassium intake, usually relevant only in severe malnutrition or in combination with other factors
- increased intracellular shift of potassium due to alkalaemia, hyperinsulinaemia, aldosterone, or salbutamol
- increased potassium losses through the kidneys or gastrointestinal tract, including primary hyperaldosteronism, Cushing syndrome, loop or osmotic diuretics, hypomagnesaemia, laxative use, and severe diarrhoea

## 6.2.2 Other electrolyte disorders

### Hyponatraemia and hypernatraemia

Sodium is the main extracellular positively charged ion. Its plasma concentration usually varies between `135 and 145 mmol/L`. Together with chloride and bicarbonate anions, it determines roughly 90% of extracellular osmolarity.

Sodium concentration is primarily dependent on the overall volume of extracellular fluid, so both fluid depletion and fluid overload can lead to sodium disorders. Mild abnormalities are often asymptomatic and self-limiting, whereas severe disturbances can be fatal.

#### Table 1. Sodium disorders: causes, clinical presentation, and treatment options

| Disorder | Causes according to overall extracellular fluid volume | Clinical presentation | Treatment |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Hypernatraemia** | **Low volume:** diuretics, vomiting, diarrhoea, thirst disorders. **Normal volume:** diabetes insipidus. **High volume:** loss of central regulatory mechanisms, for example after traumatic brain injury, or overuse of hypertonic IV solutions. | The leading symptom is **thirst**. More severe forms may lead to CNS dysfunction, including disorientation, irritability, muscle cramps, altered mental status, and reduced consciousness up to coma. | Usually treated with **fluid resuscitation**, except in states with high extracellular fluid volume, where treatment begins with increasing urine output and then careful fluid replacement. |
| **Hyponatraemia** | **Low volume:** fluid loss through the GI tract such as pancreatitis or peritonitis, urinary losses with diuretic overuse, nephropathies, or burns. **Normal volume:** SIADH. **High volume:** liver cirrhosis, cardiac decompensation, acute or chronic kidney failure, nephrotic syndrome. | Symptoms are mainly due to **CNS dysfunction**: disorientation, inertia, stupor, convulsions, coma, and death in severe cases. | Careful sodium replacement with **hypertonic sodium chloride** and **fluid restriction** depending on extracellular volume status, with optional addition of a diuretic. **Caution:** overly rapid sodium correction may cause central pontine demyelination. |

### Hypocalcaemia and hypercalcaemia

Calcium is found predominantly in bone, accounting for roughly 99% of total body calcium. The remaining 1% circulates in plasma in three forms: protein-bound, chelated, and ionised. Laboratories usually measure both **total calcium** and **ionised calcium**. Typical values are:

- **Total calcium:** `2.2–2.6 mmol/L`
- **Ionised calcium:** `1.15–1.25 mmol/L`

Calcium is essential for coagulation, neuromuscular conduction, and muscle contraction. Increased concentration is called **hypercalcaemia** and reduced concentration **hypocalcaemia**.

#### Table 2. Calcium disorders: causes, clinical presentation, and treatment options

| Disorder | Causes | Clinical presentation | Treatment |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Hypercalcaemia** | Hyperparathyroidism, malignant disease, granulomatous disease such as sarcoidosis or tuberculosis | Nausea, vomiting, constipation, ileus, hypotension, shortened QT interval, arrhythmias, polyuria, altered mental status; most common with ionised calcium levels above `3.5 mmol/L` | IV fluids, diuretics, calcitonin, bisphosphonates to block osteoclast activation, and corticosteroids to reduce intestinal calcium absorption |
| **Hypocalcaemia** | Hypoparathyroidism, vitamin D deficiency, kidney disease, magnesium depletion, sepsis, blood transfusion | Tetany, hyperreflexia, paraesthesia, epileptic seizures, heart rhythm disorders | Calcium replacement using **10% calcium chloride** or **10% calcium gluconate**, with magnesium or vitamin D replacement as needed |

### Hypomagnesaemia and hypermagnesaemia

Magnesium is a cation important in several physiological processes. It participates in the function of the `Na+/K+` pump and regulates calcium entry into muscle cells, which is crucial for normal cardiac contractility and vascular tone.

As with calcium, the biologically active form is ionised in plasma. Laboratories usually measure **total magnesium**, with normal concentrations around `0.7–1.0 mmol/L`. Values above `1.0 mmol/L` define **hypermagnesaemia**, and values below `0.7 mmol/L` define **hypomagnesaemia**.

#### Table 3. Magnesium disorders: causes, clinical presentation, and treatment options

| Disorder | Causes | Clinical presentation | Treatment |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Hypermagnesaemia** | Kidney failure | Cardiac manifestations predominate because magnesium antagonises calcium: first-degree AV block, third-degree AV block, and cardiac arrest, especially with levels above `13 mmol/L` | Emergency dialysis, calcium supplementation for temporary cardiac stabilisation, diuretics |
| **Hypomagnesaemia** | Medications such as furosemide, aminoglycosides, and digitalis; alcoholism and malnutrition; increased GI loss; diabetes with glycosuria | CNS dysfunction with attention deficit, tetany, fasciculations, generalised tonic–clonic seizures, and cardiac manifestations such as arrhythmias, ischaemia, and digitalis toxicity | Oral or parenteral magnesium supplementation |

## 6.2.3 Initial patient assessment

### A (Airway)

- Loss of consciousness caused by CNS symptoms or progression of cardiac manifestations may result in snoring due to upper-airway soft tissue obstruction.
- During seizures, foam, vomit, or other fluid contents may be present in the mouth, producing gargling sounds.

### B (Breathing)

- Respiratory rate may be increased in mild electrolyte disorders due to irritability, psychomotor agitation, or compensation for metabolic acidosis.
- In more severe cases, respiratory rate may fall, progressing to bradypnoea or apnoea as the condition worsens or cardiac arrest develops.
- Consider injuries that can cause abnormal chest wall movement, such as pneumothorax.
- Auscultation may reveal rhonchi or crackles due to airway secretions or aspiration of gastric contents.
- If `SpO2 < 94%` (`< 88%` in COPD), administer oxygen therapy.

### C (Circulation)

- Because electrolyte disorders can produce life-threatening cardiac manifestations, monitor the patient with 3-lead ECG rhythm monitoring, `SpO2`, blood pressure, and repeated standard 12-lead ECG recordings.
- Establish wide-bore peripheral venous access.
- Pulse may be bradycardic, tachycardic, regular, irregular, or absent in cardiac arrest.
- Hypotension may accompany arrhythmias.
- If arrhythmia, hypotension, or reduced consciousness is present, shock may develop with pale, cold, dry skin and prolonged capillary refill time.
- A urinary catheter may be useful to monitor urine output and response to treatment, especially when hypokalaemia or diuretic therapy is suspected.

### D (Disability)

- Assess level of consciousness using **AVPU** or **GCS**.
- Repeat assessment, because disorders of consciousness may continue to progress.
- Measure blood glucose to exclude hyperglycaemia or hypoglycaemia.
- Monitor pupils for changes associated with worsening neurological status.

### E (Exposure and environment)

- Look for signs of liver cirrhosis, cardiac decompensation, pancreatitis, malnutrition, or arteriovenous fistulas in patients on renal replacement therapy.

## 6.2.4 Patient history according to the SAMPLE approach

- **S (Symptoms):** diarrhoea, nausea, vomiting, thirst, muscle cramps or spasms, palpitations with or without chest pain, increased urine output with or without diuretics, reduced urine output, and CNS-related symptoms of excitation or depression
- **A (Allergies):** known allergies to food or medications
- **M (Medications):** diuretics, especially potassium-sparing diuretics and aldosterone-receptor antagonists, mannitol in inpatients, ACE inhibitors, beta-blockers, NSAIDs, beta-2 agonists, insulin, and antiarrhythmic drugs such as digitalis
- **P (Past or pertinent medical history):** chronic heart failure, chronic kidney disease, dialysis, diabetes, endocrine disorders such as Cushing syndrome, SIADH, primary hyperaldosteronism, diabetes insipidus, hyper- or hypoparathyroidism, hypovitaminosis D, alcoholism, and malnutrition
- **L (Last oral intake):** possible food intoxication with vomiting or diarrhoea
- **E (Events leading up to present illness or injury):** recent dialysis, food history, and deviations from regular drug therapy
