## Review

### Part 6: Blood, Fluids and Electrolytes

In every patient, begin with the **ABCDE approach**.

Humans are largely water, and not all of it is contained within the blood vessels. When thinking about maintenance requirements, use the **4-2-1 formula** to estimate basal fluid needs. Remember that this does **not** account for additional losses such as vomiting, diarrhoea, and bleeding, which often cannot be measured precisely and must be estimated clinically.

For routine fluid replacement, **balanced crystalloids** are the preferred first-line option. Use the solution that is available. Although **0.9% sodium chloride** is not physiological and not balanced, there are clinical situations where it may still be preferred, such as **brain injury, hyperkalaemia, or chloride loss**.

When an **electrolyte disorder** is suspected, always try to review the patient’s **previous laboratory results** to assess the trend rather than relying on a single value in isolation.

**Hypokalaemia** is the most common electrolyte disorder. In general, it is easier to replace a deficit than to remove an excess.

**Hyperkalaemia** is common in chronic kidney disease, although it may occur for other reasons as well. It can be life-threatening and requires a careful, structured response. Look for **ECG changes**, protect the myocardium with **calcium** if needed, and use **insulin with glucose** plus **salbutamol** to shift potassium into cells. This buys time while seeking expert help and considering or organising **dialysis**.

**Significant bleeding** is dangerous for two main reasons. It reduces **cardiac output and haemoglobin concentration**, which lowers oxygen delivery to tissues, and it also consumes and removes **coagulation factors**, which promotes further bleeding.

In **haemorrhagic shock**, the patient is typically **pale, cold, and mottled**, and may be **tachypnoeic, tachycardic, agitated, disoriented, or unconscious**.

Be particularly careful with **paediatric** and **geriatric** patients. Older infants and children can compensate vigorously for some time, but once they stop compensating, cardiac arrest may be imminent. Geriatric patients, by contrast, often decompensate early. Both groups require a lower threshold for concern.

When managing suspected blood loss of more than **15% of total blood volume** outside the hospital, first try to **control the bleeding** if possible. Establish **two wide-bore IV lines**, administer **crystalloids**, and consider **colloids** as described in the source chapter, including **6% HES up to 500–1000 mL** where this is part of local practice. If available, use **blood products** according to local protocol.

During fluid resuscitation for traumatic bleeding, follow the **targeted MAP and blood pressure goals** rather than giving unrestricted fluid volumes.
