## Introduction

Intravenous (IV) fluid is a drug administered when a person is, due to the nature of a disease, unable to sustain their own basal metabolic needs. This occurs in several situations. Human metabolic needs are normally met through water and food intake. If the level of consciousness is altered, that becomes impossible. Likewise, in severe gastrointestinal abnormalities, even if the patient is theoretically able to swallow food and drink water, absorption is impaired and a deficit develops.

In some conditions, IV fluids are administered to compensate for losses, for example blood products in the case of bleeding. To understand the indications, contraindications, and proper choice of IV fluid, some basic physiological facts need to be stated. Approximately 60% of human body weight is water, although this varies with age, sex, and body composition.

Total body water is located intracellularly and extracellularly. Extracellular fluid can be divided into the following compartments:

- interstitial fluid, which is lymph and protein-poor fluid between the cells
- intravascular fluid, that is, plasma volume
- transcellular fluid, including gastrointestinal fluid, bile, urine, vitreous body, joint fluid, and pleural, peritoneal, and pericardial fluid
- fluid in bones and dense connective tissue, which is anatomically significant but does not form functional extracellular fluid because flow between this and other compartments is slow

## Basal metabolism

Urine, gastrointestinal fluid production, perspiration, and invisible losses from the skin and lungs are responsible for basal metabolic needs. If a patient is unable to maintain basal metabolic requirements through oral intake, fluid and electrolyte disorders will occur.

The **4-2-1 formula** is used to calculate hourly basal metabolic needs. In some conditions, retrograde deficit should also be included, for example in a patient with prolonged fasting, and calculated extra losses should be added. Some losses are impossible to determine precisely, such as vomiting or diarrhoea. Clinical presentation and ABCDE assessment findings guide fluid optimisation, including but not limited to heart rate, blood pressure, capillary refill time, and urine output.

### Basal metabolic requirements

| Ideal body weight | Requirement |
|---|---|
| First 10 kg | 4 mL/kg/h |
| Next 10 kg | Add 2 mL/kg/h |
| Each kg above 20 kg | Add 1 mL/kg/h |

For a person weighing **70 kg**, the first 10 kg accounts for **40 mL/h**, the next 10 kg for **20 mL/h**, and the remaining 50 kg for **50 mL/h**, giving a total of **110 mL/h**.

## Type and selection of intravenous fluid

IV fluids are broadly divided into **crystalloids** and **colloids**. The main difference between them is plasma half-life. Crystalloids have a significantly shorter half-life, approximately **20–40 minutes**, while the colloid half-life is around **4–6 hours**. Indications, contraindications, and side effects vary depending on the properties of each fluid.

### Classification of IV fluids

| Type | Subtype | Example | Characteristics |
|---|---|---|---|
| Crystalloids | Unbalanced solutions | 0.9% NaCl (saline) | Isotonic; contains higher chloride concentration than plasma |
| Crystalloids | Unbalanced solutions | Hypertonic saline (1.8%, 3%, 7.5%) | Used for correction of hypoosmolar hyponatraemia and elevated ICP |
| Crystalloids | Balanced solutions | Ringer lactate | Hypotonic; contains buffer (lactate) |
| Crystalloids | Balanced solutions | Plasma-Lyte 148 | Isotonic; contains buffers (acetate and gluconate) |
| Colloids | Hydroxyethyl starch (HES) | HES 6% | Plasma volume expansion; numerous adverse effects |
| Colloids | Plasma derivatives | Human albumin 20%, freshly frozen plasma | Plasma volume expansion in selected indications |

## Crystalloids

### Unbalanced solutions

Saline (**0.9% NaCl**) has a higher chloride ion content. Strong ion difference (SID) is defined as the sum of strong cation concentrations (Na, Mg, K) minus the sum of strong anion concentrations (Cl, lactate). The SID of extracellular fluid is approximately **40 mEq/L**, while the SID of saline is **0**. Infusing 0.9% NaCl therefore reduces SID and causes metabolic acidosis. This has a clinically significant effect at doses greater than **1,000 mL**, when it may cause **hyperchloraemic metabolic acidosis**.

### Balanced solutions

Balanced solutions have chloride concentrations similar to human plasma. They contain added anions such as acetate, malate, and gluconate. These behave as buffers and generate bicarbonate.

Acetate is metabolised throughout the body, is not dependent on liver metabolism, and is metabolised faster than lactate. It does not interfere with glucose or insulin levels. Lactate is metabolised in the liver and can become glucose through gluconeogenesis. In severe shock, liver metabolic function may be disturbed, while acetate metabolism remains unaffected. Gluconate metabolism is not fully explained. Besides additional buffers, these solutions contain varying concentrations of cations such as sodium, potassium, calcium, and magnesium.

## Colloids

Colloids have higher colloid-osmotic pressure and reduce transcapillary filtration rate in the setting of low capillary hydrostatic pressure. Therefore, they expand plasma volume. Their half-life is longer compared with crystalloids.

However, colloids tend to have a significant adverse-effect profile, which limits both their indications and the volumes that may be infused. In emergency settings, the most commonly used colloid is **HES 6% (hydroxyethyl starch)**, which is associated with coagulopathy, anaphylactoid reactions (**< 0.6%**), and renal toxicity, making it contraindicated in patients with renal failure. Nevertheless, it may be considered in severe haemorrhagic shock when blood product availability is limited.
