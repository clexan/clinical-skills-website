Haemorrhage results from blood loss from the intravascular space, which decreases oxygen delivery to tissues. When oxygen delivery becomes insufficient to maintain aerobic metabolism, a state of shock occurs regardless of the initial cause. If this generalised ischaemia persists, cell death, organ damage, multiorgan failure, and ultimately death may follow.

Oxygen delivery to cells (`DO2`) depends on two major factors:

1. **Cardiac output (CO)** — the product of stroke volume and heart rate
2. **Oxygen content of arterial blood** — determined mainly by haemoglobin concentration (`Hb`), arterial oxygen saturation (`SaO2`), and arterial oxygen partial pressure (`PaO2`)

You are not expected to memorise the formula. The important point is that oxygen delivery, together with cardiac output, depends mainly on haemoglobin concentration, and haemoglobin is lost during bleeding.

In summary, the absolute priority in haemorrhagic shock is to **stop the bleeding quickly** and **compensate for blood loss**. The first prevents further haemoglobin loss, and the second helps the heart generate enough cardiac output to maintain organ perfusion.

## Normal blood volume

- **Adults:** `70 mL/kg` ideal body weight
- **Children:** `80 mL/kg` ideal body weight

## Classification of bleeding

1. **Stage 1: `< 15%` blood volume loss**  
   Usually without major changes in vital signs. IV fluid resuscitation is generally not required.

2. **Stage 2: `15–30%` blood volume loss**  
   The patient becomes tachycardic, and pulse pressure narrows. Compensatory mechanisms are activated, peripheral vasoconstriction occurs, and the skin and mucosa become pale and cold. The patient may become agitated. Resuscitation of circulating volume with crystalloids and colloids is required. In patients with cardiovascular disease, consider blood products.

3. **Stage 3: `30–40%` blood volume loss**  
   Blood pressure falls (`systolic < 90 mmHg`), heart rate rises (`> 110/min`), and signs of hypoperfusion or shock appear, including prolonged capillary refill time, reduced urine output, and impaired consciousness due to inadequate cerebral blood flow. Transfusion of blood products is required.

   *Haematocrit and haemoglobin values may be misleading because of haemoconcentration. Decisions on transfusion should therefore be based primarily on clinical assessment.*

4. **Stage 4: `> 40%` blood volume loss**  
   Compensatory mechanisms are exhausted.

### Blood loss is difficult to assess in the following patients

1. **Elderly patients**  
   They have reduced compensatory reserve. Even relatively minor bleeding may cause an early fall in blood pressure. Similar limitations apply to patients with so-called fixed low cardiac output states such as aortic stenosis.

2. **Patients on chronic therapy and patients with pacemakers**  
   Patients taking beta-blockers may not develop compensatory tachycardia, and blood pressure may fall sooner. Bleeding severity may therefore be underestimated if heart rate is used alone. The same applies to patients whose heart rate is fixed by a pacemaker.

3. **Pregnant women**  
   They normally have a higher heart rate, increased circulating blood volume, and increased respiratory rate, all of which can complicate assessment of blood loss.

4. **Athletes**  
   At baseline they may have a resting heart rate below `50/min`. In this group, significant relative tachycardia may already be present even when the absolute heart rate remains below `100/min`.

## Causes of severe bleeding

- traumatic injury, for example traffic accidents, falls from height, stab wounds, or gunshot wounds
- gastrointestinal bleeding, for example bleeding gastric or duodenal ulcer, intestinal obstruction with necrosis, or haemorrhoids
- major gynaecological or obstetric bleeding, for example placental abruption, placenta previa, or ectopic pregnancy
- bleeding from large blood vessels, for example aortic dissection or ruptured thoracic or abdominal aortic aneurysm
- tumour infiltration of major vessels, for example thoracic aortic infiltration by lung or oesophageal cancer

## Initial patient assessment: ABCDE + SAMPLE

### Goals

- recognise and stop bleeding as early as possible (**damage control resuscitation**)
- check breathing and pulse
- if severe external bleeding is present, attempt to limit or stop it with pressure and bandages

### A (airway) / B (breathing)

- Check whether the patient can maintain a patent airway.
- If obstruction is present, open the airway while stabilising the cervical spine if trauma is suspected.
- Assess symmetrical chest movement, respiratory rate, auscultation, and percussion.
- Use a pulse oximeter.
- Administer **high-flow oxygen** (`15 L/min`) if `SpO2 < 94%`.
- If the patient has acute respiratory insufficiency and/or impaired consciousness (`GCS ≤ 8`), secure the airway with an endotracheal tube.

### C (circulation)

- Connect the patient to ECG monitoring.
- Measure heart rate on both central and peripheral pulses.
- Measure blood pressure and repeat it every `3–5 minutes`.
- Assess capillary refill time.
- Insert a urinary catheter and monitor hourly urine output.
- Establish at least **two wide-bore IV lines** (`≥ 18 G`) or use alternative vascular access if needed, such as intraosseous or central venous access.

Try to identify the source of bleeding and estimate blood loss. Do not forget the possibility of **hidden bleeding** in the thorax, abdomen, pelvis, retroperitoneum, and thigh muscles.

If bleeding exceeds about `15%` of total blood volume, the source chapter recommends resuscitation with **crystalloids** (for example saline, Ringer’s lactate, Plasma-Lyte) and **colloids** (for example 6% HES or human albumin). Fluid resuscitation should be **targeted**, especially before blood products are available.

Whichever IV fluid is used, it should be **warmed to body temperature**, because hypothermia worsens coagulopathy.

Aggressive resuscitation with large volumes of IV fluids may increase blood pressure excessively, dislodge or disrupt an already formed clot, and worsen dilutional coagulopathy. This may increase bleeding further and create a vicious cycle.

The priority is to provide **definitive haemostasis** by surgical, radiological, endovascular, or other means as quickly as possible. Until haemostasis is achieved, blood pressure should be kept high enough to preserve short-term organ viability but low enough to avoid provoking further bleeding.

According to the source chapter, target pressure values in traumatic bleeding are:

- **Penetrating trauma without suspected brain or spinal cord injury and without other risk factors:** `MAP > 65 mmHg` or systolic pressure `80–90 mmHg`
- **Blunt trauma and/or brain injury, spinal cord injury, or other risk factors:** `MAP > 85–90 mmHg` or systolic pressure `110–120 mmHg`

Adjust IV fluid administration to these targets. Do not give fluids beyond what is necessary to reach the target pressure. Boluses of `500–1000 mL` are commonly used.

As soon as blood products are available and significant bleeding is present, the source chapter recommends **thromboelastography-guided** targeted therapy when possible. If this is not available, a **massive transfusion protocol** of `1:1:1` using erythrocyte concentrate, fresh frozen plasma, and platelets is recommended, with additional clotting factors as needed.

#### Tranexamic acid

Tranexamic acid is an antifibrinolytic agent indicated in major bleeding because it helps preserve the fibrin clot at the bleeding site. It should be given **within 3 hours of bleeding onset**.

The regimen given in the source chapter is:

- `1 g` in `100 mL` saline over `10 minutes`
- followed by `1 g` in `500 mL` saline over the next `8 hours`

The chapter reports no major side effects, but cautions against indiscriminate use in patients with existing thromboembolic events, epilepsy, or visual disturbances.

#### Other haemostatic measures listed in the chapter

Consider commercially available clotting factors such as:

- recombinant factor VII
- prothrombin complex concentrate (factors II, VII, IX, X)
- antithrombin III
- factor XIII
- cryoprecipitate

#### Vasoconstrictors

- In traumatic brain injury and spinal cord injury, vasoconstrictors may be used **in addition to fluids** to maintain an adequate MAP.

### Vital signs to repeat every few minutes

- respiratory rate and `SpO2`
- heart rate, arterial pressure, and pulse strength on central and peripheral arteries
- ECG rhythm, rate, and QRS morphology
- capillary refill time

### D (disability)

- Assess consciousness using **GCS**
- Examine pupils
- Measure blood glucose

Compensatory mechanisms during bleeding aim to preserve cerebral blood flow. If bleeding causes reduced consciousness, this usually means that more than `30%` of circulating blood volume has been lost and compensatory mechanisms are becoming insufficient.

### E (exposure)

- Measure body temperature and keep the patient warm.
- Examine the body carefully for a source of bleeding.
- In trauma, look for direct and indirect signs of long-bone fracture, pelvic fracture, rib fractures, or abdominal distension.
- In women of childbearing age with abdominal pain, consider **ectopic pregnancy**.
- Significant bleeding in later pregnancy is a major cause of maternal mortality.
- If gastrointestinal bleeding is suspected, perform a **digital rectal examination**. Black stool may suggest bleeding from the oesophagus or stomach, while fresh blood may originate from any part of the gastrointestinal tract.

## SAMPLE history

- **S (Signs / symptoms):** tailor questions to the likely source and cause of bleeding
- **A (Allergies):** ask about allergies
- **M (Medications):** ask specifically about antiplatelets such as acetylsalicylic acid, clopidogrel, or prasugrel; anticoagulants such as warfarin or NOACs; and beta-blockers
- **P (Past illnesses):** significant comorbidities and prior surgery
- **L (Last oral intake / last menstrual period):** aspiration risk after recent eating; in women of childbearing age, determine the date of the last menstrual period
- **E (Events leading up to the present illness):** identify the events preceding the current condition
