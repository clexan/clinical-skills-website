## Introduction

Anaphylaxis is a severe, life-threatening, generalised, or systemic hypersensitivity reaction to an allergen. It is characterised by the rapid development of life-threatening problems with the airway and/or breathing and/or circulation. Although commonly associated with anaphylaxis, skin and mucosal changes do not have to be present.

## Initial assessment

### A (Airway)

Assess whether the airway is open. Anaphylaxis is associated with airway obstruction at several different levels:

- Swelling of the throat and tongue (pharyngeal/laryngeal oedema): the patient breathes audibly, has difficulty breathing and swallowing, and feels that “their throat is closing”.
- A hoarse voice is a sign of vocal cord oedema, in which case the airway must be managed urgently by endotracheal intubation.
- Inspiratory stridor: a high-frequency inspiratory noise caused by upper airway obstruction.

### B (Breathing)

- Swelling of the respiratory tract and bronchoconstriction can lead to hypoxia. The patient usually tries to compensate by increasing both the frequency and work of breathing, which can lead to the patient becoming tired and unable to breathe adequately due to exhaustion.
- Bronchospasm can be heard on auscultation of the lungs in the form of wheezing, prolonged expiration, and, in severe cases, the so-called silent chest.
- A decrease in oxygen saturation is possible.
- Anaphylaxis can lead to respiratory arrest.

### C (Circulation)

- Circulatory derangements, often called anaphylactic shock, can be caused by myocardial depression, vasodilation, and increased capillary permeability, followed by fluid loss from the intravascular space.
- Signs of shock may be present: tachycardia, hypotension, dizziness, and/or impending collapse.
- Anaphylaxis can cause myocardial ischaemia and ECG changes even in patients with normal coronary arteries.
- Anaphylaxis can lead to cardiac arrest.

### D (Disability)

- The previously mentioned problems with the airway, breathing, and circulation can lead to reduced brain perfusion and affect the patient’s neurological status.
- Confusion, agitation, and loss of consciousness may be present.

### E (Exposure and environment)

- In about 80% of anaphylactic reactions, changes occur on the skin and/or mucosa.
- Erythema may be present as a spotted or generalised red rash.
- Urticaria, or hives, may be present anywhere on the body. Hives can be pale, pink, or red and resemble nettle burns.
- Angioedema is similar to urticaria but includes swelling of deeper tissues, most often in the eyelid area and sometimes the mouth and throat.
- Although skin changes are stressful and concerning for both patients and clinicians, skin changes without life-threatening problems with the airway, breathing, or circulation are not anaphylaxis.

### SAMPLE

Patients who have already had anaphylaxis are at higher risk of developing it again. Positive history of exposure to an allergen is also important, as well as previously known allergies.

The most common allergens that cause anaphylaxis in adults are insect bites, food intake (fish, shellfish, nuts), intake of drugs (penicillin and cephalosporin antibiotics, non-steroid antirheumatic drugs), and latex.

## Treatment

Once the ABCDE assessment is made and anaphylaxis is confirmed, an imperative is to call for help. Treatment of anaphylaxis is based on managing respiratory and circulatory problems. Adrenaline acts on several different levels: by constricting dilated vessels, reducing vascular permeability, and having a bronchodilating effect in the lungs. Thus, it has a therapeutic effect on the systemic effects of released histamine.

In anaphylaxis, adrenaline is given only intramuscularly (IM) in a dose of 0.5 mg into the anterolateral side of the middle third of the thigh.

Adrenaline is given intravenously only by experienced specialists in diluted form because of the potentially disastrous consequences it can have. However, it is important to establish intravenous or intraosseous access for administering intravenous fluids to replace intravascular volume, since significant hypovolaemia is present due to increased capillary permeability and vasodilatation. The airway and breathing are managed by setting up high-flow oxygen and endotracheal intubation. If you are not skilled, ask for help.

Antihistamines and corticosteroids are only second-line treatment for anaphylaxis and must not delay the administration of adrenaline, oxygen, endotracheal intubation, and intravenous fluid replacement.
