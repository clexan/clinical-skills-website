# 3.2 Oxygen therapy and pulse oximetry

*Erika Šuper-Petrinjac*

Oxygen is one of the most commonly used drugs in modern medicine, and until recently it was often considered harmless, without any need for special dosing recommendations. However, many adverse effects of oxygen are now recognised. Like any other drug, oxygen has a recommended dosage regimen, route of administration, side effects, indications, and contraindications.

We all breathe oxygen at slightly less than 21% (`FiO2 0.21`). When climbing to high altitudes, the percentage of oxygen remains the same, but the partial pressure of gases decreases.

Administering oxygen increases oxygen concentration in the inspired air. The oxygen dose is the `FiO2`, which is determined clinically by the administration route and the flow rate. Oxygen can be administered to a spontaneously breathing patient via nasal cannula, simple oxygen mask, and non-rebreather mask. Venturi masks are specially designed to deliver fixed FiO2 values and are colour-coded.

Indications for oxygen administration are compromised airway patency and/or low `SaO2` levels, below 94% in previously healthy individuals and below 88% in COPD patients. Oxygen acts as a vasoconstrictor of coronary arteries. In a patient with chest pain, oxygen administration is indicated when the `SaO2` level drops below 90%.

Oxygen produces reactive oxygen species (ROS), which demonstrates tissue toxicity. An important exception is haemorrhagic shock, where high-flow oxygen may be administered even when saturation appears normal. In bleeding, loss of haemoglobin significantly reduces the oxygen-carrying capacity of the blood and may lead to severe tissue hypoxia despite normal saturation of the remaining haemoglobin.

## Approximate FiO2 delivered by common devices

| Device for oxygen delivery | Oxygen flow rate | Approximate FiO2 delivered |
|---|---:|---:|
| Nasal cannula | 1–4 L/min | 0.21–0.30 |
| Simple oxygen face mask | 4–10 L/min | 0.35–0.60 |
| Non-rebreather mask | 10–15 L/min | 0.85 (can be up to 1.0) |
| Venturi face mask | Fixed-flow, colour-coded mask | Exact FiO2 written on the mask |

## Pulse oximetry

Pulse oximetry is a non-invasive method of continuous measurement of haemoglobin oxygen saturation. The probe is usually placed on the fingertip, and the screen shows the `SpO2` percentage, an oximetry waveform, and the numerical heart rate. It measures light absorption from blood at two different wavelengths, 660 nm and 940 nm. One fraction of light passes through the tissue and another is absorbed in the tissue. These two wavelengths allow distinction between oxyhaemoglobin and deoxyhaemoglobin molecules.

## Limitations

- Pulse oximetry does not provide information about ventilation, only about oxygenation.
- In carbon monoxide poisoning, a falsely high reading may occur because oxyhaemoglobin and carboxyhaemoglobin absorb similar wavelengths.
