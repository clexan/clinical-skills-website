# 3.3 Arterial blood gases (ABGs)

*Erika Šuper-Petrinjac*

## 3.3.1 Basic concepts

Partial pressure describes the number of molecules in a gas mixture better than percentage alone. For example, if we double the total pressure of a mixture, partial pressures double while the percentage stays the same. Atmospheric air is at 1 atm, approximately 100 kPa or 750 mmHg. Nitrogen partial pressure is about 78 kPa and oxygen partial pressure is about 21 kPa. When we breathe a 40% oxygen mixture, the oxygen partial pressure is 40 kPa.

When a gas is dissolved in a liquid such as blood, the partial pressure in the liquid is the same as the gas in contact with that fluid. That is the partial pressure of that specific gas in the sample, for example `PaO2`, the partial pressure of oxygen in arterial blood.

For arterial blood gas analysis and acid-base assessment, the following four key parameters are measured.

## 3.3.2 The four key ABG parameters

### 1. pH

Hydrogen ion concentration in the body is extremely small, so we use the pH scale, from 1 to 14, to describe it. The pH of a normal blood sample is 7.35–7.45. The relationship between hydrogen ion concentration and pH is inversely proportional. A high pH means a lower hydrogen ion concentration and is called **alkalaemia**. A low pH means a higher hydrogen ion concentration and is called **acidaemia**.

Small changes in pH reflect major changes in hydrogen ion concentration. Body enzymes function properly only in a narrow pH range. The body produces a significant amount of hydrogen ions every day and buffers them to prevent acidaemia. The most important intracellular buffers are proteins and phosphates. The most important extracellular buffers are plasma proteins and bicarbonate. Even if body pH is normal, an acid-base disturbance may still be present in compensated form.

### 2. PaCO2

Carbon dioxide is a by-product of cellular metabolism. To reach the lungs and leave the body, it is transported in the blood either dissolved in plasma or bound to haemoglobin and proteins. Normal values are `4.7–6.0 kPa (35–45 mmHg)`.

/>

Carbon dioxide acts as an acid. An increase in CO2 increases hydrogen ion concentration and promotes acidaemia. Reduction of alveolar ventilation is the leading cause of increased CO2 when metabolic production remains constant. This is **respiratory acidosis**. An increase in alveolar ventilation lowers CO2 and promotes alkalaemia. This is **respiratory alkalosis**. In healthy individuals, brain respiratory centres react rapidly to changes in hydrogen ion concentration and increase alveolar ventilation when needed.

### 3. HCO3- and base excess (BE)

The most common way to interpret acid-base disturbances in a blood sample is to analyse bicarbonate as the main buffering mechanism. Bicarbonate values are regulated in the kidney and oppose acids. They act as bases and neutralise hydrogen ions:

<FormulaCallout
  expression="H+ + HCO3− ⇌ H2CO3"
  label="Reaction"
  title="Bicarbonate buffering reaction"
/>

Normal bicarbonate values are `22–26 mmol/L`. A decrease in bicarbonate occurs when it buffers excess hydrogen ions and reflects **metabolic acidosis**. An increase in bicarbonate causes excess buffering of hydrogen ions and reflects **metabolic alkalosis**.

Metabolic and respiratory components are interdependent. Derangement of one usually causes the other to change as well in an attempt to maintain a normal blood pH.

### Note on independent determinants of pH

Because bicarbonate does not directly change blood pH and serves mainly as part of the buffering system, there are three independent variables that directly affect blood pH:

1. **Strong ion difference (SID)** — the difference between the sum of strong cations, such as Na+ and K+, and the sum of strong anions, such as Cl− and lactate.
2. **Total concentration of plasma non-volatile buffers (Atot)** — mostly determined by phosphate and albumin concentration.
3. **PaCO2**.

Hydrogen ion and bicarbonate concentrations behave as dependent variables.

Standard base excess is a calculated value defined by the SID change needed to bring the pH back to 7.4 with a steady-state CO2 value of `5.4 kPa (40 mmHg)` at normal body temperature.

Metabolic acidosis is a product of decreased SID, which leads to chemical reactions that increase hydrogen ion concentration. SID is reduced when the body loses sodium bicarbonate, as in diarrhoea, or when there is an abundance of exogenous anions, as in intoxication. Metabolic alkalosis is a product of increased SID, which occurs when strong anion concentration falls or strong cation concentration rises, for example with vomiting or diuretic therapy.

SID is regulated mainly by the kidneys, although the gastrointestinal tract also contributes. The kidneys cannot rapidly excrete large amounts of strong ions, so meaningful changes take time. Chloride handling is a major driver of SID changes. In the stomach, chloride ions are pumped into the lumen, which increases SID. Normally that chloride is reabsorbed in the duodenum, returning plasma SID toward normal. If a person vomits, chloride cannot be reabsorbed, the body loses chloride, and metabolic alkalosis develops. In contrast, the pancreas produces fluid with higher SID than plasma because it is relatively low in chloride. Significant loss of that fluid, for example through surgical drainage, can cause acidosis. In the intestine, sodium, potassium, and bicarbonate concentrations are high; diarrhoeal loss of that fluid reduces SID and causes acidosis.

### 4. PaO2

At sea level, normal oxygen partial pressure in the atmosphere is about `21 kPa (157.5 mmHg)`. As inspired gas passes through the airway, it becomes humidified and mixed with carbon dioxide, so the oxygen partial pressure falls to about `13 kPa (97.5 mmHg)`. `PaO2` is always lower than `PAO2`, the partial pressure of oxygen in the alveoli. A normal arterial value is usually **greater than 11 kPa (82.5 mmHg)**. In very elderly patients and in those with chronic lung disease, a value above `8 kPa` may be tolerated.

The fraction of oxygen in inspired air, `FiO2`, is 0.21. An estimated expected `PaO2` value is roughly **10 kPa lower than the percentage of inhaled oxygen**. For example, if a person with no disruption in lung oxygenation breathes an oxygen mixture of 40% (`FiO2 = 0.4`), the predicted `PaO2` is about `30 kPa`.

## 3.3.3 ABG analysis: ERC stepwise approach

1. **Assess the patient.**
2. **Is the patient hypoxic?** Do not ignore the `FiO2`.
3. **Is the blood acidic or alkaline?**
   - `pH > 7.45` = alkalaemia
   - `pH < 7.35` = acidaemia
4. **What is happening with the PaCO2 level?**
   - If `pH < 7.35`, ask whether `PaCO2 > 6.0 kPa (45 mmHg)`.
     - If yes, respiratory acidosis is present, or this may represent respiratory compensation of metabolic alkalosis.
   - If `pH > 7.45`, ask whether `PaCO2 < 4.7 kPa (35 mmHg)`.
     - If yes, respiratory alkalosis is present, or this may represent respiratory compensation of metabolic acidosis.
5. **What is happening with HCO3- and BE?**
   - If `pH < 7.35`, ask whether `BE < -2 mmol/L` and/or `HCO3- < 22 mmol/L`.
     - If yes, metabolic acidosis is present, or this may represent renal compensation of respiratory alkalosis.
   - If `pH > 7.45`, ask whether `BE > +2 mmol/L` and/or `HCO3- > 26 mmol/L`.
     - If yes, metabolic alkalosis is present, or this may represent renal compensation of respiratory acidosis.
