## Introduction

In acute respiratory failure, one of the most common physical findings alongside dyspnoea and tachypnoea is wheezing. It occurs with prolonged airflow through narrowed central and distal airways. It is more pronounced during exhalation, unlike in cases of upper respiratory tract obstruction, when stridor is more pronounced during inspiration.

Airway obstruction can occur due to bronchial muscle contraction (bronchospasm), smooth muscle hypertrophy, increased mucus secretion, and/or peribronchial inflammation. Wheezing is usually associated with lower airway diseases such as asthma or other obstructive pulmonary diseases, primarily chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD). Paradoxically, patients with severe airflow obstruction may not have audible wheezing, the so-called trapped lung syndrome.

Approach to the patient, diagnosis, and treatment differ depending on the underlying disease and the obstruction or spasm site. The most common diseases that lead to bronchospasm are asthma and COPD. Wheezing is also very common in heart failure. Elderly patients without a history of asthma may present with other symptoms and signs of heart failure. Other conditions that can manifest with wheezing and mimic asthma are listed below.

## Asthma

Asthma is a chronic inflammatory disorder characterised by increased reversible airway reactivity that can occur in people sensitive to numerous stimuli. It leads to recurrent episodes of shortness of breath, chest tightness, and coughing, especially at night or early in the morning. A common clinical finding is wheezing in the chest. However, the severity of the wheezing does not correspond to the severity of the obstruction.

Attacks often start with a feeling of chest tightness and a cough. As the exacerbation progresses, wheezing becomes audible, exhalation is prolonged, and the use of accessory respiratory muscles can occur. Acute asthma attacks are reversible and regress spontaneously or within minutes to hours of therapy, with symptom-free intervals between attacks. Despite this, many patients with asthma develop chronic airway obstruction.

The findings on physical examination vary widely, from coughing and end-expiratory wheezing in patients with mild attacks to respiratory distress in patients with severe asthma attacks. Sometimes wheezing can be heard without a stethoscope. A change in mental status such as lethargy, exhaustion, agitation, or confusion is a serious warning sign of potential respiratory arrest. The “silent chest” reflects a very serious obstruction in which airflow is insufficient to produce audible wheezing. Hypercapnia is an extremely unfavourable sign in this group of patients.

### Assessment and oxygenation

If patients with acute asthma attacks have SpO₂ < 92%, arterial blood gas analysis is necessary due to the risk of hypercapnia. In acute asthma attacks, ventilation is usually stimulated, resulting in a decrease in arterial partial pressure of carbon dioxide (PaCO₂). Therefore, a normal or slightly elevated PaCO₂, for example > 42 mmHg, indicates extreme airway obstruction and fatigue.

The aim of oxygen therapy is to titrate oxygen flow and concentration to a target SpO₂ of 94–98%.

### Therapy

1. Salbutamol (5 mg in a nebuliser) is the standard therapy in acute exacerbations of asthma. The dose can be repeated every 15–20 minutes. Oxygen or air flow on the nebuliser must be at least 6 L/min. If the patient does not breathe sufficiently, the nebulised drug will not be delivered adequately.
2. Nebulised anticholinergics such as ipratropium bromide 0.5 mg can produce an additional bronchodilating effect.
3. Systemic corticosteroids are also useful.

### Magnesium

In severe asthma, intravenous magnesium 2 g in slow infusion may also be useful. Side effects include nausea, fatigue, headache, and hypotension.

### Aminophylline

There is no evidence of benefit from aminophylline compared with standard therapy. However, if it is decided to use it in the presence of an experienced specialist, administer 5 mg/kg over 20–30 minutes, followed by infusion at a dose of 500–700 mcg/kg/h.

### Fluid therapy

Fluid therapy is used in patients who are dehydrated or have hypovolaemia.

Caution: beta-2 agonists and corticosteroids may cause hypokalaemia.

### Patient monitoring

If the patient does not respond to initial therapy and develops signs of life-threatening asthma, intensive care is required. Sometimes it is difficult to distinguish between severe life-threatening asthma and anaphylaxis. If patients have asthma-like symptoms and do not have previously known asthma or COPD, they should be treated as if the cause were anaphylaxis.

## Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease

Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) is characterised by persistent airflow disturbances that are progressive and associated with an impaired inflammatory response to numerous harmful particles in inhaled air. COPD has two main forms, chronic bronchitis and emphysema, which often occur together, so the traditional classification has limited clinical significance.

Chronic bronchitis is defined by the presence of a chronic productive cough for 3 months in two consecutive years, excluding other causes of chronic cough. Emphysema results from destruction of the bronchioles and alveoli.

Obstruction of the lower respiratory tract causes expiratory wheezing, especially during maximum forced expiration and prolonged expiration. In the early stages, arterial blood gas analysis reveals mild to moderate hypoxaemia without hypercapnia. As COPD progresses, hypoxaemia becomes more severe and hypercapnia develops. Arterial oxygenation worsens during acute exacerbations, exercise, and sleep. Clinical signs include swelling of facial blood vessels due to secondary polycythaemia, tremor, somnolence and confusion due to hypercarbia, and right heart failure.

### Chronic therapy

1. Pharmacotherapy does not affect disease progression, but decreases symptoms, reduces the frequency of exacerbations, and improves quality of life.
   - Inhaled long-acting beta-2 agonists and inhaled corticosteroids
   - Theophylline as adjunctive therapy; it inhibits phosphodiesterase and has an anti-inflammatory effect, but is reserved for a limited number of cases
   - Mobilisation of secretions by abundant oral fluid intake and room humidification; limit the use of antihistamines, antitussives, mucolytics, and decongestants; expectorants are of no clear benefit
2. Smoking cessation is the only intervention that can reduce loss of lung function and mortality from respiratory causes.
3. Pulmonary rehabilitation can improve exercise capacity and quality of life and is recommended for patients with moderate to severe COPD.
4. Vaccination against pneumococcus and influenza is key to combating acute infections.
5. Long-term home oxygen therapy reduces COPD mortality. The goal is to increase arterial oxygen partial pressure (PaO₂) to ≥ 8 kPa or arterial oxygen saturation (SaO₂) to ≥ 90% at rest.

### Acute exacerbations of COPD

Acute exacerbations of COPD are characterised by worsening respiratory symptoms beyond normal daily variation and are usually caused by infection in more than 75% of cases or by irritation of the tracheobronchial tree from cold weather, hypoxia, narcotics, or sedatives.

Acute exacerbations of COPD are primarily due to mismatches of ventilation and perfusion rather than the expiratory airflow limitation seen in acute asthma attacks. The clinical presentation is dominated by hypoxaemia, including tachypnoea, tachycardia, systemic hypertension, cyanosis, and changes in mental status, together with CO₂ retention and the development of respiratory acidosis. Diseases such as pneumonia, pneumothorax, pulmonary embolism, congestive heart failure, or acute abdomen can precipitate COPD exacerbation.

A patient with acute exacerbation of COPD should have arterial blood gas analysis, venous blood tested for inflammatory markers, blood count with differential, lung ultrasound for pneumothorax, and chest X-ray, with further testing depending on the clinical presentation.

### Therapy for acute exacerbations

The objectives of treatment are relief of reversible bronchospasm, correction of tissue oxygenation, and treatment of the underlying cause of exacerbation.

1. **Oxygen ± mechanical ventilation.** The goal is to correct PaO₂ > 8.0 kPa or SaO₂ 88–92%, with caution and monitoring of PaCO₂ so that hypercapnia does not occur. Oxygen therapy increases ventilation-perfusion mismatch through loss of hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction and reduces haemoglobin affinity for CO₂ through the Haldane effect. Choosing the correct oxygen-delivery aid and avoiding excessive oxygen concentrations are crucial for preventing respiratory acidosis. If adequate oxygenation is not achieved and/or respiratory acidosis develops, assisted ventilation, primarily non-invasive, is required. If response remains inadequate or deterioration continues, invasive mechanical ventilation is required. Non-invasive ventilation provides better outcomes in terms of lower intubation rates, lower mortality, and shorter hospitalisation.
2. **Pharmacotherapy.** Short-acting beta-2 agonists and anticholinergics are first-line treatment for acute severe COPD exacerbation.
3. **Corticosteroids.** Systemic administration for 5–7 days improves lung function and hypoxaemia.
4. **Antibiotics.** These are administered if infection is suspected, for example when there is a change in sputum volume or appearance. Common causes are *S. pneumoniae*, *H. influenzae*, and *M. catarrhalis*. Choices include macrolides, tetracyclines, or amoxicillin with or without clavulanic acid.
