## Introduction

Acute respiratory failure happens as a primary failure in breathing or as a secondary failure following other disorders in the body. It is characterised by the inability of the lungs to meet the body’s requirements for oxygen and/or carbon dioxide removal.

Respiratory control is a complex activity. Automated rhythmic breathing is controlled by the brain stem through multiple neuronal groups. It can be interrupted by coughing and vomiting and is susceptible to voluntary control, for example during speech. Central chemoreceptors in the medulla oblongata are sensitive to hydrogen ion concentration (H+). An increase in the arterial carbon dioxide partial pressure (PaCO2) increases the PCO2 in the cerebrospinal fluid (CSF), which causes a drop in pH and drives breathing. Peripheral chemoreceptors in the carotid bodies are sensitive to hypoxia, but only by sensing a drop in the arterial oxygen partial pressure (PaO2), and much less the total oxygen content. Acidaemia, fever, and hypoperfusion are also important ventilation drives.

## Oxygen transport in the blood

Oxygen is transported via haemoglobin (oxyhaemoglobin), and a smaller fraction is dissolved in the blood.

Under normal circumstances, oxygen delivery is abundant compared to oxygen consumption. When something pathological occurs, there is an imbalance between oxygen delivery and oxygen consumption. This is not exclusive to breathing difficulties. When a patient bleeds, the haemoglobin content decreases, which also alters the oxygen delivery capacity.

## Oxygen-haemoglobin dissociation curve

The oxygen-haemoglobin dissociation curve describes the relationship between oxygen saturation and partial pressure. It is not linear because haemoglobin-oxygen affinity increases in an oxygen-rich environment. Haemoglobin is a tetramer, and each molecule binds four oxygen molecules. Each oxygen molecule binds independently. Binding of the first oxygen molecule increases the affinity for the next one, and so on.

The curve shifts left or right depending on certain conditions. A leftward shift, which means higher affinity and less oxygen release to tissues, occurs with hypothermia, alkalosis, and carbon monoxide poisoning. A rightward shift, which means lower affinity and greater tissue oxygen release, occurs in fever and acidosis. In practical terms, more oxygen is released in the tissues during fever, which is useful when the body is fighting an infection.

> Reduced oxygen delivery and/or increased consumption are the main causes of tissue hypoxia.

## Carbon dioxide transport

Unlike oxygen transport, in which most oxygen is bound to haemoglobin and only a small fraction is dissolved in the blood, carbon dioxide transport is different. About 10% is dissolved in the blood, 10–20% is conjugated as carbamates on deoxyhaemoglobin, and the majority, about 70–90%, is transported as bicarbonate after the following reaction occurs in the erythrocyte:

<FormulaCallout
  expression="CO2 + H2O ⇌ H2CO3 ⇌ HCO3− + H+"
  label="Reaction"
  title="Carbon dioxide transport reaction"
/>

Deoxyhaemoglobin has approximately 3.5 times greater affinity for CO2. This is clinically important in chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), where patients may have difficulty exhaling effectively, which increases total body CO2 content. Usually, this CO2 binds to deoxyhaemoglobin. When such a patient receives oxygen therapy, this can lead to a further increase in PaCO2 levels, which can be fatal.

## Four types of respiratory failure

If we organise these concepts in a clinically useful way, we can define four types of respiratory failure.

### Type 1: classic hypoxaemic respiratory failure

`PaO2 < 60 mmHg (< 8 kPa)`

Causes include:

- alveolar oxygenation failure, such as low FiO2, alveolar collapse, alveoli filled with fluid or blood, or infiltration
- disrupted transition of oxygen from the alveoli to the blood, such as interstitial lung disease or pulmonary vascular disease
- reduced blood oxygen transport, such as obstructed flow, shunting, low haemoglobin concentration, or a defective haemoglobin molecule

### Type 2: hypercapnic respiratory failure

`PaCO2 > 45 mmHg (> 6 kPa)`

This is defined as an inability to adequately remove CO2 because of reduced alveolar minute ventilation. The increase in CO2 also leads to hypoxaemia because oxygen is displaced by carbon dioxide. Hypoxia in this case is usually easily corrected with oxygen therapy.

### Type 3: perioperative respiratory failure

This specific type of failure occurs because of perioperative circumstances. It is caused by abnormal movements of the chest and abdomen due to surgery, anaesthesia with residual drug effects, or injury. Both hypoxia and hypercarbia occur.

Treatment focuses on prevention, including an adequate level of anaesthesia and analgesia, reducing unnecessary sedation, and maintaining head elevation.

### Type 4: high-demand respiratory failure

This is the inability of healthy, or not primarily affected, lungs to maintain increased ventilation during a systemic hypermetabolic state. Sepsis is one such condition. If the primary cause is left untreated, or if there is no therapeutic response because of the nature of the illness, respiratory muscle fatigue may eventually require mechanical ventilation.
