## Introduction

Pulmonary oedema is an emergency condition requiring immediate medical attention. It can be cardiogenic, due to an increase in hydrostatic pressure, or non-cardiogenic, due to an increase in microvascular permeability.

If no lung injury is present, fluid flow in the intercellular space of the lung is primarily defined by hydrostatic pressure.

## Initial patient assessment: ABCDE + SAMPLE

### A (airway)

- Possible auditory phenomena from the upper airway because of fluid accumulation

### B (breathing)

- Impaired gas exchange with subsequent hypoxia increases the work of breathing (WOB)
- This manifests with accessory respiratory muscle activation, high respiratory rate, and diaphoresis
- Hypoxia-caused confusion may occur
- Auscultatory findings include crackles and, in the case of pleural effusion, absent lung sounds

### C (circulation)

- Signs of shock may be present: tachycardia, initial hypertension as a product of sympathetic activation, and hypotension in later stages
- Skin is pale and clammy
- ECG findings may vary and often depend on the underlying pathology causing pulmonary oedema

### D (disability)

- All of the above can lead to impaired cerebral perfusion and affect consciousness
- Confusion, agitation, and coma may occur

### E (exposure and environment)

- Search for signs of heart failure, for example pretibial oedema, which indicates a probable cardiac cause

### SAMPLE

Symptoms may include dyspnoea, orthopnoea, weakness, and similar complaints. Cardiogenic pulmonary oedema is usually preceded by chronic heart failure of various causes. Non-cardiogenic oedema is more probable with a history of traffic accidents, infections, fever, and similar triggers.

## Non-cardiogenic pulmonary oedema

Non-cardiogenic pulmonary oedema encompasses all clinical states that promote increased vascular permeability in the lungs, resulting in inflow of liquid and proteins into the interstitial and alveolar space.

### Causes of increased permeability

- Direct injury, such as gastric fluid aspiration or pneumonia
- States promoting acute capillary endothelial injury, including systemic infections (sepsis), burns, and trauma; these cause acute lung injury (ALI) and acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS)

### Consequences

- Respiratory failure, primarily type 1, with progression to combined type 1 and type 2 in severe forms

### Treatment

- Treatment of the primary cause
- Supportive treatment
- Mechanical ventilation

## Cardiogenic pulmonary oedema

If there is underlying heart disease and it progresses or is left untreated, pulmonary oedema may occur at some stage, for example in valvular defects or cardiomyopathies. More rarely, it may happen due to other causes such as volume overload, metabolic diseases, or uncontrolled arterial hypertension.

Typically, patients present with progressive dyspnoea. They are seated upright, coarse crackles are audible, they are tachypnoeic, and peripheral saturation is low, with evident use of accessory respiratory muscles. Heart rate is usually rapid, the skin is diaphoretic, cold, and pale, and capillary refill time is prolonged. Blood pressure is usually elevated. The 12-lead ECG may show various pathological findings such as ischaemia or rhythm abnormalities. In advanced stages, altered and reduced levels of consciousness can be expected.

The purpose of this text is not to describe all pathological cardiac states that can lead to heart failure, but to present the most typical presentation and its treatment. The primary cause that leads to this presentation is left ventricular systolic failure. Because of reduced ejection fraction, LVEDP (left ventricular end-diastolic pressure) rises and fluid retention occurs. This increases hydrostatic pressure in blood vessels and fills the alveoli with transudate. Large amounts can lead to pleural effusion.

When the alveoli are filled with fluid, it is difficult for oxygen to reach the capillary bed, and because of reduced systolic function, blood delivery to the organs is compromised.

## Basic principles: treatment of cardiogenic oedema

### A (airway) and B (breathing)

- Apply oxygen therapy if the patient is fully conscious and peripheral saturation is lower than 90%.
- If a fully alert patient responds inadequately to oxygen therapy and still has peripheral saturation < 90% or PaO₂ < 8 kPa with respiratory rate ≥ 25/min, non-invasive mechanical ventilation (NIV) should be considered.
- Endotracheal intubation and invasive mechanical ventilation are suitable for patients with life-threatening hypoxia and/or altered level of consciousness.

### C (circulation)

- High-dose loop diuretics with monitoring of urine output; caution is required because of hypokalaemia.
- Intravenous furosemide: the decision about initial dosing depends on the patient’s chronic intake and dosage. If acute heart failure is new in onset, the initial dose is a bolus of 40 mg IV, and subsequent doses are adjusted according to diuretic response.
- In acutely decompensated chronic heart failure, patients often take doses as high as 125–250–500 mg daily. Repeating the chronic dose does not always result in expected or significant urine output, so repeated doses are often necessary.
- Response can be monitored by placing a urinary catheter and measuring urine output. Relief of symptoms is also a sign of adequate dosing. Always treat the patient, not the numbers.
- Nitrates or nitroprusside are contraindicated if systolic blood pressure is below 110 mmHg. Continuous intravenous infusion via perfusion pump is the preferred route.
- Nitroglycerin may be used at 5–40 mcg/min. It has rapid onset and offset, should be titrated carefully to the desired clinical effect, and hypotension should be avoided. It significantly decreases afterload.
- Inotropes in continuous intravenous infusion, such as dobutamine, enoximone, adrenaline, or levosimendan, have numerous adverse effects and are indicated only when systolic blood pressure is below 90 mmHg. Seek expert help.

### D (disability)

- Morphine may be administered intravenously for anxiolysis. It enhances oxygen delivery to the myocardium indirectly by reducing sympathetic activity, tachycardia, and hypertension, and it can improve the patient’s compliance with NIV.
- Initial dosage is 2–4 mg IV, followed by additional 2 mg IV doses until the desired clinical response is achieved, such as a cooperative and calm patient.
- In the case of overdose, administer naloxone.

## Cardiogenic shock

Cardiogenic shock is defined as inadequate tissue perfusion following heart failure, after excluding hypovolaemia as a possible cause. It is the most advanced stage of acutely decompensated heart failure and occurs in less than 2% of all cases.

### Characteristics

1. Systolic blood pressure below 90 mmHg, or a drop in mean arterial pressure greater than 30 mmHg
2. Heart rate over 60/min
3. Oliguria
4. Signs of organ congestion may or may not be present

The most common cause is acute decompensation of previously known heart failure. It occurs only rarely in patients with previously normal heart function or previously unknown heart disease, for example in massive myocardial infarction.
