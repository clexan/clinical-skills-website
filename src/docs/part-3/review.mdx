This review brings the respiratory section back to a single bedside frame: recognise respiratory failure early, use oxygen and ABGs deliberately, and escalate rapidly when the airway, breathing, or circulation is collapsing.

## Recognise respiratory failure

- In every respiratory emergency, start with the **ABCDE approach**.
- **Acute respiratory failure** may occur as a primary failure of breathing or as a secondary consequence of another systemic disorder. It is important to distinguish between these, because respiratory compromise is not always the primary pathology.
- The **oxygen–haemoglobin dissociation curve** is not linear. Because of this, a seemingly small drop in **SpO₂** may correspond to a much more significant fall in **PaO₂**, especially once the patient is on the steeper part of the curve.

## Use oxygen and ABGs carefully

- **Oxygen therapy** should be used only when indicated. The main indications are **low SpO₂/SaO₂**, **impending airway closure** such as in anaphylaxis, and **significant blood loss** such as trauma or hypovolaemic shock. Remember that **FiO₂ is the oxygen dose**.
- **Arterial blood gas (ABG) analysis** usually does not tell you the exact cause of the patient’s condition, but it does help you assess its severity and physiological consequences. ABG results must always be interpreted in the clinical context. Use a **stepwise approach**, and when the primary problem is **metabolic**, remember to consider the **strong ion difference (SID)** or **anion gap**.

## Treat time-critical respiratory emergencies

- In **anaphylaxis**, give **adrenaline 0.5 mg IM immediately**. At the same time, administer **oxygen**, **call for help**, **secure the airway**, and establish **large-bore IV access** so that fluids can be given rapidly.
- In **acute asthma** and **acute COPD exacerbation**, treatment generally includes **oxygen**, **nebulized bronchodilators**, and **corticosteroids**. In **COPD patients**, oxygen must be used with particular caution and titrated appropriately.
- **Cardiogenic pulmonary oedema** requires rapid treatment. Give **oxygen**, and consider **non-invasive ventilation (NIV)** if the patient is fully alert but in severe distress. Use **high-dose loop diuretics**, while monitoring for **hypokalaemia**. **Intravenous nitrates** or **nitroprusside** may be used if blood pressure allows. Seek expert input before giving **inotropes**, because although they may appear beneficial, they carry important risks and adverse effects. **Morphine** may also be used in this setting to reduce **sympathetic drive**, not primarily for analgesia.
- Non-invasive mechanical ventilation is an important skill area in respiratory emergencies and should be understood well, because in the right patient it can be life-saving.
