The first ventilators, the so-called **iron lungs**, were used during the polio pandemic. The patient was placed in a chamber that generated cyclical negative pressure, mimicking the respiratory cycle and enabling mechanical ventilation. This is physiologically similar to natural breathing, in which the respiratory muscles create negative pressure and literally draw air into the lungs.

These ventilators had the advantage of imitating normal respiration, but they also had major limitations. The patient was effectively trapped in the chamber, which made access difficult and worsened quality of life. Advances in modern medicine led to the positive-pressure ventilators used today. Their main principle is the generation of positive pressure that **pushes** air into the lungs, which is the opposite of normal breathing.

The aim of mechanical ventilation is to bridge the period in which various reversible clinical states make spontaneous breathing inadequate, for example traumatic brain injury requiring deep sedation, severe COVID-19 pneumonia, septic shock, or haemorrhagic shock. Such patients would not survive without ventilatory support.

**Invasive mechanical ventilation** is applied when a patient is endotracheally intubated. **Non-invasive mechanical ventilation** is applied in a fully alert patient and is contraindicated in an altered state of consciousness.

In the early stage of treatment, an intubated patient is usually sedated and does not breathe spontaneously. Later, when the clinical effect becomes favourable, the clinician reduces the number of mandatory breaths and increases the proportion of spontaneous, although usually still supported, breaths. There are many ventilation modes that can be used to achieve this.

Non-invasive ventilation is usually delivered via tightly fitting continuous positive airway pressure (**CPAP**) masks, which must provide a good seal to prevent air leakage. The patient must be fully awake and cooperative. It is most commonly used in patients with severe respiratory failure after an unsuccessful attempt at oxygen therapy, although it can also be a first-line therapy, for example in COPD exacerbation with acutely elevated CO2.

Whether a transport ventilator or a stationary ventilator is used, the basic principles and initial setup are the same. This chapter discusses **only the initial setup in emergency settings outside the ICU** and does not go into detailed ventilator management.

## Basic settings of lung-protective mechanical ventilation

The basic settings of lung-protective mechanical ventilation are as follows:

1. **FiO2** — desired oxygen percentage in the inspired gas mixture (**21% to 100%**). It may initially be set as high as 100%, but only for a short period. It should then be titrated to target SpO2 and to avoid hyperoxia; **40% to 60%** is often sufficient.
2. **Tidal volume (Vt)** — **6 to 8 mL/kg of ideal body weight (IBW)**.
3. **Respiratory frequency (f)** — set to ensure adequate CO2 removal.
4. **Positive end-expiratory pressure (PEEP)** — depends on the disease, usually **4 to 15 cmH2O**.
5. **Plateau pressure (Pplat)** — avoid values **above 30 cmH2O**; set the **Ppeak limit at 35 cmH2O**.

These recommendations arise from studies designed to identify settings that reduce complications of mechanical ventilation. Since mechanical ventilation is not a physiological way of breathing, inappropriate settings can cause **ventilator-induced lung injury**.

## Ventilator-induced lung injury

1. **Volutrauma** — lung injury caused by inappropriately high ventilation volumes.
2. **Atelectrauma** — lung injury caused by insufficient PEEP. Alveoli collapse at the end of expiration and reopen on inspiration, and repeated closing and opening damages them.
3. **Barotrauma** — lung injury caused by excessive airway pressures.
4. **Biotrauma** — lung injury caused by release of pro-inflammatory mediators, oxygen radicals, and related mechanisms.

## Main ventilation modes

Ventilators are equipped with many ventilation modes, some of which are brand-specific and proprietary. The two principal concepts are:

- **Volume-controlled mode** — a target volume is guaranteed with each breath, regardless of the pressure required to deliver it. This ensures tidal volume delivery but may expose the lungs to high pressures and barotrauma.
- **Pressure-controlled mode** — a breath is delivered at a fixed time and constant pressure. This is often gentler on the lungs, but tidal volume may become inadequate and therefore requires close monitoring.

Modern ventilators often combine these concepts by providing volume-guaranteed, pressure-regulated breaths. In emergency settings with transport ventilators, mode choice is usually limited, and pressure modes are used less often because close monitoring is more difficult.

## Non-invasive ventilation

Non-invasive ventilation is usually performed in **CPAP** mode. By setting the **PEEP**, this mode delivers continuous positive pressure in the airways and alveoli, helping to prevent alveolar collapse.

If a patient has an inadequately low tidal volume, **pressure support (PS)** may be added so that each spontaneous breath receives additional pressure and achieves a more appropriate tidal volume. Non-invasive ventilation can be used in COPD exacerbation, pulmonary oedema, pneumonia, and similar conditions.

## Invasive ventilation in emergency transport settings

**Synchronised intermittent mandatory ventilation (SIMV)** is the most important invasive ventilation mode on a transport ventilator. It is appropriate for sedated or comatose patients without spontaneous breathing effort.

Compared with fully mandatory modes such as CMV, SIMV still allows occasional spontaneous breaths, reducing patient–ventilator asynchrony. Asynchrony can lead to desaturation, elevated CO2, lung injury, and increased intracranial pressure, all of which should be avoided. In emergency settings, asynchrony usually results from inadequate dosing of sedatives and/or muscle relaxants.

## Basic settings of mechanical ventilation in medical emergencies

| Setting | Non-invasive mechanical ventilation | Invasive mechanical ventilation |
|---|---|---|
| **Mode** | CPAP | SIMV/MMV |
| **Tidal volume (Vt)** | Patient-dependent. If spontaneous tidal volume is lower than **6–8 mL/kg IBW**, set **PS** to achieve about **6 mL/kg IBW**. Typical **PS: 2–8 cmH2O**. | **Vt 6–8 mL/kg IBW**. Pressure support can also be adjusted to support spontaneous breaths when present. Typical **PS: 2–8 cmH2O**. |
| **Respiratory rate (RR)** | Patient-dependent | Depends on the CO2 value; initially **12–20/min** |
| **Positive end-expiratory pressure (PEEP)** | **4–8 cmH2O**. If the setting does not suit the patient, they usually complain. | **4–15 cmH2O**. Start at least at **4 cmH2O** and increase as needed, up to **15 cmH2O**. If intracranial bleeding is suspected, **never exceed 4 cmH2O**. |
| **Inspiration/expiration ratio (I:E)** | Patient-dependent | Usually **1:2**; in COPD or asthma, **1:2.5** or **1:3** |
| **Pressure limit (Ppeak limit)** | Set **35 cmH2O** | Set **35 cmH2O** |

## When to use mechanical ventilation in emergency settings

Mechanical ventilation is used in the following situations:

- when patients require analgesia and sedation and their breathing becomes inadequate because of the drugs
- in respiratory arrest
- in severe respiratory failure with increased work of breathing (**WOB**)

If a patient has low saturation and low PaO2, when available, oxygenation is most effectively improved by optimizing **PEEP**. PEEP prevents alveolar collapse and increases the gas-exchange surface area.

If CO2 elimination is impaired, or if small tidal volumes are accompanied by tachypnoea and accessory respiratory muscle use, adding **pressure support** in non-invasive ventilation or adjusting **tidal volume** and the **I:E ratio** in invasive ventilation may further help.
