Sudden neurological deterioration is a common reason for patient admission to emergency medical services. It can be caused by various critical illnesses or injuries. Prompt treatment is essential to limit secondary brain injury and improve patient outcome.

Primary injury is the initial brain damage caused by a critical illness or injury. Regardless of the cause, primary injury triggers secondary injury, which can lead to further brain damage. Our management can affect secondary brain damage through interventions that address hypoxia, hypotension, and increased intracranial pressure.

## Causes of sudden neurological deterioration and potential differential diagnosis

### Neurological causes

- Ischaemic or haemorrhagic stroke
- Traumatic brain injury (TBI), with morphological substrates ranging from epidural haematoma, subdural haematoma, and brain contusions to diffuse axonal injury and brain oedema
- Brain tumours and other intracranial lesions

### Systemic causes

- Infection, including septic encephalopathy and central nervous system infection in the form of meningitis and/or encephalitis
- Organ system failure, such as shock, renal failure, hepatic failure, hypoxaemia, and/or hypercarbia due to respiratory failure
- Endocrine disorders, including hypoglycaemia, hyperglycaemia, thyroid dysfunction, and Addison crisis
- Electrolyte and acid-base disorders, including hypo- or hypernatraemia, hypercalcaemia, and hypophosphataemia
- Drugs and poisoning, including narcotics, benzodiazepines, and carbon monoxide poisoning
- Hypothermia and hyperthermia

## Initial patient assessment and management: ABCDE + SAMPLE

### A (airway)

- Check airway patency, as CNS depression may cause partial or complete loss of airway patency.
- Noisy breathing or snoring, together with asynchronous movement of the chest and abdomen, suggests partial obstruction.
- Complete obstruction is silent, with visible accessory muscle use and a see-saw phenomenon for a brief period before cardiac arrest.
- In suspected trauma, apply cervical spine stabilisation.
- Aspirate any blood or gastric content from the airway, if present.
- Use simple airway-opening manoeuvres, such as head-tilt chin-lift or jaw-thrust, or place an oro-/nasopharyngeal airway if the airway is not patent.
- Tracheal intubation and mechanical ventilation are indicated in patients with **GCS ≤ 8**, as they may lose protective airway reflexes and develop hypoventilation with resultant hypercapnia, which worsens intracranial hypertension.
- Advanced airway techniques, including the use of anaesthetic and neuromuscular blocking drugs, should be performed only by experienced personnel, because failed tracheal intubation attempts can worsen secondary brain injury.

### B (breathing)

- Assess breathing using the “look, listen, and feel” approach to evaluate respiratory rate and effort, breath and added sounds, chest movement symmetry, and breathing patterns associated with decreased consciousness, such as Cheyne–Stokes and Kussmaul breathing.
- Look for possible causes of hypoxia, such as subcutaneous emphysema, tracheal deviation, and jugular venous distention.
- Monitor oxygen saturation (**SpO2**) continuously using pulse oximetry and end-tidal carbon dioxide (**EtCO2**), if available.
- Administer oxygen if **SpO2 is below 90%**.
- Ensure adequate oxygenation at all times to prevent secondary brain injury and maintain oxygen saturation above 90%.
- If the patient has insufficient minute ventilation and oxygenation even after airway opening and oxygen therapy, start manual ventilation with a tight-fitting face mask and self-inflating bag, and consider anaesthesia and tracheal intubation if appropriately experienced.
- Insufficient minute ventilation can be expected in bradypnoeic patients, for example after opioid overdose or with imminent brainstem herniation, and in patients with respiratory muscle fatigue due to acute respiratory insufficiency.

### C (circulation)

- Assess heart rate, blood pressure, capillary refill time, skin colour, and hourly urine output, and monitor these parameters every 3–5 minutes.
- Look for patterns that may indicate the cause of sudden neurological deterioration, such as Cushing’s triad.
- Apply continuous ECG monitoring.
- Hypotension, together with hypoxia, is a major contributor to secondary brain injury.

- In the initial management of a patient with neurological deterioration, aim for **systolic blood pressure above 110 mmHg** or **MAP above 70 mmHg** to achieve adequate brain perfusion.
- If blood pressure is below the target values, consider:
  - volume resuscitation with **0.9% sodium chloride** or **hypertonic sodium chloride**, as these crystalloids have osmolarity similar to or higher than plasma and will not worsen brain oedema
  - vasoconstrictors such as **noradrenaline 0.01–0.5 μg/kg/min IV**, titrated to target blood pressure with regular reassessment
- Once IV access is established, obtain blood samples for testing, including complete blood count, glucose, sodium, potassium, and coagulation tests. Add CRP, procalcitonin, urea, creatinine, ammonium, liver enzymes, arterial blood gases, and other tests according to the differential diagnosis.

### D (disability / other acute considerations)

- Before performing the neurological assessment, correct hypoxia and hypotension, as both can cause or deepen decreased consciousness.
- Begin by assessing blood glucose, Glasgow Coma Scale (**GCS**), pupillary size, symmetry, and light response.
- Check for differences in motor response between the left and right side.
- Perform a targeted neurological examination for signs of increased intracranial pressure, meningeal irritation, subtle myoclonus such as eyelid fasciculation, and evidence of previous seizure, such as lateral tongue biting or uncontrolled urination.

Normal pupils are equal in size, reactive to light, and usually range from 2 to 8 mm in diameter. Any asymmetry or decreased responsiveness to light in a patient with sudden neurological deterioration is pathological and can help in the differential diagnosis.

### Targeted neurological examination

Targeted neurological examination includes the following:

- **GCS**, which assesses the patient’s ability to open their eyes, vocalise, and move their limbs. If the patient is awake, assess orientation. If the patient does not respond to verbal command, apply a painful stimulus, for example by pinching the trapezius muscle, supraorbital ridge, or temporomandibular joint, and assess the best motor response together with any left–right asymmetry.
- Breathing pattern, which may help identify both the depth and the cause of the comatose state, for example Cheyne–Stokes breathing, bradypnoea, irregular breathing, or Kussmaul breathing.
- Plantar and deep tendon reflexes, which are useful when there is asymmetry in motor response.
- Brainstem reflexes such as pupillary, corneal, and oculovestibular reflexes, as well as the response to tracheal suction if brain death is suspected.

### Reversible and urgent causes

#### Hypoglycaemia

Hypoglycaemia is a common and easily treatable cause of a comatose state. If present, administering glucose is an absolute priority, ideally in high concentration such as **40% glucose**, titrated until recovery of consciousness and adequate blood glucose levels. If rapid glucose testing is not available in a patient with sudden neurological deterioration, **50 mL of 40% glucose** should be administered, as the potential benefit of treating hypoglycaemia outweighs the potential risk associated with hyperglycaemia.

#### Seizures

Repeated or prolonged seizures add substantially to secondary brain injury and should be stopped immediately. Administer **IV benzodiazepines**.

#### Signs of elevated intracranial pressure

Examples include decreased consciousness with anisocoria or dilated unresponsive pupils, Cushing’s triad, and similar findings. Consider the following measures:

- **mannitol 0.5–1 g/kg IV** with or without **furosemide 20 mg IV**; place a urinary catheter before applying this therapy
- brief hyperventilation
- elevation of the head of the bed to **15–30°** to improve venous drainage and decrease ICP
- if adequately trained in the use of anaesthetic drugs, give analgesia and sedation and proceed with tracheal intubation and mechanical ventilation

#### Drug overdose

If available, administer a specific antidote, for example **naloxone** in opioid overdose or **flumazenil** in benzodiazepine overdose.

## SAMPLE

- **S (Signs/symptoms):** Obtain information from any witness present. Clarify how the deterioration developed, over minutes or hours, and whether the patient complained of headache, vomiting, fever, or other symptoms beforehand.
- **A (Allergies):** Ask about known allergies or hypersensitivity to medications or other substances.
- **M (Medications):** Ask about current medications, with special emphasis on antiplatelet and anticoagulation therapy, psychiatric therapy, insulin, oral antidiabetic drugs, and antiepileptic therapy.
- **P (Past illnesses):** Ask about recent surgery, infections, and other comorbidities. Clarify any relevant family history, including psychiatric disorders if overdose or self-harm is suspected.
- **L (Last oral intake):** If less than six hours have passed since the last meal, the patient is at higher risk of regurgitation and aspiration of gastric contents during analgosedation and tracheal intubation. Follow rapid sequence intubation principles if these procedures are planned.
- **E (Events leading up to present illness/injury):** Look for empty medication containers or bottles near the patient. Consider whether the presentation may represent suicide attempt or intentional overdose.
