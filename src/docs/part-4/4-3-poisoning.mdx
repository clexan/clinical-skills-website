## Introduction

Poisoning is a clinical condition characterised by a sudden or gradual onset of illness due to contact with a poison.

The symptoms that develop are closely related to the type of poison. For systemic toxicity to occur, absorption into the bloodstream is essential. Acute poisoning requires prompt diagnosis and urgent treatment. Because some toxic substances are uncommon in routine practice, clinicians should always have access to reliable toxicology information and therapeutic guidance, typically through a poison control centre. Any substance taken in the wrong dose can become toxic.

Acute poisoning may be accidental, suicidal, criminal, environmental, or industrial. Most cases are accidental. Suicidal poisoning is caused by deliberate intake of a toxic substance with the intention of causing death. Environmental poisonings are often food-related, but may also result from plants or animals such as venomous snakes or spiders.

## Initial patient assessment: ABCDE + SAMPLE

### A (airway)

- Airway patency primarily depends on the effect of the poison on the patient's level of consciousness.
- In cases of poison ingestion, airway injury is possible, for example after exposure to strong acids.
- Gastric contents may be present in the mouth if vomiting has occurred.
- Some poisons can cause seizures, and bite marks on the tongue may be visible.
- Airway manoeuvres depend on the patient's clinical state.

### B (breathing)

- Respiratory rate may be affected, for example a low respiratory rate in opioid poisoning.
- Abnormal auscultatory findings may suggest aspiration of gastric contents in a patient with altered consciousness.
- If the airway is not patent and breathing is inadequate, oxygen saturation will fall. In that situation, start oxygen therapy and secure the airway appropriately.

### C (circulation)

- Cardiovascular abnormalities vary depending on the poison and may include tachycardia, bradycardia, or arrhythmias.
- Blood pressure may be altered depending on the substance and dose.
- Skin appearance and capillary refill time may suggest shock.
- Bleeding may occur in some poisonings, for example unintentional anticoagulant poisoning.
- Intravenous access and standard monitoring are mandatory.
- Some poisons have specific antidotes that should be administered in life-threatening situations.

### D (disability)

- Level of consciousness may be impaired either by the direct central nervous system effect of the poison or indirectly through disruption of other vital functions.
- In coma, advanced airway management and mechanical ventilation are indicated.
- Pupillary findings may help differentiate toxic syndromes, for example miosis in opioid poisoning and mydriasis in sympathomimetic poisoning. Pupils may also indicate severe CNS injury, such as diffuse cerebral oedema with fixed dilated pupils.
- Blood glucose may be high, normal, or low. For example, a person with type 1 diabetes may attempt suicide with insulin.

### E (exposure)

- Examine the patient and the surroundings carefully. They may provide critical clues about the type and amount of poison, as well as any associated injuries.
- In a suicide attempt, the means of poisoning are often found nearby, such as empty pill blisters.
- Take a thorough **SAMPLE** history. Its content will vary depending on the type of poisoning.

Specific measures include decontamination, stimulation of excretion, and administration of specific antidotes. These should be considered as soon as the poison is identified and the patient has been assessed. Because the number of potential poisons is enormous, it is impossible to memorise specific treatment for all of them. This is why poison control centres are essential and should be accessible 24/7.

## Decontamination, excretion, and antidotes

Decontamination depends directly on the route of exposure. This may include gastrointestinal decontamination such as gastric lavage, or irrigation of the eyes after chemical exposure. Current evidence does not support routine usefulness of gastric lavage and/or activated charcoal in all cases.

Stimulation of excretion may be attempted through the gastrointestinal or urinary tract. Activated charcoal is one method used to enhance elimination in selected cases. If a poison is dialysable or can be effectively excreted in urine, renal replacement therapy may be used in critically ill patients. Examples include barbiturates, lithium, metformin, salicylates, and methanol.

Antidotes are drugs that counteract the effect of the poison. Airway management must be secured before antidote administration when indicated. It is also important to remember that some antidotes have a shorter duration of action than the poison itself. Continuous monitoring and repeat dosing may therefore be necessary. Naloxone is a classic example.
