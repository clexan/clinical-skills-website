## Introduction

Seizures are caused by abnormal electrical impulses in the brain that disrupt normal brain function, resulting in acute neurological deterioration. This can manifest as decreased consciousness and focal or generalised involuntary movements. Diagnosis is usually based on clinical presentation, laboratory findings, electroencephalography (EEG), and imaging studies such as CT or MRI of the brain. Treatment involves identifying and addressing the underlying cause, administering anticonvulsant drugs, and in some cases surgical intervention.

Overall, understanding the causes and types of seizures is crucial for the effective diagnosis and management of patients with seizure disorders. As medical professionals, it is important to have a thorough understanding of these conditions in order to provide optimal patient care.

Seizures can be classified into different types, including:

- **Epilepsy (epileptic convulsive disorder)**, a chronic brain disorder characterised by recurrent seizures that are not caused by a reversible factor
- **Symptomatic epilepsy**, in which seizures occur due to a known cause, such as brain tumour, TBI, or stroke
- **Non-epileptic seizures**, caused by a temporary systemic disorder that interferes with normal brain function, for example electrolyte disorders or hyperthermia

### Clinical classification and age-related clues

The patient’s age at the time of the first seizure can provide clues to the underlying cause. Febrile states and hereditary neurological or metabolic disorders are common causes of seizures in patients younger than 2 years. Idiopathic convulsive disorders are the most common cause of seizures in patients between 2 and 14 years of age. In adulthood, seizures most often occur due to brain tumours, TBI, or stroke.

To classify seizure disorders properly, it is important to recognise whether the seizure is focal or generalised, and whether a motor component is present.

Generalised convulsive disorders involve electrical impulses that affect both hemispheres of the brain and often lead to impaired consciousness. Within this category, seizures may present with tonic-clonic, exclusively tonic, or exclusively clonic motor activity.

In contrast, focal seizures are characterised by impulses originating from one hemisphere, usually from subcortical structures. These seizures may present with automatisms, muscle atony, or clonic muscle spasms, with or without loss of consciousness. Focal seizures may also occur without a motor component and may instead involve autonomic dysfunction, such as gastrointestinal sensations or feelings of heat or cold, or cognitive and emotional impairment. A focal seizure can progress to a generalised seizure at any moment.

### Status epilepticus

Status epilepticus is a life-threatening condition caused by a continuous burst of impulses in the central nervous system. It can occur in two forms:

- **convulsive status epilepticus**, characterised by tonic-clonic activity lasting longer than **5 minutes** or by **two consecutive seizures without regaining consciousness**
- **non-convulsive status epilepticus**, typically characterised by changes in mental status and often more difficult to diagnose

## Treatment

Effective management of seizures requires attention to both general measures and drug therapy. One of the most critical aspects of management is securing the airway to ensure adequate airflow.

In patients with generalised tonic-clonic seizures, clear the area around the neck to prevent compression from clothing. Do **not** attempt to remove the patient’s tongue, as this can injure both the rescuer and the patient. Turn the patient onto the **left side** to reduce aspiration risk if vomiting occurs. If a generalised motor seizure occurs, medication should be administered immediately.

There are three lines of drug treatment for seizures.

### First-line treatment

First-line drugs are benzodiazepines such as midazolam, lorazepam, and diazepam.

In the prehospital setting, the following options can be used:

- **midazolam IM**: **0.2 mg/kg**, maximum **10 mg**
- **midazolam intranasal**: **0.2 mg/kg**, maximum **5 mg**
- **midazolam buccal**: **0.5 mg/kg**, maximum **10 mg**
- **lorazepam buccal**: **0.1 mg/kg**, maximum **4 mg**
- **diazepam rectal**: **0.5 mg/kg**, maximum **20 mg**

In the hospital setting, drugs of choice for convulsive status epilepticus include:

- **midazolam IV**: **0.1 mg/kg**, maximum **5 mg**
- **lorazepam IV**: **0.1 mg/kg**, maximum **4 mg**
- **diazepam IV**: **0.3 mg/kg**, maximum **10 mg**

Prehospital medication administration should be taken into account when choosing the next treatment line and dose.

### Second-line treatment

If the patient does not respond to first-line treatment, or if first-line medication has already been administered twice, second-line drugs may be used:

- **phenytoin**: **20 mg/kg**, maximum **1000 mg**
- **phenobarbital**: **20 mg/kg**, maximum **1000 mg**
- **levetiracetam**: **60 mg/kg**, maximum **3000 mg**

If the patient remains in a convulsive state after the first administration of a second-line drug, another second-line drug can be given.

### Third-line treatment

If symptoms persist, third-line drugs may be used in the intensive care setting:

- **propofol**: **2.5 mg/kg** initially, then continuous infusion **3 mg/kg/h**
- **thiopental**: **2–5 mg/kg** initially, then continuous infusion **1–4 mg/kg/h**
- **midazolam**: continuous infusion **1 μg/kg/h**

In such cases, airway protection with endotracheal intubation and mechanical ventilation is important, together with other intensive care measures.

## Initial patient care: ABCDE + SAMPLE

Proper initial care is essential when managing a patient with seizures. The following steps help ensure patient safety.

### A (airway)

- Maintain an open airway by head tilt and the use of supraglottic aids such as the oropharyngeal or nasopharyngeal airway.
- Endotracheal intubation may be necessary if hypnotics are used.

### B (breathing)

- Administer **100% oxygen** via a face mask with reservoir.
- Continuously monitor **SpO2**.
- Regularly assess respiratory rate and the need for mechanical ventilation.

### C (circulation)

- Continuously monitor vital signs.
- Check pulse and blood pressure, particularly for hypotension when hypnotics are used.
- Place **two wide-bore venous lines** as soon as possible.
- Perform standard laboratory tests to assess for electrolyte disorders.

### D (disability)

- Check glucose levels, as hypoglycaemia is a common cause of seizures.
- Administer **40% glucose** in the case of hypoglycaemia.
- Assess the patient’s level of consciousness using the **AVPU** or **GCS** scale.

### E (exposure)

- Remove the patient’s clothing, but do not tie or restrain the patient in a way that may cause injury during a seizure.
- Remove clothing or jewellery from around the neck to prevent compression or suffocation.
- Check for injuries such as tongue biting and sphincter relaxation.

## SAMPLE / AMPLE history

In addition to the ABCDE approach, obtaining a SAMPLE or AMPLE history is important:

- **S (Signs/symptoms):** Examine the symptoms, including any aura before the seizure, such as headache, dizziness, or sensory phenomena.
- **A (Allergies):** Check for drug and food allergies, especially those relevant to antiepileptic drug use.
- **M (Medications):** Determine whether the patient is already taking antiepileptic drugs or other medication.
- **P (Past illnesses):** Clarify whether this is the first seizure manifestation or whether the patient already has a diagnosed seizure disorder.
- **L (Last oral intake):** Determine when the patient last ate and whether there is a risk of gastric content aspiration.
- **E (Events leading up to present illness/injury):** Identify possible precipitating factors such as loud sounds, bright lights, insomnia, or similar triggers.
